Agent of the Messageries Maritimes S.S. Company guilty of discrimination in refusing passage to a U.S. Minister. No. 73. Consulate of the United States, Goree-Dakar, November 11, 1887. Honorable James D. Porter, Assistant Secretary of State, Washington, D.C. Sir: I have the honor to enclose the testimony which I have this day given to the examining magistrate in the inquest which is being held following orders from Paris on the now somewhat famous "Fez affair". With reference to the last paragraph of my deposition I would remark that Mr. Cleret, the Delegate of the Interior Department of the Colonial Government to the second arrondissement, has but recently arrived, which perhaps explains his confounding in his letter "The United States Consul on board the 'Fez' off Dakar" as he wrote on the envelope, the English Vice-Consul who was actually concerned in Captain Kent's letter, and myself who had simply handed it to Mr. Restoul. I bet to add that it seems to me that the refusal to receive Mr Taylor on board board the mail-steamer is a clear case of discrimination on the part of Mr. Restoul, the agent of the Messageries Maritimes line of steamships, (also a new-comer) because those steamers come from Brazil where yellow fever and cholera are endemic, whereas the "Fez" was put in quarantine simply because it is customary here at this season to inflict five days on all arrivals from the south. It follows that the passengers already on board the mail were more likely to be contaminated than the gentleman who was refused. Two days had already elapsed, and supposing Mr. Taylor to have finished the five days on shore he could not have been refused, which is absurd when it is remembered that the voyage to Europe is considerably longer. The fact is that it is a procedure quite regular to thus transfer passengers from the south, and one often effected in the case of those arriving by the "British & African" boats. Whether Mr. Restoul enjoys such limitless discretionary powers, I do not know. I am, Sir, your obedient servant, George Strickland, Vice-Consul 1 Enclosure. He desired that she might be allowed to proceed immediately or that he might land in order to wait the first northern bound mail, and engaged me to procure for him such authorization. I therefore sought an interview with the principal medical authority, Doctor Geoffrey, who said that if Mr. Taylor was in quarantine at Dakar, he could not land without going to the lazaret, but that he could be transferred directly to another ship with the consent of the captain. I thereupon embarked to go to the steamer but had hardly left the wharf when I met a boat from the "Fez". The officer in her gave me three letters, one for me from Mr. Taylor, and the other two for the "Governor of Dakar" and the British Vice-Consul. He asked me to find out if the papers of the "Fez" were in Goree saying that such information had been given them by the Port at Dakar where they had asked for them in vain; their boat having been ordered away each time that it went to the mole. Mr. Ramade, the consignee of the of the steamer at Goree who was on the wharf, manifested surprise and made the remark that it was curious for the "Fez" to be quarantined at Dakar when she had had free pratique at Goree. I then went alongside the English Steamer and there I saw Mr. Taylor, Captain Kent, his officers, and several passengers, who told me that they had arrived at Goree the 7 at 8 AM where they were admitted without quarantine, and after having conferred with their consignee, they had steamed over to the coal-hulk to take twenty tons. While they were thus occupied some negroes arrived alongside and asked for the bill of health which was given up to them, and they disappeared with it on shore. No notification of quarantine was given them, and they thought that there could not be a distinct regulation for Goree and for Dakar. After having finished coaling, they went to the landing to get their papers and to find some washing which they had left on a previous voyage, but they were received like criminals and were threatened with imprisonment if they should land, notwithstanding that it was the first time that an authority had mentioned quarantine. Although they asked for their bill of health, it was it was withheld, and for that reason the steamer could not go to sea. I thought that it was probably a misunderstanding and promised them that I would see the English Vice-Consul and arrange the matter When I reached Dakar, I inquired at the "Direction of the Port" in passing where the papers of the "Fez" were to be found and was answered that they were still with the visiting doctor. I then directed my steps towards the house where lives Mr. Restoul, General-Agent of the Messageries Maritimes line of Mail Steamers at Dakar, and also British Vice-Consul, but before I had reached there, I met a former servant to whom I gave the letter entrusted to me for the English consul and charged him to deliver it and at the same time to announce my visit. I had gone but a little way when my man came running after me, panting and excited, and said that Mr. Restoul had seized him by the collar, handled him roughly and had threatened to have him arrested. I went immediately to see what was the matter, and the gentleman explained that he had taken my servant for some one from the "Fez" who was violating the quarantine, as some of them had already done. He further said that the that the captain and others from the steamer had been outrageously disrespectful to French authorities and that they owed money in Dakar. He intended to complain to the Foreign Office and said that the case would be submitted to the State Prosecutor here. I replied that I came from the steamer and the captain and gentleman on board complained on the contrary of the difficulties which the authorities themselves had raised and in order to have an equitable hearing they wished to confer with him. After he had promised to go off in the afternoon, I left him but in talking with other persons of note, I observed that the people of Dakar were generally against the "Fez". At about half past two, I went again alongside and told them that the trouble was more serious than I had supposed, that I was told that some of them had been on shore the previous night and that people were generally stirred up. I added that I could do no more because their own consul was mal-disposed towards them. They acknowledged that two passengers had been on shore but they were themselves Frenchmen and they declared that they had not been formally advised of quarantine and the threat threat of imprisonment before spoken of was the only intimation. Here Mr. Restoul arrived, and a lively discussion took place between him and the captain in which the latter asked the former if he meant to tell him to his face that he would not protect him. During the colloquy their Vice-Consul persisted in demanding the sum of 600 francs for damage done to a coal-lighter of his company by the "Fez" on her former voyage. The captain replied that he would never pay it because the fault was not his and he was not responsible for the accident; that on the contrary, had his steamer suffered injuries, he should have claimed damages from the company which has no right to obstruct the harbor with the hidden chains of its lighters; and that in any case, he could not be stopped on his voyage for such a reason, and as he carried the Belgian mail, he should make it his business to push the matter the fartherest possible and make somebody pay every hour of his delay. For my part, thinking that from the look of things the "Fez" would be still in the harbor when the regular mail of the Messageries Maritimes which was expected should (and did) (and did) arrive, I asked Mr. Restoul if the United States Minister could take passage on board, and he refused absolutely saying that he came from an infected country and he could not allow him on a ship of his company. In the end Mr. Restoul demanded that the captain write his refusal to pay and promised him his papers on that condition. Captain Kent withdrew in the cabin and in a moment reappeared with the paper which after being read by Mr. Restoul was returned to be signed. Upon leaving the latter required that the captain send in his boat to bring back the papers and the latter remarked that that would make the fourth time that he had already sent it for the same purpose. I then returned to Dakar on personal business, and when I came down to the landing to reëmbark I saw the boat of the "Fez" and those in her told me that they had received their papers. They desired me to ask at the Port office if there would be any difficulty about passing the money on Shore to the washerwoman for whom they were waiting, and I therefore saw the Harbor-master's son who said that there would be none in the least. He told me at the same time time, that his father had been insulted the day before by the people from on board the "Fez". I then started for Goree and in passing the steamer I told Mr. Taylor and Captain Kent that their papers were in their boat and that they might begin to get their anchor. Arrived at Goree, I was asked if I had been on board the "Fez" but on my affirmation was allowed to land. On the 10th, I wrote to Mr. Restoul asking him his reasons for having toul refused the United States Minister to Liberia passage on his mail-steamer, in order that I might give explanations to my government. I was aware that there were precedents and that Mr. Taylor would bring up the subject upon his arrival. I have never received a reply of any kind. I finish my testimony by respectfully protesting against the lesson which the Hon. Delegate of the Interior has seen fit to give me. He reproaches me for not having covered with an envelope a letter by Captain Kent of which from complaisance I served as simple carrier to the British Vice-Consul.
