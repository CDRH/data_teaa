Sir: Since my despatch No. 20, dated February 13, 1892, no material changes have taken place in the political and commercial situation of this Province. Still some events may be of interest to the Department. Evasion of the Oports Rebels: Most of the common soldiers who took part in the Oporto Rebellion of January 31, 1891, and who had been exiled to this Province and that of S. Thom√© have been pardoned in the early part of this year and have returned to Portugal. Of the chiefs of the rebellion only one is still here, and for the simple reason that he has no desire at all to leave Loanda. All the other leaders of the Rebellion have contrived to escape to France. One embarked at Mossamedes on a sailing vessel bound for Gaboon and reached his destination without difficulty. The Director of the Custom-House at Mossamedes was censured and the "Official Bulletin" for conniving with the evasion, but was not otherwise punished. Two other leading rebels were at Loanda, put in boxes and embarked on French steamer bound for the North. At Ambriz, however, sea-sickness caused by the usual heavy rollers of that coast compelled one of the secret passengers to open the wicket of his box, and led to the discovery, of the two fugitives. The captain of the steamer had them bound with chains and delivered them to the Portuguese authorities of Ambriz. Here they were accompanied to the fort by a detachment of soldiers and by a chasing crowd who hailed in them the martyrs of the Republic. After a few days of banqueting with their Republican friends of Ambriz and receiving 300 dollars subscribed in a few hours by all the leading traders, the rebels again escaped on a sailing-vessel bound for Gaboon, when they met their comrade from Mossamedes and whence all embarked for France. After leading men of Ambriz were arrested and brought to Loanda in a man of war; but as the whole population of Ambriz had openly taken part in the envasion, and the population of Loanda too was favoring it, the men were again sent home unmolested. A fourth leader of the Rebellion escaped in the same manner - with the secret assistance of Republican Government officials from Principe (Prince's Island) and the accomplices were not more seriously prosecuted. Public feeling. The public opinion of Angola which for a time leaned strongly towards separation from the metropolis, ceased agitating this question, when the Dias Ferreira ministry took the helm of government, because many hoped it would by a coup d'etat establish the Republic. Then the tariff question called public attention to another side, and now the presiding feeling is one of discouragement. "Everything is lost; Portugal is dead" is now a frequent expression. Commercial situation and the new tariff. The main provisions of the new tariff are: (1) Most duties are charged according to the weight of the merchandise (2) Portuguese goods are free or well-nigh so (3) All foreign imports pay much higher duties, (4) The duty in foreign alcohol is prohibition, (5) The Governor has no longer the power to exempt any goods from paying duties, (6) The Kongo District, which enjoyed free-trade is now, by an agreement between Portugal and the Kongo State, subjected to a tariff more moderate than that of the Kongo State. This new tariff does not interfere much with American imports: machines continue to be free; kerosene and flour are necessities and Portugal can not compete in them, and timber pays little more than previously as to the general effect of the new tariff, everybody is now agreed that it was a mistake, that its only result will be a rise in the prices of all necessities and hence of labor, and the final desertion of these markets by the native trade of this far interior (rubber-trade) which will prefer the Kongo-route. Of all events, Angola which had enjoyed unusual prosperity during the terrible crisis of Portugal is beginning to lose confidence since the new tariff has come; credit is being withheld and the air is full of fears for the future Railroad extension. On the 17th of May the fifth section of the Loanda railroad was opened to the public. The daily up and down trains run now over 220 kilometres. The territories, Cassoalala, is on the Lucalla river, near Dondo. The coffee district of Cazengo, which is the main object of the railroad, is not yet reached, but every new section of 20 kilometres will already help the planters to bring their coffee to the terminus of river navigation. The rates are still too high to compete with the river boats or to induce the native trader of the interior to make use of the railroad. Most of the Angolan natives, too, prefer to walk over the track rather than pay the fare. The railroad as far as opened, is still far from paying; but the Government guarantees the interest on all the capital actually spent by the company on the road. This is the reason why the company does not care to increase either the passengers or the freight traffic; which could easily be done by a general reduction. Poorly built and managed as this railroad is, it is a decided blessing to the country and is sure to pay a good dividend when the coffee region is fully tapped, that is in three to six years The new trek of Boers. When, last year, the Boers of Humpeta, in the district of Mossamedes, helped the Portuguese government to subdue Bihe, they were so pleased with that cool and salubrious high plateau that they wrote to the Transvaal inviting their countrymen, to come and join them. Now this new trek of Boers, estimated at about 200 waggons is daily expected in the south. The Portuguese government has sent there the officer who knows best how to deal with the Boers, having married one of their daughters, and the people and government hope he will be able to settle the new corners without conflict. Hitherto the colony of Boers has been a State within the State, jealously maintaining their own religion, customs, administration, language and race. They elect their own commandants, carefully and intermarriage with the Portuguese, and only go to war for the Portuguese government when the latter agrees to pay them what they ask. Nor, have they ever paid taxes. If, on the arrival of the new trek, the Boers should decide to found a new Republic, there is no military power in Angola that could prevent it; and if the Portuguese government should try to subject the Boers to the common administration, there is no doubt but that it would hasten the formation of a Boer State. It does not take a prophet to foresee the loss to Portugal of the highlands of Bihe, Gangda and Ambuela, and the formation in the near future of a State which European statesmen never dreamt of, when they partitioned Africa. King Mushidi of Katanga shot. According to a letter from Captain Stairs to a missionary in Bihe, King Mushidi of Katanga (Garenganze) was shot by an officer of Stairs, as he was ordering his soldiers to make fire on the approaching column of Stairs' expedition I already informed the Department of how Mushidi has coolly rejected the British Protectorate offered him by Mr. Sharpe of the South Africa Company. It appears he was going to do the same with the expedition of the Kongo State; but the latter felt strong enough to fight, and so made short work with the King's resistance. Previous to Stairs' expedition, the King had kindly "received the Kongo-State expedition under Lieut. Le Marinal, whom I had the pleasure of meeting here in Loanda on his way to Europe. Though Le Marinal, missionaries on the spot, and nation just come from there, I learn that the country between Kasangi (Cassange) and at the confluence of the Lulua and Kassai, is in a state of anarchy.
