Sir: I have the honor to enclose herewith copies and translations of letters received at this Consulate from the undermentioned commercial houses of this city, Viz: Messrs Newton, Carnegie & Co, Messrs Baines Larsen & Co., Messrs Palhares and Irmãos, Messrs Souza Lara & Co., and Messrs Sevart & Co. Referring to inclosure No 1, "Messrs Newton, Carnegie & Co." it seems to me as being worthy of study, in so far that, although it bears the signature of the firm above mentioned, it is an emanation of the individual. Mr Newton, who is a Vice Consul of the United States, But but I fancy to do it justice, it should be read as coming from a Consular Officer of the British Government, and an English merchant jealous of opposition. It appears singular that after the many years Mr Newton has acted as U.S. Consul here, that the first merchants in the place can say "that American goods are not used because they are not known," considering this, the fact that he can see "no inducements for American merchants &c," may find explanation in his method of performing the duties of U.S. Consul as far as they relate to the extention of our foreign trade. It is apparant that the inclosure above referred to, in its treatment of the subject under consideration either does not grasp its importance or is influenced by a desire to discourage new departures. It seems strange tho', that a successful merchant with large experience, in referring to causes affecting the commercial growth of a nation, should allow his thoughts to be confined to a question of three or four years, or that he should limit himself to the consideration of one product when commenting upon the resources of a country so rich in natural agricultural and manufactured products as ours. Enclosure No 2: Messrs Baines Lauer & Co. interested in the growth of commercial relation between this province and the U.S. adopt a tome that carries with it the impression of honest expression and considerable study. I am unfortunately, because of limited experience, unable to judge correctly as to the value of their suggestions relative to the method to be adopted to enable American Capital to get a firm foothold in the part of Africa, but, as far as a knowledge of the West Coast gives me a right to judge of here. I think they are in some respects, right. It is worthy of attention that this firm in referring to cottons would strengthen their statements by forwarding samples showing that if the U.S. desires to place cotton on this market, it will be necessary for her manufacturers to enter into a competition of inferiority, but an examination of those used in the Northern districts shows an improvement in quality such as "our honesty," allows us to manufacture. These samples with samples of corals mentioned by this firm, I have forwarded to the Department. Inclosure No. 3. Messrs Palhares & Irmãos, in contradiction to Messrs Newton, Carnegie & Co. though acquainted with only one of the products of our country, believe that the introduction of American commodities is to be desired. Inclosure No. 4. Messrs Sousa Lara and Co., have an experience limited to yellow & pitch pine. Inclosure No. 5. Messrs Sevart & Co, refer to England and Germany as the countries of supply for this trade, ignorant of our capacity, they seem to think that our products are only such as would would appeal to the requirements of the European population, yet they believe, "American goods, may be introduced with advantage on this market." These letters are from of the first commercial people of Loanda the longest shippers and importers. It will be recognized that, with one exception they are remarkably unanimous in looking at things in a narrow way, a comprehensive view of the possibilities of the country seems to beyond their ability or desire and to any influence beyond those which affect small and immediate pecuniary returns, these are dead. The great nations of Europe find here, and in adjoining districts valuable outlets for their industries. Taught by experience the value of representation, there is not a spot of commercial value on the coast where English or German houses have not planted their need. The factors with which these countries have excused these gratifying results, should not be be particular to them, but ought to be common to all alike. Commercial enterprise environed by good judgmt, is their secret. American industries can find their way into and secure a safe place in these centres, if Americans will adopt the methods as practiced by the English and Germans. Resident representative, coming for contact with the native, learning his needs and securing an intimate knowledge of the trade, a supply of merchandise, steady in its flow and independent of good and bad seasons alike, direct communication, cheap and rapid. Given these qualities, it would be seen that in a few years our increasing industries I would find outlets in many parts of the world where now the reports of our country's industries and greatness are looked upon as almost a romance. To open the eyes of these people to the value of our goods it is necessary that some method be adopted to bring them under their notice. This might be done by the establishment here, under the supervision of this Consulate, of a Sample Room, where could be displayed, at a nominal cost, full lines of our commodities. This Enterprise, preparing a way for future and increasing movements, would receive valuable assistance from Messrs Baines, Lauer & Co. I have the honor to be, Sir: Your obedient servant, Henry F. United States Consulate. Lisbon Office. 90, Rus dos Capellistas No. 1. Copy! St Paul de Loanda 10th, Nov. 1887 Henry F. Downing, Esq United States Consul, Loanda. Sir: In answer to your letter of the 1st inst., we beg to reply as follows. We see no inducement for American merchants to invest in the Angola trade as there are too many houses already for the trade and they would have every thing against them. They would have no direct communication and unless their good were shipped through Lisbon they would have to pay the import duty which is a difference of 30%. We do nothing with American goods, although a few years ago we used to have two cargoes a year. There is however a considerable quantity of pitch pine timber imported and this year three cargoes have come from Pensacola, but now the port is supplied for some time to come. We have given up the trade ourselves as we found the profit to be too small to be worth the time and trouble. When the Railway is finished we have not the slightest doubt but that trade will greatly increase but that will take three or four years at least. The American import which is timber, will, we think instead of increasing be reduced as good wood is to be had in the interior. We have really no statistics before us to offer you but we shall always be happy at any time, in assisting you to get any information you may wish for. We are sir, Your most obedient servant (S'gnd) Newton, Carnegie & Co. Enclosure #1. New York Loanda December 28th 1887 Henry F. Downing Esq. U.S. Consul, Loanda. Sir: In reply to your official letter asking us for information relative to local outlets for American products we have the honor to inform you that we have made a special study of this question, and have for the last year, exerted ourselves to open up correspondence on the subject. Doubtless you have consulted other firms in our city and have become familiar with the local idea that American products are limited to Lumber and Petroleum. We take it that you wish to have a plain statement on our side of what can be done and how to do it. The matter is however, a very complex one and must be treated at length, for our ground extends not only to this neighborhood but to the North Coast and the Congo River. Angola. Loanda. Here very little has come in beyond Petroleum, lumber and occasional lots of flour and soap. Petroleum. The consumption in Loanda = about 30 cases of 10 gal. each, per diem = 900 cases per month. Novo Redondo, Benguela, Mossamedes, S. Thomé and Banana = probably 2500 cases per month. Dondo and the other River and interior towns another 1600 = 5000 cases of 10 gals. ea. per month for this province. To secure a monopoly of this trade it would be necessary to crush competition, and to do this the stuff would have to be put cif in most of the ports mentioned @ Reis fortes 1$250. In Banana and Cabinda better prices rule. In Cabinda one contrast with the Government is for Reis 1$960 per case. Custom duties in all these ports except Banana and Cabinda, which are free = 20 Reis per kilo. Lumber. There are now five Loanda forms selling lumber and we are awaiting a consignment from Norway. Occasional lots, say 400 tons, would sell in about 3 months @ an average of Reis 25$000 per 1000 ft. run. If you wish we will give you Pro forma a/c sales. The duty is 15% a.d. kind of lumber 5" sq yellow pine logs, match boarding 1" & 1/2" and . Shingles might do well north but have no sale in Loanda. The one thing necessary for Angola trade is to put "something called Lop" - hard, and dark in color - on this market at a maximum of Reis 3$000 per 50 kilos. cif Loanda. Consumption about 200 cases per month. Flour. Fair quality such as that offered @ $3.50 to $4.00 in America should be put here cif at $6.50 to $7.00. It sells well at about $8.00 in the Custom house. The consumption in Loanda and interior towns average 500 barrels monthly. By taking in the other Angola, and the Congo ports this quantity might be trebled. Butter. Not good business in Loanda where Normandy butter is preferred. It however sells well North, selling price 450 reis per lb: duty 20%. Hams. In small lots have regular sales through out. same prices and duties as butter. Paints & Oils. Are of importance in view of the various Government contracts. Red lead (powder), in kegs should arrive cif @ 161 to 171- per 50 kilos as a maximum, others in proportion Terpentine and Raw Linseed oil at 5d and 6d kilo. Furniture. In carefully assorted lots, cheap and showy stuff (best taken to pieces and put up here), such as carpet seated and fancy chairs, wardrobes and chiffoniers, writing tables (plain with two to four drawers), stools, folding hatracks, commode, &c. &c. &c. sell well if cheap. Take for instance as a basis a "showy" 5 ft. wardrobe @ from $35.00 to $40.00, (without mirror). Soft Goods. Table cloths and napkins; handkerchiefs; &c, have regular sales at low prices. Duties linen 780 reis per kilo, cotton 500 reis. Tea. Of fair quality (black and green) sells @ 800 to 900 reis per lb. Duty 240 reis per $. Cheese. Regular sales in small lots, selling @ 400 to 450 Reis lb. duty 25%. Canned Goods. American canned goods are not known. Canned Beef put up by the "Armour Co" is however much favored @ 500 reis per 2 lb tin. Lard. Good business in regular lots sells @ 220 reis per lb. Duty 20%. Matches. Cannot compete with the Germans. Cod fish If very dry has large sale. It would be advisable to experiment with Newfoundland. Cotton Goods. So far as this province (apart from the District of the Congo) is connected there is one great obstacle in the way of American products, and that is American honesty. Our cottons (equalling in value 50 Rs 500:00 monthly), are made up with starch and clay and generally falsified, a fair price for a fair article cannot be obtained. We well give you samples. Guns. Flintlock guns (called Lazzarino) 1 ball 3/4 ball & 1/2 ball), are supplied by England and Germany at 5/- each. These sell here in the Custom house @ about 5/9. Demijohns. Of 5 gals. wicker covered sell @ 600 to 700 reis. Duty 25%. Sales from 500 to 1000 per month. They are usually sent filled with rice. Rice. Rangoon sells @ 1000 reis per 15 kilos. Duty 15 reis per kilo. Large consumption. Sugar. Sells well in regular lots @ reis 2,200 per 15 kilos for lump chystallized and reis 1,900 per 15 kilos for Brown Sugar. Duties refined 40 reis kilo. unrefined 20 reis kilo. Tobacco. Prohibition duties = 3$200 reis per kilo. Clocks. American alarm clocks and the very cheapest watches. good sale for small lots. Candles Are invoiced to us at 35 francs per 100 kilos Cast off Army Uniforms. sell largely in the interior but should not be invoiced at more than 400 to 500 reis per garment. Barrel & Pipe Staves. Sell well Small Articles. We are now about to experiment with these in great variety, and it will be better to advise you later on. Rum. 40° clear. Should be put here cif at Rs 28,000 per 130 gals. safe shipments including northern ports about 50 pipes. The foregoing remarks will serve to give you some idea of the prospects offered in this province proper. In view of the future, however, we have to consider the probable commercial changes resultant upon the completion of the Loanda and Ambaca Railway. The Railway Service, was originally formulated with the idea of opening up the gold fields to the North of Golungo Alto. Its value, however does not depend upon any speculative enterprise but upon the natural resources of the province. The fertile districts of Cazengo, Golungo Alto. Enconge &c., producing coffee, rubber, palm oil, kernals &c. in great abundance are practically robbed of their value by the difficulties of transport. At times carriers are not to be had to the river ports, but were there no difficulty in this respect the excessive rates of the Companhia da Vapor de Quanza would be sufficient to heavily handicap any enterprise. We will give you the figures, premising that the produce is practically unlimited; given proper merchandise and rapid transport the trade of Angola would assume an importance it has never yet possessed. From Enconge to Donda, 5 days journey Each carrier will take kilos of coffee, price per carrier per trip 4$000 ries. Therefore each ton of coffee requires 14 1/2 carriers and costs from Enconge to Donda 58$000 Reis From Donda to Loanda by the Quanza River Steamers the freight is 40 reis per ton per kilametre. Donda is 200 kilametres from Loanda, Ex. 200 x 40 = 8000 Reis x 58 Rs 000 = 66$ about $70.00 per ton over 124 miles. The natural consequence is that the interior plantations are continually losing money and are all to the Bank. Here again we have the secret of the falsified goods. Now all this the Railway will remedy by tapping the Cazengo, Galungo and Enconge districts, enabling us to vend our goods up cheaply and rapidly and get our produce down equally advantageous circumstances. The Railway will be built and will pay well. It is however, at present in dubious hands. If things go wrong it may be five years before the lines : if not three years will enable it to at least mitigate the we have mentioned. the line will be built because if the company fails in its obligation a Syndicate of African merchants is prepared to come forward and push the thing through. District of the Congo We would rather not go into detail as to the River Congo itself as we are of opinion that any statement would fall short of the reality. We have, however, powerful friends there, and if we could go on a comprehensive visit together much might be arranged. Outside of the Congo itself but within its district we have outlets at Landana, Cabinda and Ambrizette, not to mention S. Antonio on the Congo. These places are supply under Government Contracts with all kinds of provisions &c. &c. In this district trade is essentially different to that of Angola proper, goods are only partially adulterated and falsified and there is yet a good field for American Cottons of the lowest quality. In Cabinda alone we can guarantee 150 tons of produce per month and with a few more houses in the Chiloango river we have no doubt that it could be quadrupled. As merchants we could sell you about 100 tons per month. Kernals @ 400 reis per 32$, not to mention splendid hard woods in any quantity. Such produce is all bought in barter for: 1° Cotton as per samples, pieces 40" wide x 24 yds & 24 folds Handkerchiefs in piece of one dozen. Best sales, blue boxes, checks, Hdkfs and red calico. 2° White and yellow metal anklets weighing 1 to 2 $ 1/2" - plain 3° Cheap Bells of different sizes up to 6" 4° Rum 40°. 5° Red Corals as per samples. To resist heat and hot water. 6° Keg powder. 2x4 Here also can be sold regular lots of flour, soap wine, shot, and sundry other article which we will advise later. Products are kernals, Palm oil, Rubber and Ground nuts. We think also that a little pushing might arrange for a fair quantity of ivory. Estimated capital for this enterprise as far as concerns establishing and stocking ten (10) factories = $60,000.00. No American labor required nor timber construction. In conclusion we are of opinion that for America to be advantageously represented in Africa it is necessary that: 1° A company be formed in America with at least $200,000.00 Capital. 2° That an energetic and pushing firm be appointed as African Manager with a "good interest" acting under supervision. 3° That the company own at least 4 sailing vessels of from 350 to 400 tons burden and that these run regularly between the two countries: paving the way for steam communication. Given the conditions it is easy for American products to obtain a form footing in Africa, and for America to assume that position as regards Africa which as one of the most important nations of the world she has only exert himself to command. We would also call your attention to the fact that there are some hundreds of registered in the Secretarial of this town: including gold, coal, petroleum, copper, and other valuable minerals. In Cabinda we have copper, lead and iron in great profusion, and were informed by the Government sometime since that there exists every indication of coal. We do not ask you to accept our views without question, but are quite ready to put you in possession of the means of informing yourself as to the value of our remarks. U.S. Consul, Loanda. Sir: We beg to acknowledge your favor of the 4th inst., and have much pleasure in replying to your various question. The commerce of the United States, altho' of great importance in itself, has only manifested itself on a very small scale in our possession of Angola, owing principally to the limited market we have for any article not essentially proper for the native trade. This branch of manufacture has been held by England from the outset and include such articles as raw and striped cotton fabrics, crocking, guns (flintlock), and powder. We are not aware as to whether America can compete in this line: the one known fact being that the English and German market are drawn upon exclusively. As to other goods for the consumption of the European population their importance is relatively insignificant. We may however remark: 1° We believe that American goods may be introduced with advantage on our market. 2° The principal articles which have been handled by us in dealing with America, have been pitch pine, petroleum, soap soap and furniture Of these we have received consignments from Wilmington. It is however necessary to note that petroleum and soap shipped direct cannot compete in price with the same articles via Europe. Petroleum in direct lots costs about double the price of European shipments. 3° We have every hope that the commerce of Angola will sooner or later improve in tone owing principally to the Loanda & Ambaca R'y Scheme. 4° As we have already observed our relations with the United States have been limited and it has only been in timber that any real business has been done. We remain Sir, Yours respectfully, (S'gd), Sevart & Co.
