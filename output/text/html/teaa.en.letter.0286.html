No 1. Comcl Agency of U.S.A. at Gaboon Augt 12th 1865. To the Hon. Wm H. Seward, Secretary of State, Washington D.C. Sir: There is, and long has been, an active trade in Slaves, amounting to several hundreds a year. (I can not give exact figures) Carried on between Princes and St. Thomé, (Portuguese islands 150 miles west of Gaboon) and Gaboon & Coast South. Last year I coasted as far as 2°40' South. On the way I saw a schooner of about 100 tons burden, which had been boarded by the agent of an English factory the day before, and found to be "full of slaves" I also saw a boat, capable of carrying 30 or 40 persons, but darkness prevented our distinguishing what it contained. On a previous voyage our vessel saw three such slave-boats. We have children in our school taken from such boats, and given to our Mission to be free and educated. At Cape Lopez I learned there were two slave factories (one of which I saw) whence Slaves were carried to the islands in boats and larger Canoes. During the present voyage I heard of a slave factory in Fernan Vas river, of another large one at Cape Lopez, & I saw at the latter place a schooner at anchor in the . I had with me a son of the late King of Cape Lopez, who was very communicative on the subject of the slave trade. He knew all about the movements of the schooner which I saw at anchor. He said she seldom carried slaves; is engaged in taking cargoes of Fariña &c.; it has a bundle of certificates of good characters received from Eng. Cruisers; is owned at Princes; is recognized at Gaboon as a lawful trade &c. This is no doubt the same schooner I saw last year a little further south. A few months ago a Portuguese schooner from St. Paul de Loanda, bound for Princes or St. Thomé put into Gaboon leaking very badly. It had a great many blacks aboard, servants to the officers & crew of the schooner, for whom regular passports had been made out at St. P. de L. I learned that each citizen may taken 10 such servants. according to Portuguese law. The question arises, What becomes of all these slaves? Whence the Cash and Merchandise that pay for them? Agriculture on these islands is at a very low ebb. Only small quantities of Coffee & Cacao are raised for exportation and nothing else. No kind of manufactures is carried on. Whence then the demand for so many slaves? Are they reshipped to another market? This would seem probable. An American supercargo, an acquaintance of mine, was told, when there a few years ago that they could furnish him with 200 slaves in ten days time. The schooner in which I coasted south last year, was at the islands a short time before, when the Capt. saw there a brig, with hatches down and no appearance of trade day after day. When her Capt. was questioned about his trade his answers were very evasive. At the same time the Portuguese schooner above mentioned "was absent at Cape Lopez, for a cargo of Pea-nuts" Our captain had no doubt the brig was there for a cargo of Slaves. I have heard of there being no slaves on the island to gather the little Coffee that grows there, but heard nothing of any epidemic that swept them off. The trade is contrary to the laws of Portugal, and I have heard of persons imprisoned at these stands for engaging in it, or was it that they had not paid the governor a bonus on the importation? The French claim Cape Lopez as well as Gaboon, yet slave trading is carried on at both places almost continually. There is now a trader in Gaboon, but probably not known to the Authorities. Occasionally the Authorities here make some effort to stop it, and I have, once, in the last 5 years, taken a canoe and slaves. They freed the slaves & released the traders. Such is about the sum of the information I have. I have communicated it, unofficially of course, to the British Consul of Fernando Po. I am sir your obedient servant J. M. Preston Enclosure.
