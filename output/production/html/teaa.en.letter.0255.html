Rec 4 Mar

Gaboon W. Africa Dec. 20 1861

Hon. W. H. Seward
Washington.

Sir

Your communication of July 3rd, and Circular of August 28th with the form of the Oath of Allegiance have been received. The duties of the Agency in Gaboon have not been onerous. In consequence of the Presidents Proclamation of July, I think, relating to Citizens of the United States returning to the States, I gave a certificate of citizenship to Miss Jane A. Van Allen, who sailed from this port Nov 1st in the Brig 'Ocean Eagle', Phillip D. Yates Master, bound to New York. As this is a free port, & neither my duties as a Missionary permitting, or the interests of humanity requiring, I did not perform any official duties toward the vessel. I also am in doubt whether your letter of July 3d authorizing any such official acts.

 

But it is now my painful duty to inform you of the death of Capt. R. E. Lawlin of New York, Agent for James Bishop & Co. of No. 3 Beaver Street New York. Captain Lawlin was a trader of thirty years standing on this Coast. He died at Camma, about 150 miles South of the Gaboon. His principal business was at that place. He had no responsible person about him. I have sent the best man I could find, to go & take charge of the property until I can find the means of bringing it to this place. I have written to the U.S. Sloop Saratoga, & the French Commodore, Baron Didelot has offered any assistance in his power. But I shall not be able to give you particular information before the departure of this month's mail. I have no means of estimating the amount of property left in Camma. It is in the midst of a barbarous people.

 

21st. I have just returned from paying a visit to the Commodore. He told me at once that he had read a communication (the enclosed) which I had the honor to address to him on the 18th Inst., at his request for a written statement of the Case of Capt Lawlin. The proposed at once to send a steamer to protect the property, in case the natives made any resistance to its removal.

In regard to the Oath of Allegiance he said there was no one here authorized to administer the oath, but he would direct the Commandant on shore to witness my signature to it. This I will attend to at my earliest convenience. The Commodore assured me that the simple request as an American citizen, for assistance to secure the property of an American citizen was all he required. I have engaged an English Barque to go & bring the goods to this place. I have written very fully to Messrs James Bishop & Co, & have requested   them to despatch an Agent immediately to take charge of this business. I shall try & keep within the law in my business transactions, & must beg your indulgence for any informalities in my communications.

I would simply add that, after considerable inquiry, I suppose the amount of African produce & goods in the factory at Camma must be between $10,000 & $15,000. Ten thousand, & fifteen thousand dollars. My associate in Missionary labor, Rev. J. M. Preston, will go to Camma in the vessel to attend to the shipping of the goods & settling up Capt. Lawlins business there. He is a perfectly reliable man. It seems to be impossible for me to go.

I am, Sir,
Your obedient Servant
Wm Walker

 

Memorial
To Baron Didelot
Commandant Superior in Gaboon.

Sir

Capt. R. E. Lawlin, a citizen of the United States of America and trading at Camma as the Agent of James Bishop & Co. of New York, died the 6th Inst. at Camma. The enclosed will give you all the information we have received as to his last sickness and death. We feel much concern for the property left by Captain Lawlin, in the care of only the Spaniard who wrote a letter, or which the enclosed is a copy (translation). We have despatched a colored man to Camma to look after the goods until a vessel can be procured to bring them away. But we fear trouble from the natives when we attempt to remove the property. Our wish is to bring all to Gaboon for safe keeping until   advices can be received from New York. We are deeply sensible of your kindness in offering to order a vessel to call at Camma, and render aid in settling this affair. But we have not at hand any sufficient means of bringing the factory here, though a vessel is daily expected, which we hope to engage to go on this business. The factory is 18 miles from the mouth of the Ferrian Vaz River, but it is only 3 miles from the sea-shore. But the intervening space between the factory and sea-shore is, at this season, very wet. A letter from yourself when [?] King Rotimbo, when we have the means of bringing the goods to Gaboon, may perhaps answer our purpose. When a vessel is found I will inform you immediately.

The U.S. Sloop Saratoga is probably South, in the vicinity of the Congo. Should your vessel fall in with her, will you be   so obliging as to ask the Commander to deliver the enclosed to the Commander of the Saratoga?

I remain Sir
Your very obedient servant
Wm Walker.

Gaboon Dec. 18. 1861.

To the Commander of the 'Saratoga'
Gaboon Dec. 18. 1861.

Sir,

I have but a moment to write by a French man-of-war, 'Decheiche[?]', going South, to inform you of the death of Capt. R. E. Lawlin of Camma. The French Commodore, Baron Didelot, has kindly offered to order the vessel which carries this letter, to stop and communicate with the factory and people. But it would detain her one or two days, and we have no vessel to go and get the cargo. So I have not thought it worth while to trouble him. I presume though, when we get ready to settle the business, he will be willing to render any needed assistance.

 

It is a sad case that Capt. Lawlin should die there alone. Can you not make it in your way to go there immediately and protect the property? I received a letter from his merchants by last mail, & they spoke of the possibility of this same event. I shall engage at the earliest moment a vessel to go and take off the goods, and bring them to Gaboon. But we fear difficulty with the natives. I shall write you via Fernando Po.

In the mean time,
I remain
Very Truly Yours
Wm. Walker
Acting U.S. Vice Commercial Agent.
