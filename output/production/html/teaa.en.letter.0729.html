Department of State Received Nov 18 1884
No 16

US Consulate, Goree-Dakar
October 22ond, 1884.

Mr Peter Strickland
To the Department of State

Subject

Response to Circular dated August 21st 1884, asking for information with a view to the reorganisation of the Consular Service

 
No. 16

US Consulate, Goree-Dakar.
October 22ond 1884.

To the Honorable John Davis
Assistant Secretary of State
Washington

Sir:

By last mail I received a Circular dated August 21st, 1884, requesting information with a view to the reorganisation of the Consular service. I will then as briefly as may be, and of necessity in round roughly estimated numbers, give you as graphic an idea as I can of French Senegal, its commercial facilities and its possibilities.

Geographically, French Senegal extends from the "Great Desert" to near Sierra Leone, embracing a section of Coast about six hundred miles in length, for the most part extremely fertile, and abounding with navigable Rivers which render access to the interior comparatively cheap and easy. Of these the Senegal River is navigable for 600 miles during half of the year, and is being connected with the Niger by a Railroad from Bafoulabe to Bammakou which will give it the trade of the whole upper Niger valley. The other Rivers of importance are the Salvum[?], the Cosamanza[?] the Cassini, the Nunez, the Ponga, the Debreeka, and the Mellacoree; all navigable for distances ranging from 10 to 150 miles. Sandwiched between the French sections of Coast are some English Settlements confined closely to the banks of the Gambia, which is a magnificent River navigable for 400 miles, and some Portuguese settlements about   Bulama, where there are also three fine Rivers each navigable far more than a hundred miles. The Commerce of both the English and Portuguese possessions is essentially under French control, and Goree-Dakar from its central position and accessibility to mail steamers is the Commercial emporium of the whole coast.

Of Cities & towns.

St. Louis, built in handsome European style on an island in the Senegal River, is the capitol, with a large commerce, and contains with its environs[?] about 30,000 inhabitants. Goree-Dakar with their environs[?], including Rufisk in sight on the Goree Bay, contain fully 25000 inhabitants, and there are numerous towns further South in French Senegal which contain from one to five thousand inhabitants, all doing a thriving trade with Goree, (which is a free port) by means of Coasters ranging from 10 to 150 tons, many of them timbered with mahogany and rosewood which abounds at different places on the Coast.

The magnificent Bay of Goree is 20 miles across from East to West and from 3 to five miles deep, forming one of the safest and most capacious harbors in the world. It contains no detached dangers of consequence, the navigation being clean in all parts at the distance of a mile from the shore; and in it no lasting gale even blows. Of its present commerce & have no statistics at hand from which to infer the amount, but have seen, exclusive of war vessels of which there are almost always a number in port, more than ten thousands tons of shipping in the Bay at one time.

A Railroad

was begun from Dakar last year, and trains are now running over it into the interior upwards of 30 miles, and it is designed next year to have it extended as far as St. Louis, more than a hundred miles. A telegraph already connects the two places, which is to connect by Cable next year to the Canaries, and from thence to Europe.

The Mails

to Europe are carried by the Steamers of the Messageries & Maritime Company, which are from three to four thousands tons each, and in going and coming call at Goree four times a month. In addition to these we have a boat from the English line of steamers once a month, and one from a Hamburgh line of steamers once a month, besides several belonging to merchants which sometimes carry the mails but do not make their trips regularly.

Amount of American Trade

The Amount of American Goods at present consumed yearly in French Senegal cannot amount to less than one Million dollars in value, but of this only about one fifth part is brought here by Americans, who have been steadily driven off by competition because the conditions of the trade are against them. French agents in Boston and New York buy such Goods only as can be obtained in the most reasonable terms in America, and either ship them direct mostly in foreign vessels which afterwards load Groundnuts for Europe or else they ship them to Europe by steam from whence they are brought down here in vessels which carry on the Trade regularly. Owing to this state of affairs, which in my opinion might easily be prevented, our direct commerce is not holding its own in this one of the fairest prospective markets in the world; there being not less than 3,000,000 of natives here now fairly under French jurisdiction, with[?] wants which Americans can supply better than others increasing in almost a Geometrical ratio, and good produce to exchange for goods.   But I hope to explain fully about this in the near future, and will now end by stating that with the present depressing influences there will not probably be exported directly to America from Goree-Dakar more than $60,000 in Produce. It will consist of Hides, Gum Copal and India Rubber, and much of the same kinds of produce which go from here to Europe will finally reach America to pay for goods which French agents buy and ship from there.

The foregoing remarks I hope will serve to give the Government a sufficient idea of the importance of this place in reorganising the Counsular service, but there is much that would perhaps be more interesting to be added as soon as some statistics will be available at the conclusion of the current quarter. In brief it seems to me that Goree-Dakar is one of the most important places between Cape Spartel and that of Good Hope. Only that it has a somewhat unsavory reputation on account of epidemics Dakar would soon rival St. Vicente as a coaling station for steamers, and as things are it does a good deal of that business. There is unfortunately no question but what the Coast of Africa is unhealthy for Europeans, but Goree-Dakar is one of the best places an it in that respect, probably the very best place.

Of Governors, Senegal has for a long time had Faidherbe one of the most distinguished of her Generals and more recently Briere d' l'Isle, at present high in command in Tonquin. How the Colony would relish the American representation here changed back to a Vice Consulate as seems to be proposed I can only guess from a long knowledge of the French Character, but I presume I shall not do wrong in suggesting that I think such a measure would be hardly favorable to American interests here, and perhaps cause whoever held the position to be treated with a frigid politician[?] by the Officials which might be far from agreeable.

I am Sir with respect
Very truly Yours
Peter Strickland
US Consul
