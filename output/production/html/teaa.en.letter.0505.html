U.S. Consulate
St. Paul de Loanda
Washington, June 28, 1888

Hon. G. L. Rives
Assistant Secretary of State
Washington, D.C.

Sir:

Referring to certain changes contained in despatch of Acting Vice Consul Julius L. Judson of St. Paul de Loanda dated May 16, 1888, I have the honor to inform the Department, that leaving Loanda on the 17th of March I arrived at Cabinda on the 19th of the same month where I found Mr. Judson and delivered to him the Consular Seal and Stamp with instructions to proceed immediately to Loanda, but not to assume the duties of his office until the 31st, when I would be leaving St. Thomé, the Northern limit of the Consular district which place I desired to visit for the   the purpose of gathering information which might be of commercial value.

It is true that I returned to Mr Judson his bond. My motive for doing this was not so much that it was not properly made out, but that, having no knowledge of the sureties named in the instrument, I was not satisfied as to their responsibility, and I judged that by making it necessary for him to provide another, he would be forced to have it certified to by some of the Portuguese authorities and thereby give reason to believe in its value.

Against the charges as to my general behavior while in Loanda, I do not know how to defend myself, unless they are specified, but will say, that wherein any action of more as U.S. Consul at Loanda, has been subject matter for criticism, i have to learn.

1st So far from being able to take advantage of the "confidence not   not to say ignorance of the Portuguese" for instance, by "paying my bills with bogus drafts." I found it impossible to get them to accept good, honest government drafts, for, with one exception, viz. Messrs Newton Carnegie & Co. There was no house doing business in Loanda that would handle an U.S. Consular draft of any kind, and furthermore, If Mr Judsons statements are to be considered, value should be given to that part of his despatch which I here quote, "It is thought here that the smallness of the salary, is the reason that U.S. Consuls sent here generally resort to financial treachery and intrigue and almost invariably leave in disgrace." If this has been the experience of the Portuguese in Loanda with U.S. Consuls it is would seem that they must be fond of having their confidence and ignorance again and again taken advantage of   of.

To my knowledge there has been no business done by me with paper, except with the well informed houses of Newton, Carnegie & Co. and Baines, Lauer & Co. and neither of these houses are Portugese.

Referring to the statement that on his arrival at Loanda, he found that my creditors had seized every thing belonging to the Consulate I would respectfully inform the Department that in accordance with arrangement entered into between me, Baines Lauer & Co. and my creditors, Messrs Baines, Lauer & Co. relieved me from all other creditors but themselves, by taking upon their house my debts and the seizure, I think, will resolve itself in a matter of an owner taking possession of what he was authorized by me to take. This is explained as follows: I had certain furniture from a dealer, for household use, for which   which I was to pay 40 mil reis per month. To protect himself, Mr Quadra, the owner just before my departure furnished me with an inreceipted[?] bill of sale and requested me to attach a note to same stating in set terms that the property was his, and that he was authorized to remove some at such time as best suited his convenience. This I did, and I suppose that Mr Quadra acted upon his rights. Mr Judson was cognizant of this arrangement for he, with me, called upon Mr Quadra and arranged with this dealer to carry the contract over, on the same terms. Mr Judson at the same time called with me on the owner of the house where was located the Consular office, and contracted to continue my lease. Therefore, that this property was taken away may be true, but, that the property of   of the United States was seized I fancy a statement that should be viewed with suspicion, particularly that part which refers to the action of his Excellency the Governor General, i.e., in refusing to take cognizance of the matter. From my knowledge of this Portuguese gentleman, I think that he is too careful and well informed an official to subject himself to reprimand or recall, by encouraging an infraction of the Treaty between the U.S. and Portugal. Furthermore, how could my "victims" be led to believe that the U.S. Government would be any more responsible for my debts of Consuls who preceeded me. If this view of the case is accepted, it will seem that the payment of my debts, means the purchase of household furniture by Mr. Judson, for his personal use.

I would respectfully inform   inform the Department that one copy of Consular Instructions of 1881 as well as all correspondence properly docketed and filed away, as well as furniture somewhat in excess of what is allowed is or should be at the office of the Consulate.

Referring again to Mr Judsons statement, that he has no Consular Instruction no forms, no products or anything I would say that Mr. Judson was a guest at my home there fore quite two weeks when I tried to educate him into the duties of the office, and it seems quite a coincidence, that he could so happily name the right acct, "miscellaneous expenses." for his draft in favor of Mr Hass of Lisbon.

Mr Edmond A. Baines may not be in Loanda, but he is then in Cabinda where Mr Judson resided, but, Mr Baines should   should be in Loanda no later than August.

In connection with the above, I would respectfully submit the enclosed letter from Mr Judson to me which I find, bears the same date as his despatch to the Department.

Knowing nothing of the Rev. Mr. Judson, from others, that can reflect unfavorably, upon his character, I have confined myself to facts.

I have the honor to be,
Sir,
Your obedient servant,
Henry F. Downing
U.S. Consul
