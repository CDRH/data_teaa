Sir: The Dispatch from the Department No 68 in reference to a special duty of 7% which has been levied on merchandise not imported into the Colony from France is received, and as requested. I shall be able to reply by return of mail.

The Subject in question has already been mentioned in two of my Dispatches, Nos 128 & 129 to which I would again respectfully call the attention of the Department. Since they were written an opportunity has been afforded to test the working of the new system, which has without doubt proved prejudicial to foreigners and correspondingly advantageous to French Commercial interests. As an index to the check which American Commercial interests have received I may mention that my Consular Fees, which amounted to $87.01 for the March Quarter of 1890 which was before the new system began to take effect, were reduced to $53.49 for the March Quarter of 1891 and to $23.82 for the March Quarter of 1892, the diminution for the other quarters being about in the same ratio. The supply of American Goods however has not diminished quite in this ratio, as those which were accounted indispensable for the trade and were small in bulk as compared with cost have come by the expensive steam route via Liverpool.

But there appears to be an error in the assumption that the Colonial Government here is primarily responsible for this special duty of 7%. The party responsible is the "Minister of the Colonies and of Commerce" in Paris. He I understand wanted to make the special duty more than seven per cent, but was restrained by advice from the colony to the effect that a greater rate might drive the natives to the English possessions adjoining and thus defeat the intent of advantage. Senegal it should be borne in mind is not a Colony in the independent sense that Canada & Australia are Colonies. It sends its Deputy to the "Chambre", and is to all essential intents & purposes closely federated with France.

The motive of the French Government in so shaping the Commercial policy of this Colony is to give a greater proportion of its now valuable trade to French Manufacturers and to French vessels. And as some success seems already to have resulted from the means adopted the name Government will not be induced I think lightly to abandon this policy.

Concerning Senegal Gum and whether a threatened duty on this article by us would cause the French Government to relax something in our favor or not I do not feel qualified to give an opinion. I do not know in the first place to what extent it may be a necessity with our manufacturers. If it is a necessity the higher price caused by a duty would of course have to be borne by ourselves, and by augmenting the prices of such articles as it entered into it would by so much incapacitate them for competing in foreign markets. On the contrary if cheap substitutes could be provided in case of an advance in price the balance of Hedgehog advantage might for ought I know be rather against France. Senegal cannot export to us its principal product, Peanuts, because we have a tariff which has proved prohibitive against them. But they come to us just the same in the shape of "Olive Oil" which is manufactured in France, and who knows what might be done with the very limited quantity of Gum Senegal there is in the world if an enhanced price should put it beyond the reach of our own manufacturers? These are merely suggestions on my part. I do not profess to very deeply understand the subject.

I am Sir
Very respectfully & truly yours
P. Strickland
US Consul

Dear Sir:

Duties at the French Colony of Senegal, W.C. Africa.

Besides being large exporters to the Spanish Colonies, we are very largely interested in, not only the French West Indies, but also the French Colony of Senegal, having a branch house at St. Louis in that colony.

Our Manager there, Mr. Francis Lawton, who is also Consular Agent for the United States, under Consul Strickland at Goree, informs us that of late the Provincial Council at St. Louis (the capital of Senegal) have levied a special 7% duty against all foreign importations, other than those coming from the mother country, France, which pay only the 5% general duty, thus making the duty of American imports 12%; or, in other words, placing American goods, which are about the only articles xompeting against those from France, at 7% disadvantage.

On the other hand, this Colony of Senegal, finds its largest and best paying outlet in this country for their Senegal Gum, which is laregly used here among Cotton Manufacturers, Confectioners, etc, etc, and which under our present tariff, is allowed free entry.

But very little, if any other production of Senegal, is imported for consumption in the States, consequently while we can only ship to Senegal under an almost prohibitive protectn duty our Government is allowing them free entry of one of their most important, and largest production, the consequence is, at the present time, our trade, as well as that of the Boston and New York shippers, has been very much reduced, in fact, almost stopped, while the Senegal imports from France have been correspondingly on the increase, and it is only a question of a short time when our U.S. trade with Senegal, which heretofore has assumed to show most favorable new openings for American productions, will be completely lost while they will continue to enjoy the favorable market which we are furnishing them so freely, for their large Gum productions, all, or nearly all of which, comes here via France, where the bulk of the productions of Senegal are controlled and handled.

Yours very truly,
J. H. Hamlen & Son.
