Liberia, Notes.
Department of State Received Jan 9 1891

1039 Lombard Street,
Philadelphia, Pa.
November 20th, 1890.

Sir

In the short interview with which you honored me on Friday last, I informed you I that had been commissioned by the President of Liberia to approach your Government with the hope that you would be induced to make such a demonstration in our favor as will prevent foreign nations from making encroachments on our territory.

Unfortunately for me, the presence of business did not permit of your giving me time to lay the facts before you and you told me to call the next day, which I did and I called again on the following Monday and Tuesday; but with no better success owing to the great pressure of business in your office.

As I had to leave your city, on Tuesday last I make bold to[?] adopt this method of laying the matter before you, trusting that you will be kind enough to give it your consideration.

In the year 1885 a French Man of War came into the harbour of our capital, Monrovia and the Com- mander The Honble James G. Blaine,
Secretary of State
Washington D.C.   mander called upon the President. During the conversation the Commander said that he had stopped at Bereby — a place within our boundary line and had an interview with the chiefs. The President asked "Why did you stop there and have that interview? Do you not know that territory belongs to Liberia?" The Commander replied that he did not know it belonged to Liberia and that he had been instructed by his Government to stop there and have that interview.

After the departure of the vessel, the President instructed our Representative in Paris to call the attention of the French Government to the action of their office and to point out the fact that our boundary on the South East extended to the San Pedro River as you will see by referring to the Imperial Atlas in your Office and that they had virtually admitted it by their Naval Charts and otherwise.

The French Government then claimed that they had commercial treaties with the chiefs of Berreby as old as 1846 I think; but we have Deeds of Cession of that territory made to us as far back as 1836, [unclear, one word]

We laid this matter before your Government and asked for your kind offices; and in a Despatch to Mr. McLean dated, January 13th 1886, Mr. Bayard instructed how to interview and to say to the French Government "We exercise no protectorate over Liberia; but the circumstances, that the Republic originated through the Colonization of American citizens and was established under the foster[?]   ing sanction of this Government give us the right as the next friend of Liberia to aid her in preventing any encroachment of foreign powers on her territorial sovereignty and in settling any dispute that may arise. The South easterly boundary at the River San Pedro has never been questioned and the powerful sanction of general admission for many years."

The matter has remained in abeyance as we supposed since that time; but it lately came to our knowledge that one Captain Binger — who was a prominent officer in the late expedition against the King of Dahomey — claim to have made explorations in the near and South east of Liberia and he has published, in a "Supplement Le Temps Mars 1890," a map representing Liberia as a very narrow strip along the coast, representing the country in the immediate rear of Liberia as far as Musardu as "unexplored" country and liable to become a Protectorate of France, although our Mr. B. Anderson had explored that country as far as Musardu and published an account of this journey and cutting off our territory on the Southeast up to the Cavalla River thus taking the very territory in dispute and which Mr. Bayard says belongs to us by "the powerful sanction of general admission for many years." The territory retaken[?] from us is represented in his map of French Territory.

Whilst the French have not as yet made an open movement towards taking possession of our territory yet, Mr. Secretary under the circumstances we feel warranted   warranted in appealing to your Government as "Liberia's[?] next Friend" and asking you to make such a demonstration in our favor as will prevent Foreign Governments from making such encroachments on our territory. If your powerful Government would make us a present of one of your smallest steam-gunboats to be used in guarding our coast from foreigners and preventing them from settling at points remote from our civilized settlements and tampering with the aborigines we feel that our territorial limits will not be encroached upon. Mr. Secretary my Government believes that if you will be pleased to give us the benefit of your kind offices and and will induce His Excellency the President to recommend the giving of this vessel to Liberia we shall be successful and the very recommendation by the President will indicate to the world the friendship of the United States for our little Republic.

Trusting, Mr. Secretary, that you will bring this matter to the notice of His excellency the President, and that you will advise his recommending it to the Congress and trusting that you will pardon my trespassing on your valuable time.

I have the honor to be
Sir,
Your humble Servant
W. M. Davis
Liberian Commissioner
