Department of State Received Jun 16 1891

Department of State
Monrovia, May 11, 1891.

Sir,

I have the honor by direction to inform you that in the month of July 1890, the President availing himself of a visit of the Honorable Mr. McCall Davis, Attorney General of the Republic, to the United States, thought fit to commission the said offices as a general Commissioner from the Republic of Liberia, to the Government of the United States on behalf of Liberia, with respect to an impending violation of her territorial integrity by certain of the European Powers now engaged in a scramble for the remaining portions of the African Continent not yet appropriated by them.

Mr. Davis returned to Liberia in the month of January last, and his Hon: James G. Blaine
Secretary of State
Washington
DC
U.S.A   his report to the President was to the effect that he had been honored with an interview with the Honorable The Secretary of State at Washington on the 12th November last but that an account of a previous engagement on the part of the Secretary, the interview necessarily terminated in a few moments, to be renewed the next day. That in pursuance of this arrangement he had repeatedly called at the Department of State, but that the Secretary's time being occupied with negotiations of a most important nature between the United States and the South American Republic, it was impossible to obtain further audience of the Secretary, within the time allotted for this stay in the United States.

Mr Davis further reported that before leaving the United States, he had addressed you from Philadelphia, a Despatch under date of 20th November 1890, in which the object sought to be attained by a personal interview was fully set forth, a copy of which I have the   the honor to enclose, but so far no reply has been received to the same.

I am therefore directed to intimate to you Mr. Secretary, that within the last few months, it has come to the knowledge of the Liberian Government, that an arrangement has been entered into between the Governments of Great Britain and France for the delimitation of their respective colonies to the north and south of Liberia, which agreement if carried out in its integrity would have the effect to reduce the Republic to a mere strip along the coast of the Atlantic Ocean, extending coastwise from the Mannah River on the Northwest to Cape Palmas on the Southeast, with an average breadth of not more than 40 miles from the Sea Coast. An inspection of a series of Maps published by the Royal Geographical Society of Belgium shows the proposed French possession on the West Coast   Coast of Africa immediately southwards of Liberia to extend from the British Gold Coast to Cape Palmas, and fixes the furthest interior limits of Liberia at Boporo, assigning the intervening country between Boporo and the Niger as either French Territory, or as liable to become a protectorate of France.

The Government of Liberia on becoming cognizant of this proposed arrangement recently addressed a memorandum to the British Government, protesting against the conclusion of the said Agreement so far as the deprivation of its territory by France is concerned and soliciting the good offices of said Government on its behalf. By command I herewith enclose a similar document soliciting the good offices of the Government of the United States to avert the appropriation of Liberia territory by France, said appropriation being contrary to the Hinterland law recently set up by the European Powers to regulate their section in making fresh requisitions of territory on the African Continent.

 

I have the honor to be
Sir
Your obedient Servant
E. J. Barclay.
