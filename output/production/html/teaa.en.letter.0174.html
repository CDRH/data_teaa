Hôtel de Russie,
Hamburg, July 9, 1862

Sir,

Having left Liberia in March duly vested with authority to transact any necessary official business for the government of Liberia with any other government, it was my original purpose to visit the United States of America, after having finished any business with Her Britannic Majesty's government. Being anxious to negotiate a treaty on behalf of the government of Liberia with the United States government, I have for nearly three months been watching the progress of the passage of the bill by To Hon. W. H. Seward, Sec. of State, &c., &c., &c.   by Congress recognizing and establishing diplomatic relations between the United States government and the government of Liberia, the passage of which I was highly gratified to see announced in the papers a week or two before I left London; and I would have immediately gone over to the United States for the purpose aforesaid, but was deterred by the consideration that very likely the great pressure of business upon, and the prosperity and anxiety of the government of the United States, growing out of the unrighteous rebellion of some of the States, might render such a proposition inopportune, just now, to secure such speedy action by the government of the United States as the necessary shortness of my visit there would require.

As   As I purpose returning to London, and shall probably then decide whether I will visit the United States early in August, or return to Liberia from London, the object of this communication is simply to inquire what will be Your Excellency's pleasure in the promises; first, whether Your Excellency favors the negotiation, on behalf of the United States government, of a treaty of amity and commerce with the government of Liberia: if so, then through what medium do you suggest that it shall be affected? by my visiting the United States for that purpose, or your government empowering some one to effect that object with me in London? Either method will be agreeable to me. I am very desirous, and I think it very important, that   a treaty should be negotiated between the two governments before my return to Liberia and the commencement of the session of the Liberian Legislature. The Hon. Mr. Adams, your minister at the Court of St. James, has already signified to me the pleasure it would afford him to do so, if duly authorized by his government.

I shall feel particularly obliged if Your Excellency can, amid the pressure of business, conveniently place one in possession of a reply to this communication in all of next month (August).

I have the honor to be Your obdt. servant,
Stephen A. Benson
