Treasury Department
Feb 10th 1862

Sir.

I have the honor to return herewith the letter of C. P. Clark of 20th Ulto & accompanying papers, upon which you have requested my views.

I find by a reference to the correspondence of this Department with the Department of State that Mr Secy Cass on the 4 Feb 1859 addressed Mr Secy Cobb, a letter requesting to know upon what "terms Liberian vessels will be admitted into the ports of the U States." Mr Cobb replied on 8th Feb. /59 – "as the law now stands Liberian vessels would be subjected in ports of the U States to a duty of One dollar per ton, and their Cargoes to a discriminating duty of 10 pr ct." He adds–"Whether in view of the information   communicated by the US. Consul they should be exempted from the discriminating duties of tonnage and imposts, is for the President to determine under authority conferred upon him by the 1st Section of the Act 24 May 1828." Mr. Cobb returned the papers upon which this letter was founded to your Department.

By the Act of 24 May 1828 (Laws Vol 4. p. 308) where "no discriminating duties of tonnage or impost are levied in the ports of any "foreign nation" upon vessels of the US," the President of the U States is authorized to issue his proclamation declaring that the foreign discriminating duties of tonnage and impost within the U States shall be suspended, so far as respects the vessels of said foreign nation and the produce or merchandize imported into the U. States   in the vessels from said foreign nation"

Proclamations under this Act have been issued by John Q. Adams, in behalf of Hanover, and by Andrew Jackson in favor of Austria and Oldenburg.

I have no question that Liberia is de facto a nation, and a "foreign nation": and so soon as the act now before Congress shall have passed Liberia will then be a nation within the provisions of the Act of 24 May 1828. In the mean time it is perhaps, inexpedient to raise the question whether Liberia, being already a foreign nation within the equitable constitution of the Act and imposing no discriminating duties on American tonnage or imports, the President should, in absence of formal recognition, issue the proclamation as in cases of Hanover, Austria and Oldenburg.

With great respect
S P Chase
Secretary of the Treasury

Hon Wm H Seward
Secretary of State
 

Department of State,
Washington, January 27, 1862.

Hon. S. P. Chase,
Secretary of the Treasury.

Sir:

Your letter of the 23d instant, acknowledging the receipt of C. P. Clark's which had been addressed to this Department, and enclosing another letter form Mr. Clark, addressed to you, has been received.

In regard to the papers which Mr. Clark requests you to obtain from this Department, the one first mentioned merely contained a printed copy of a message from President Benson to the Legislature of Liberia, which has been mislaid, but a new copy of which has just been procured and is herewith transmitted to you; the second (of 23d August, 1859) was never received; and the third and last (dated 5th November, 1858)   was sent to the Secretary of the Treasury in a letter from this Department of 4th February, 1859.

I beg leave to suggest that all the additional information desired may be found in the Report of the Foreign Commerce made by the Secretary of State for 1858– Part 2d of Ex. Docs. No. 4, H.R., 36th Congress, 1st session, pp. 207, 208, and 287, and also in the similar Report for 1860–Part 2d of Ex. Docs. No. 6, Senate, 36th Congress, 2d Session, pp. 171, 172, and 238.

Mr. Clark's letter to you is herewith returned.

I am, sir, your obedient servant,
F. W. Seward,
Acting Secretary
