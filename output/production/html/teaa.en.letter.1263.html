Subject.

Scarcity of Food and Water supplies.

 

No. 24, Consular Series
Legation of the United States,
Monrovia Liberia,
Mar. 21, 1899.

Hon. David J. Hill,
Assistant Secretary of State,
Washington.

Sir:

I have the honor to transmit to you the intelligence, that food and water supplies are very scarce; and high proportionately! The scarcity of food may be accounted for, first; owing to the decline in the price of coffee; the staple and principal production of the Liberian farmer. The decline, in the price of coffee, has almost paralysed the prosperity of the Republic.

Secondly; the unsettled state of affairs, between the interior tribes and the Liberian Government, has   has brought about a state of non-commercial intercourse, between the former and the latter. Thirdly; the prevalence of small pox, among the native fishermen, has brought about a voluntary temporary quaranty[?] between the native fish markets and the Liberian.

If a beef is to be butchered, the owner of it; propositions it out to his near relatives and friends, before it is killed. The salesman is guided by a list in parcelling out the beef, and a stranger with money in hand and the most urgent plea cannot purchase it!

Having no reservoir nor artificial water supplies; the few who have wells on their lots, are charging as high as 1 cent per gallon or two dollars per month for water   water! The tension, may in part, be broken, in about 6 weeks, as we usually have showers, in the latter part of April.

I have the honor to be your obedient servant;
Owen L. W. Smith,
Consul General.
