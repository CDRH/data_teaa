Subject.

Report of official visit &c., to Cape Coast Castle, Consular Agency, Jan. 30 - Feb'y 25, 1899.

 

No. 21. Consular Series.
Legation of the United States,
Monrovia Liberia,
Feb'y 28, 1899.

Hon. David J. Hill,
Assistant Secretary of State,
Washington.

Sir:

Having made a personal visit to the Cape Coast Castle, Consular Agency, Gold Coast Colony, Africa, I have the honor to transmit to you the following report, viz.

1. I beg to be permitted to correct an erroneous misstatement, in my No. 12. "Consular," of Nov. 16, 1898, in which I said, upon the authority of the Steamship Company's Agent, that the distance from Monrovia to Cape Coast Castle was 11 days   11 days journey, or over "2000 miles." We made the distance in ten days. The Captain of the Steamer informed me that it was between 900 and 1000 miles.

2. Before reaching Cape Coast Castle, I learned that the former resident and office of George E. Eminsang Esq., late United States Consular Agent, at Cape Coast Castle, was at Elmina, about 8 miles west of Cape Coast Castle, even, before reaching the Cape. Hence I disembarked, at Elmina, Feb'y 8, 1899.

3. Upon inquiry, I soon learned that J. E. Eminsang Esq., son of the late George E. Eminsang, and the person whom I had temporarily appointed Consular Agent,   Agent, in August 1898, was living at Cape Coast Castle. On the 9th Feb'y I telegraphed him to come to Elmina. He came in the afternoon of the same day. He informed me that the Colonial Secretary, at Accra, had refused to grant him an exequatur, upon the ground, that his appointment should have came from the Secretary of State, Washington. Hence, the younger Eminsang had not been in the discharge of the duties of the Agency. Having sent for him, and finding myself unable to speak the [unclear, one word] tongue, I employed, J. E. Eminsang, as clerk, interpreter and guide, for two days.

4. I found the office fixtures, archives &c., in the room   room, formerly, occupied by George E. Eminsang Esq., late United States Consular Agent. They were in a very bad and much dilapidated condition. After, taking a full inventory of same, I purchased a new lock, as the key to the old lock was in the possession of the administrator, had it put on the case containing archives &c., locked it; and by permission of J. E. Eminsang Esq., son of the late George E. Eminsang deceased, I left the case, archives &c., in the room, which was locked, taking the key to the case with me. In case the Agency is cautioned see accompanying requisition for official supplies, for said Agency.

5. The last official quarterly report   report made to the Legation, by the late George E. Eminsang, was made June 30, 1897. His son J. E. Eminsang, who acted as clerk for his father, says his father made up a subsequent report, for the quarter ending September 30, 1897, but on account of ill health never forwarded said report to the Legation. The records of the Agency show that the work was regularly discharged, upto Feb'y 28, 1898; one note of protest, the which I forward to the Auditor for State and other Departments, is duly executed by the Captain of the ship, and bears date of March 1898. Accompanying this note of protest C. C. Aikins Esq., Administrator of the estate of the late George E. Eminsang, [illegible]   entered Gold Coast Colony ports during 1897.

Sail vessels cleared from Gold Coast Colony ports, with cargoes for the United States, in 1897, 7, tonnage 3799, crews 64."

7. Willie F. Hutchison Esq., whom I some time since, recommended as Consular Agent, at Cape Coast Castle Agency, is still in London, with no certainty when he will return. I therefore take pleasure in nominating Fynn E. Asaam, Esq., of Cape Coast Castle, a man who was educated in England, and is very highly recommended to me as a man suitable for the position. He is about 38 years of age.

 

8. In returning, I was forced to embark upon the fast Royal Mail Steamship, Axim, from Cape Coast Castle to Sierra Leone, with the hopes of making connection at Sierra Leone, for Monrovia; or to make an indefinite stay in Cape Coast Castle, awaiting an all port Steamer by the way of Monrovia; but after geting on board the Steamer, and before leaving the Cape, the Captain received a telegram to call at Monrovia for Bishop Grant of America. Hence, I availed myself of the opportunity of getting off at Monrovia, without going by the way of Sierra Leone. I have the honor to be your obedient servant;
Owen L. W. Smith C. G.
