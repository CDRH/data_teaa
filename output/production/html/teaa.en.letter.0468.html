Commercial Agency
Stanley Falls, Kongo Free State
March 17th 1893.

To the Honorable
Assistant Secretary of State
Washington, D.C.

Sir:

I have the honor to inform you of my departure from Stanley Pool February 9th and arrival at this place March 9th.

The situation here is extremely interesting as the State has began its war against the Arabs who occupy the government territory to the Eastward of the red line marked on the enclosed chart.

In the South Lieutenant Dhanis Commissary of the Kassai District with headquarters at Lousambo has with him six thousand men eight hundred State soldiers, and five thousand natives under the command of Kongo Lutete, a former slave of Tippo Tibs, but who declared himself free, and has thrown his whole force in with the Government. This army is marching to Kassongo and Nyangone, possibly to Lake Tanganyka where they would meet the troops of the anti-slave society under command of Captain Jacques. Mr. Dhanis met the Arabs near Nyangone under command of Seffon, (a son of Tippo Tibs) and gained a victory killing some thousand men, and capturing twelve hundred guns. The next day the town was captured and the Chief Munnci Moherra & his son killed and Seffon badly wounded. He is now on his way to Kassongo, after which his movements are uncertain. This two Arab chiefs killed at Nyangone were the ones who massacred Mr. Hodister's expedition last year between Bena Kamba and Riba Riba. In the month of December they also killed the two State officers who had been their prisoners for nine months in Kassongo.

An expedition consisting of two large river steamers and one thousand men leaves Basoko this month to co-operate with Mr. Dhanis using Bena Kamba as a base of supplies. This force goes down the Lomami River, and is under the command of Commandante Charltain. He has been kind enough to ask me to accompany him as his guest and I have accepted the invitation.

This most aggressive action against the Arabs has been taken in accordance with Chapter I Article I Paragraphs 2. and 6. of the General Act of Brussels for the suppression of the slave trade.

The Arabs are gradually forcing themselves to the Westward, and are now hunting slaves in the Lopori Tschonapa and Loukenye Rivers. The captured negroes are used as money to purchase ivory and if the ivory is sent to the East Coast they are sold to dealers from the North.

It is impossible for me to write lengthy reports on the subject while I am so constantly on the move, but I have been keeping a journal in which every thing of interest I have so far seen is noted and when I finish shall write out a full report and present it to the Department in person.

I am, Sir,
Your obedient Servant
R. Dorsey Mohun
Commercial Agent
Kongo Free State
