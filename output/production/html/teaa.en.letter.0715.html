Department of State Received Jan 2 1885

United States Consulate
St Paul de Loanda
November 11: 1884

Sir

During the last month I have been on a visit along the coast in one of the mail steamers as far as Mayumba and also visited Banana Landana and Loango and returned here in H.M.S. Flirt on the 23rd October, the Captain having been kind enough to give me a passage to Loanda.

As the particulars of my trip may be of interest to the Department I beg to submit same. At Banana I met Sir Sir Francis de Winton, Chief Administrator of the International Association, and from a conversation I had with him he seems anxious to eradicate certain bad opinions that are prevalent among the traders affirming that he is willing to assist them in every way and glad to see them going ahead and following in the footsteps of the Association, and that he has no idea whatever of putting any difficulty in the way of any person wishing to open trade. He is trying to get things into good working order as up to the present there has been no system whatever and   and a great waste of money.

He talks of a small railway from Yur[?] to Stanley Pool a distance of about 230 miles to open up the country calculating that the coast would be £2000,000. but to show any result he would require a traffic of at least 100 tons a day at freight of about £4 pr ton. I think this would be a very bad investment as there are not many qualities of African produce that could afford to pay such a price unless it was obtained in the interior for mere normal value and I must say that I do not see where the 100 tons a day are to come from.

I I also met here Captain Grant Elliott, Administrator for the Association at Loango, and his idea is a railway from the Quillo and up the Niarri valley which would be almost a direct line to Stanley Pool and much less expensive as they would not have such a mountainous district to go through.

Lately[?] in the Congo between Nokki and Boma the Dutch House had two factories, one at Caicamazio[?] and the other at Mussuca burnt down by the natives, suffering great loss, there being at the time over 6000 lbs of ivory and 7 tons of rubber besides a a great quantity of goods for trade; the loss is estimated at £15,000.

The Dutch have attacked the natives and burnt a number of villages and taken the King and Queen of Caicamazio prisoners.

I found the German Man of War "Moewe" had been at Banana taking Coal and sailed for Angra Pequena on the 16th September. She had Dr Nachtigal on board and I heard that a large German Expedition is reported as about to start form Angra Pequena for the Upper Congo, so no doubt Dr Nachtigal has gone there to meet them

At Landana I found the French French Man of War "Segond" and the Portuguese Gunboat "Sado" with the Secretary of the Government of Angola on board, owing to a question between the French missionaries established there and the natives about some ground that they (the missionaries) had taken charge of, and they had requested a Man of War to come to their assistance, as they would not recognize the authority of the Portuguese Delegate who was in charge, as Landana was supposed to be within the Portuguese Protectorate.

The first question the French took up was that Landana was within the disputed territory of 5° 12' & 8° south, and after the Observations observations had been taken by the officers of both ships it was determined that Landana was not within the Portuguese Protectorate, but within the disputed territory, so the French were perfectly justified in settling the question with the missionaries without the interference of the Portuguese.

The line of 5° 12' south comes nearly to the south bank of the Chiloango river, so that Landana is within the territory of 5° 12' south and 8° south and the Protectorate of Massabi and Landana is really of very little importance as nearly all the trade is passed through Landana; no doubt it is a great disappointment to the Portuguese but but as they have got a treaty with the Chief at Landana they may take charge of it at some future day. I think it little else but just that they should as the natives with few exceptions talk Portuguese and any palaver whatever is obliged to be decided in that language

At Loango I visited the merchants and the Chief of the International Association who gave some information that may be useful about the disputed points on the coast. I therefore beg to enclose a sketch map. The colours Ochre show the territory occupied by the French.

Do "Green" which the French are disputing with the Association.

Do "Blue" the Portuguese Protectorate of Massabi and Landana as it is now admitted to be.

The red points are places at which the Association has stations. At Loango there is a French Lieutenant and some soldiers who are in constant questions with the Association as they claim the territory from Granville to Ponta Banda, and are giving them as much trouble as they possibly can, as they say they wish to pay them off here for the manner in which De Brazza was was treated by the Association at Stanley Pool

On arrival of any vessel on the coast it is usual for the Merchant to hoist their flags to show their nationality and I was surprised when I went on shore accompanied by the captain of the Man of War to see the Merchants hauling down their flags, and was informed by them that the French Commandante has issued a circular informing them that they were not allowed to hoist flags except on Sundays or when the mail steamer came. Here the houses are two American and four Portuguese but the trade is nearly all direct with England I I noticed at several of the French places all the Merchants are obliged to hoist the French flag no matter what nation they belong to and I hear that any vessel proceeding up the river above Gaboon are obliged to hoist French colors.

Black Point is also a French station, and there is here established a sailor and 4 or 5 soldiers, but is a place of little importance as to trade. I touched at several other places on the coast, but I have nothing of importance to relate regarding them. Trade is bad and there are great complaints all along the coast.

I am
Sir
Your most Obedient Servant,
Robt S. Newton
U.S. Vice Consul

The Assistant Secretary of State
Washington D.C.
