1. Report–"Ex-Minister E. E. Smith's Mission."

 

Ex-Minister E. E. Smith's Mission.

The Bark "Liberia" arrived at this port on the 6th day of April, 1892, bringing fifty Emigrants (14 families) who came from the vicinity of Little Rock, Arkansas (U.S.A.) Beverly Y. Payne of this city was deputized by the American Colonization Society to receive and care for them until the arrival of Ex-Minister E. E. Smith who had been appointed the Company's Agent, in place of Mr. Scipio King, removed.

Mr. Smith arrived (via England) on the evening of April 13th.

The Emigrants peferred to settle on the Mesurado River, near Johnsonville, about 15 miles from Monrovia. Land was given them in accordance with the Liberian allotment–10 Acres to unmarried persons over 18 years of age, 25 Acres to heads of families or 20 Acres and one town lot as they preferred.

The Society furnishes supplies for six months and by that time it is expected that each family will have raised enough   enough Cassada, rice, and garden vegetables to supply them with food.

Three families (14 persons) finding that they would be obliged to wait three or four years for their coffee trees to bear before they could expect to handle much[?], money, having to undergo an acclimation process, and having no one to plan or[?] rather outline work for them as they do in America, re-embarked for America the[?] next voyage that the Liberia made J[unclear, one word] 18th., 1892.

The other families are getting along very well and express themselves as being well satisfied. One has his own house completed and has planted over five thousand coffee scions. Another has opened a business house and is doing well.

Mr. Smith left on the Steamer Sarache, on August 12th., leaving Mr. Beverly Y. Payne as his Deputy.

Among the recommendations that Mr. Smith will make to the Society are[?] the following:

1. That instead of 14 or 15 families (40 to[?] 50 persons), from 80 to 100 families (400   (400 to 500 persons) be sent out even if they be not sent as often.

2. That the companies be made up of mechanics and artisans as well as farmers, instead of nearly all farmers as in the past.

3. That they be sent in a steamer instead of the cramped quarters of a 300 ton Bark.

4. That the Liberian Government use the appropriation made (see Acts of the General Assembly, Inclosure of Despatch No. 11, Page 15) in preparing a receptacle or receptacles for the Emigrants and in having a sufficiently large garden planted with vegetables that shall be ready for use by the time said Emigrants arrive.

5. That inasmuch as nearly all planting and cultivating is done with the hoe that Emigrants be encouraged to bring improved machines and adopt modern methods which will yield them much larger returns, besides the repairing and even making of which will furnish employment to the mechanics and artisans referred to in Sec. 2, above.

6. That Emigrants be supplied nine months   months instead of six as at present. This[?] will give them more time to acclimate and[?] get their houses and surroundings in a more comfortable condition.

All of which is respectfully submitted.

William D. McCoy
Minister-Resident and Consul-General
