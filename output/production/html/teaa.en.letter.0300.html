Rec April 28th
No 2.

Consulate of the United States of America, at Sierra Leone 16th March 1866.

Sir,

I have the honor to inform the Department of a great and important change in Her Majesty's Government with regard to the colonies of her dominion on this Coast.

The four colonies Gambia, Sierra Leone, (this Colony includes the Sherbro Island and a very large tract of the mainland interior of the island and known as the Sherbro Country) Lagos and Cape Coast, which heretofore have been separate Governments, are now consolidated in one, under a Governor General His Excellency Samuel Wensley Blackall, until this change Governor To the
Hon W. H. Seward
Secretary of State
&c &c &c Governor of the Colony of Sierra Leone.

His residence will be in this city, (Freetown) which will, because of its central position, and commercial importance, be the head-quarters of the new style of Government.

I respectfully desire to request the Department to inform me, if section 420, in Chapter 26 of the Consular Regulations which reads thus, "Consuls general are entitled to a salute of nine guns, consuls to a salute of seven guns, vice consuls, consular agents, commercial agents, and vice commercial agents, to a salute of five guns;" is now in force.

The U.S.S. "Kearsarge", Commander A.D. Harrell, arrived in this port on the 7th of March last, I visited the vessel, and on leaving received no salute, salute, governing myself by the above section of the Consular Regulations, I wrote to Commander Harrell asking his reasons for with-holding the usual salute, and received in answer, "I have to say in reply, that although I have thirty years experience in the Navy, I have never seen an officer of your grade (Consular Agent) saluted. According to the Naval Regulations, I am not permitted to salute any person below the rank of Consul." this is my reason for desiring information on the subject, because if my Regulations are not correct (the book is dated 1856) there are newer ones, and it is necessary I should have them, as Commander Harrell told me, U.S. vessels of war would, hereafter, frequently visit this port for the purpose of coaling. This is the first vessel of war of the United States which has visited this port since the date of my commission in September 1860, and as others are coming, I desire to be perfectly acquainted with all that is required of me, and all that is due to me, in my official intercourse with such vessels, of Chapter 26 in the Regulations does not cover the whole subject.

I forgot to mention that the island of Bulamo, at the mouth of the Rio Grande river, is a dependency of this colony of Sierra Leone.

I would recommend, when sending official paper is a place like this, it should be white, as the blue deteriorates very rapidly.

I have the honor to be
Sir,
Your ob'd't servant
Henry Rider
U.S. Consular Agent
