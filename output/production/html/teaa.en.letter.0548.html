No. 23.
Department of State Received Jan 27 1891

Consulate of the United States,
Loanda, November 19, 1890.

Mr. Ed Bannister
To the Department of State.

Subject:

Claim of Mrs Holmes.

Abstract of Contents.

Reply to claim, and grounds of sauce, as set forth by Mrs Holmes, widow of late Captain Holmes of barque "Sea Fox," for effects of his alleged to have been stolen from that vessel while at Anchor in Loanda.

 
No 23.

Consulate of the United States,
Loanda, November 19, 1890

Honorable W. F. Wharton
Assistant Secretary of State,
Washington, D.C.

Sir:

I have the honor to acknowledge the receipt of your despatch dated September 11, 1890, enclosing a copy of a letter from Mrs Adelaide W Holmes to the Department of State in which that lady claims the sum of Rs[?] 360.00 for property belonging to her late husband and alleged by her to have been robbed from the Cabin of the barque "Sea Fox" (of which vessel he was Captain) when the arrived in Loanda and was placed in the charge of the officials of the Port. This claim she makes on the ground of the "inefficiency or incompetency neglect" of the Consul at Loanda.

 

The whole of the Sad circumstances attending the explosion on board the vessel in question were reported at the time by Vice Consul Newton to the Department. I will therefore confine my reply, to the allegations contained in the claim set forth.

1st "That with the assistance of a Portuguese national vessel their lying[?] in or near Equimina the "Sea Fox" was taken to St. Paul de Loanda."

The Portuguese national vessel took a number of burnt and wounded men to Loanda but had nothing to do with taking the ship there. The Vice Consul sent a man by the Earliest opportunity to bring her to this port.

2nd "That at the instance of said consul or through his agency, Customs officers were put on board the said "Sea Fox""

The Customs officers were sent on board by the Director of Customs as   as is done according to law in every case of arrival of a vessel in port, and the Consul has nothing whatever to do with such proceeding.

3rd "And that during the time the ship was in charge of the said officials, the Captain's entire effects, previously scheduled by the Consul or his agent, and consisting of clothing &c, &c. were stolen and taken from the ship."

When the vessel arrived here the V Consul was at Mossamedes very ill, and the writer acting for him, immediately proceeded on board and found the entire space which had once been occupied by the Cabin, a black, foul smelling hole without a vestige of Cabin from side to side and from deck to Keelson, the result of the explosion, and the fire consequent thereon which the crew said raged for some time.

Burnt and otherwise mutilated portions   portions of written matter, unrecognizable as documents of importance or otherwise, were lying[?] scattered about the desk which was shewn with wreckage caused by the explosion. On my asking if anything belonging to the Captain had been saved (simply with a view to secure for his friends some souvenir), the reply was in the following terms as far as my memory serves me, and I know that at the time if made a great impression on me, considering the awful circumstances: "My God Sir, do you think any one thought of anything else when he recovered from the shock but putting out the fire and saving the ship from going right down under us"

There remained more than 20 men of the crew, and they demanded to be taken ashore   and provided for. I had to make provision for them (they had neither food nor place to sleep in), and also provide for the ship's safety and that of her cargo. The man who brought the vessel from Equimina said he would not have gone for her if he had known what he would have to put up with. He had to sleep (when he could) on the open deck, there was no food except a little fish and sweet potatoes, and when he arrived here he was in a high state of fever.

I would ask respectfully if under such circumstances the Consul's duty was to hurt up a few trinkets or other articles (which I am firmly convinced did not exist), or do his duty by the ship, her cargo and the crew?

"The Captain's entire effects previously   previously scheduled by the Consul or his agent."

I will refrain from making any comment on this assertion, but respectfully ask the Department to demand from the concocters of this claim the list of effects "scheduled by the Consul or his Agent" with the signature of the Consul, or his agent attached.

4th. "And that upon representation of the robbery being made to the Consul by the Captain who had been sent to St Paul de Loanda to bring the ship to her home port, the Consul replied that nothing could he done about" it".

This of course implies that a list as set forth in the claim and "as scheduled by the Consul" or his agent" really existed. Let them produce it. In the absence of Mr Newton, late Vice Consul, I have no means of verifying   verifying the truth of such alleged conversation. Considering however the character of Mr Newton, the conscientious manner in which he performed the duties of this Consulate for a period extending over twenty years as I gather from the archives, and his willingness at all times to assist in getting justice done even where he is not personally concerned, confirm me in the opinion that either the whole truth regarding such reply of his as alleged, is suppressed, or that it is entirely imaginary on the part of Captain Luce or whoever is responsible for the statement.

To establish the claim I again submit that it is necessary for the claimant to produce the list "as scheduled by the Consul or his Agent" duly authenticated by the signature of   of such Consul or Agent; and in conclusion would ask if the Consul is responsible to the owners for safe delivery of property from the time their agent takes change in a foreign port until he delivers such property in the home port. If any robbery was committed it may have been done in such interval.

I trust the Department will accept my assurance that all the foregoing is simply in answer to the claim and the terms in which it is made, and I should regret very much if any single phrase should be construed as showing a want of the high respect due from me to the Department.

I am, Sir,
Your most obedient
servant
Ed Bannister.
Vice Consul.
