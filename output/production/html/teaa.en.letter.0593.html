Consulate of the United States at Bathurst 11th August 1859

Sir:

I have the honor to enclose herewith The Quarterly Return of Arrivals & Departures of American vessels for the Quarter ending the 30th of June last also The Quarterly Return of Fees for the same period amounting to sixty two Dollars and fifty six cents.

You will also find herewith a copy of the Treaty between France and England relative to Portendie and Albreda, and the free navigation of the Gambia, conceded to France by the latter power.

I have indirectly learned that Her Majesty's Government has sent a Despatch to the present Honorable Lewis Cass
Secretary of State of the United States
&c &c &c
Washington   Acting Governor of these settlements the Contents of which are to this purpose, that, the Home Government experienced many difficulties in regard to the right of free navigation of the Gambia accorded to the French, from the Powers with whom England has treaties with the "most favored nation" clause; that it could be agreeable to the Home Government to have the opening of the river to the whole world made the subject of a petition from the Colonists.

I am inclined to believe that the Colonists, particularly the Commercial part of the community are opposed to this scheme, as they also were to the opening of the River to the French, and but to little purpose.

The advantages to the desired from the force navigation of the Gambia, as far as the United States are concerned, are at   present, perhaps, unimportant. But I am convinced that the future will tend to increase those advantages.

The river is navigable to vessels drawing not more than eight feet of water as far as Mc' Carthy's Island, the principal British settlement, and about two hundred miles above this. The principal articles of produce procured in the River are Ground Nuts (Pea Nuts), of which the annual export for the past year was 15,728 tons; requiring a considerable amount of shipping to bring them here to market.

About 70,000 Hides and 100 tons of Beeswax exported in the same period of time.

This carrying trade is now performed by British vessels almost entirely. Most of the French vessels being too large or drawing too much wake to ascend   the river to any distance. American vessels from their peculiarly flat build and large deck capacity are better adapted than either British or French for this carrying trade.

The River trade as at present carried on is not a very profitable one. There are Eight English & three French Houses engaged in it from this port. Prices obtained for goods, in the River, are large and remunerative, but the disadvantages and losses arise 1st. From employing native Mundingos (in whose language there appears to be no such word as honesty) for traders. 2d From the number of small vessels required to bring the produce to town, the expense of which, increases very considerably the price of the produce.

It has occurred to me that this matter would be worthy the consideration of the United States   and that perhaps the present would be favorable moment for bringing it before Her Majesty's Government.

Should it be the wish of the Department I should be glad upon the return of the healthy season commencing in November or December to ascend the River as far as Mc'Carthy's Island and make a report thereon.

I regret that my present state of health requires a chance of climate and ascending to the order of my medical man I shall be obliged to leave my Consulate for the coming two months and proceed to the Island of Teneriffe.

I have appointed Mr. Richard Charles Lloyd Acting Consul during my absence.

I have the honor to be
Sir
Your most obdt servt
Danl RB Upton
United States Consul

Imperial Decree

The Convention relative to Portendie and Albreda Concluded between France and England the 7th of March 1857.

Napoleon:
By the Grace of God and the National will Emperor of the French:

To all to whom these presents shall come greeting:

Upon the report of our Minister Secretary of State for the Department of Foreign Affairs. We have decreed and do hereby decree as follows:

Article 1st. A Convention having been signed on the 7th of March 1859 between France and the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland to regulate the right of trade at or near Portendie on the Western Coast of Africa, as also in regard to Albreda on the River Gambia and ratifications of this act having been exchanged at London on the 25th of the   the present month of March: the said Convention the tenor of which is as follows shall receive fall and entire execution.

His Majesty the Emperor of the French and Her Majesty the Queen of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland wishing for the future to put an end to all causes of discord relative to the right of trade at or near Portendie on the Western Coast of Africa, as also in regard to Albreda on the River Gambia a right to which both Governments laid claim and also the subjects of France and Great Britain, they have resolved to hold a Convention to that effect and have ruined[?] for their Plenipotentiaries respectively, as follows: His Majesty the Emperor of the French Mr. Jean Gilbert Victor Fialin Count Persigny, Senator, Grand Cross of the Imperial order of the Legion of Honor, Grand "Cordon" of the Imperial Order of the Medjidie of Turkey, Grand Cross of the order of Saint Maurice and Lazarus of Sardinia, Grand Cross of the   the order of Danebury of Denmark and His Ambassador near Her Britannic Majesty. And Her Majesty the Queen of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland: The Right Honorable George William Frederic Earl of Clarendon, Baron Hyde of Hindon, Peer of the United Kingdom Her Britannic Majesty's Privy Councillor, Knight of the very noble order of the Garter, Knight Grand Cross of the very noble order of the Bath, principal Secretary of State of Her Britannic Majesty for Foreign affairs: and the Right Honorable Henry Laborchere, Her Britannic Majesty's Priory Councilor, Member of Parliament and Her Britannic Majesty's Principal Secretary of State for the Colonies, who having duly and mutually communicated their respective full powers which were found good and in due form they have decided upon and concluded the following articles.

Article 1st. Her Majesty the Queen of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland renounces the right which treaties   treaties up to the present time gave to the subjects of Her Britannic Majesty to trade from the mouth of the River Saint John as far as the Bay and the Fort of Portendie inclusively.

Article 2. His Majesty the Emperor of the French cedes to Her Majesty of Great Britain the French Territory or settlement of Albreda on the northern bank of the River Gambia Western Coast of Africa, as also all the rights of possession and others that may pertain to the said Factory.

Article 3. Her Britannic Majesty consents or grants to French subjects free access to the River Gambia for carrying on their trade. They are permitted to reside in Bathurst or at such other commercial treaties as may be at some future time designated by Her Majesty in Council, but at no other points, same[?] as concerns Albreda, where the French who are now resident and desire to remain are authorized by the fourth article of the present Convention so to do: provided always, they conform to   to the local laws and regulations which are or may be in force in the British settlements on the Gambia.

French vessels in the River Gambia shall be subject to the same duties charges and regulations as British vessels and all articles imported or exported by French vessels shall pay the same duties as are imposed on similar articles imported or exported by British vessels.

The French Government will be allowed to have a Consular Agent at Bathurst: it being well understood that the said Consular Agent shall be previously recognized and acknowledged in the ordinary form by Her Britannic Majesty's Government.

Article 4th. French subjects already established at Albreda who may wish to remain after its session to Her Britannic Majesty shall retain their property and the rights appertaining to the same and shall be protected, and treated in all respects in the same manner as   as all other French subjects in Her Britannic Majesty's possessions.

Article 5th. The present Convention to be ratified and the ratifications to be exchanged as soon as possible at London and within three weeks following the signing of the said Convention

In faith of which the Plenipotentiaries have respectively signed and sealed with their arms.

Done at London, the seventh day of March in the year of Grace 1857

L.S. signed F' de Persigny
" " Clarendon
" " Henry Labouchere
