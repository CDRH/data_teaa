Department of State
Monrovia, Liberia
May 15th 1879

My Lord

Referring to my dispatch of Oct. 16th 1878, in which I informed your Lordship that the Liberian government had appointed two Commissioners to meet the Mixed Commission on the North Western Boundary Question: I have the honor to express the very deep regret of His Excellency the President that the said commission failed to accomplish the results so anxiously desired.

I need not stop here to give you a detailed account of the tedious The Most Honourable

Commissioners, and then for their instructions from the Foreign Office; for no doubt you have been fully apprised of this by the despatches that have reached you, both from His Excellency Governor Rowe, as also from your Commissioners.

As much as this Government regret the inconvenience and expense to which both the Republic and many of her citizens were subjected in consequence of H. M. Commissioners not being prepared to meet until three weeks after the time fixed for the commencement of the   of the session yet believing that I arose from unavoidable causes they waive all complaint on that score. But where after the meeting of the Commission points arose upon which the Commission disagreed, this Government fail to see upon what Grounds H. M. Commission was refused to submit those points to the Arbitration of the United States Government. This occurred first in Sierra Leone where a point of disagreement arose, which in the opinion of this Government should have been submitted to the U.S. Arbitrator Commodore Sufeldt; according to the terms of the Draft Proposals which has been suggested by H. M. Government and accepted by Liberia as the basis of the investigation. To such a course   course H. M. Commissioners persistently objected. The Commissioners on the part of this Government, firm in the conviction of the justice of Our Claims, and anxious for a settlement of the Question; yielded.

An adjournment was agreed upon by the Mixed Commission, in pursuance of which they met at Solymah on the 1st of April last. The result of eighteen days deliberation-during which the Commission examined the Title deeds of this Government and took oral testimony of witnesses brought forward on the part of this Government as well as on behalf of the native Chiefs repudiating our Claims to Sovereignty, was another disappointments. The Liberia Commissioners holding   holding that we had proved our title to the territories known as "Sugaree," "Manna, Rock & River" and "Solymah" and H. M. Commissioners averring that no such countries existed.

The Liberian Commissioners submitted that, according to the terms of the Draft Proposals, mentioned above, and the Instructions of both the English and Liberian Commissioners, the matter should be referred to the American Arbitration for his decision.

This your Commissioners refused to do, averring their inability to do more than take the opinion of the American Arbitration, and claiming that such opinion could have no effect in settling the Question unless   unless approved by H. M. Government. In the face of their instructions and the Draft Proposals above cited our Commissioners could not agree to this and the Commission adjourned.

Unless, My Lord, the Liberian Government have utterly failed to understand the terms of the Draft Proposals, accepted by both governments as the basis for the settlement of this question, I beg most respectfully to insist that there is a manifest error on the part of H. M. Commissioners.

I say error because I cannot conceive that, in the face of the very positive instructions emanating from H. M. Government enjoining   enjoining upon the Commissioners "strict impartiality" they could allow themselves to be influenced by any other than proper motives.

I am quite aware that there is a very strong effort put forth on the part of British Traders in that Neighborhood to render successful the efforts of the Aborigines to repudiate over authority, and while we have abundant reason to believe that the dismemberment, aye even the extinction of Liberia would not in the opinion of these Traders be too great a price at which to secure for themselves the produce of that country, Yet I am sure that there is too high a sense of justice and honor not   not to say magnanimity in H. M. government, as well as those who represent her in official relations, to sympathise with any such selfish feelings. And I cherish the opinion that it is only necessary to call the attention of H. M. government to this subject, in order to have the points in dispute referred to the Arbitration.

Your Lordship will pardon me for further trespassing on your kindness, while I call your attention to one or two points.

In this Question of the North Western Boundary of the Republic, which has for so many years being pending and which has   has caused incalculable loss to this Government; H. M. Government sets up no claim to the territory and has therefore nothing to lose by the delay of its settlement; but Liberia on the other hand is continually losing in consequence of this dispute. If you will take into consideration the large amount of money that would have accrued to thus Government had we been permitted to enforce our Revenue Laws, as well as the outlays that we have made from time to time first in efforts to suppress the Slave Trade, and then to enforce order and keep peace among the several tribes of that section, you can readily understand   understand our wish for a speedy settlement of the matter.

And furthermore there is a point of far graver character to which it is my duty to call your attention. I refer to the general spirit of insubordination and rebellion against the Authority of the Government which is springing up among the natives in almost every part of this Republic, growing out of this Question.

Extensive combinations are being formed among the tribes to throw off then allegiance to this Government under the impression that H. M. Government will afford the same encouragement and support that it is   is alleged to be giving to the tribes in the disputed Territories. This Government cannot for a moment entertain the idea that their expectations will be realized, since it has long since been a matter of record that Great Britain acknowledges the claims of Liberia to all territory between the "Sugaree" on the North West and the "San Pedro" on the South East. Nor can I suppose for a moment that Great Britain would allow on the part of her officials or citizens any actions that would tend to undermine our National Fabric. But I simply make this reference to show the effect that the attitude of H. M. Government in connection with the North Western Boundary Question is   is producing on the tribes within our domain.

Trusting that your Lordship will take an early opportunity of calling the attention of H. M. Government to the subject of this despatch, and assuring you of very high consideration,

I have the honor to be
Your Lordship's Obt. Svt.
