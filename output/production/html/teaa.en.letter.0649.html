Department of State Received May 19 1879
No 8

John A Parm to Department of State

Subject
Acknowledgement of the receipt of Dispatch No 10, with enclosures.

Abstract of contents. reply in reference to the statement made by Capt O'Brien of the Barque "Annie & Lily"

 
No. 8

U.S. Consulate at Sierra Leone April 17th 1879

To F. W. Seward
Assistant Secretary of State.

Sir:

I have the honor to acknowledge the receipt of your Dispatch No 10 with enclosures refering to the matter of Capt O'Brien of the American Brig "Annie & Lily" &c

I am sorry to say that I cannot confirm Capt O'Brien's statement throughout. He did not come to my Apothicary shop & asked for his papers, he, on his way to his consignees, between 7 & 8 O'clock A.M., in passing my shop, stoped and informed me that he would that day go to sea.

I knowing that he could not clear from the Custom house before 10 Oclock, and that his object was that I should get   get his papers ready. I asked him at what time would he be ready to recieve the two seamen on board? He replied he would rather see the men at my office that I might give them orders that they were to work, as Consul's men were disposed to [unclear, one word] & spoil the crew, & when told to do any thing they would refuse". I accordingly sent for the men. Capt O'Brien returned about 12 Oclock M, saw the men at my office, and "refused to take them, saying they were too sick to do anything on board of his ship." I admit they were a little sick, as they come to this Consulate under the effects of Fever, but were able each day to walk to my office the distance of a quarter of a mile, when they wish to do so: My experience of nine years in the treatment of the African or Coast fever (4 years as Chief Physician in the American Colonization Hospital or [unclear, one word] in Liberia, & 5 years private practice on the coast.) was sufficient for me to   have concluded that a few days at sea would set-them right, as I have frequently sent patients suffering with African fever, to sea, with good results.

Capt O'Brien after refusing to take them, asked for his papers, which I refused to give unless he consented to take them or give a written reason; this he also refused to do.

In his fit of exasperation he said that "the American laws governing American Merchant vessels were a swindle, & that it was a disgrace now a days to sail under that Flag; he would rather give one dollar to pay for each sailor's coffin than to be bothered with them." or words to this intent. This took place at the Consulate and not in my medical office.

Capt O'Brien left, and in a short time after, the correspondence I sent in my Dispatch No 3 passed between us. At 8 the next morning Capt O'Brien called at this Consulate and asked to be directed to the seamen's boarding house   house as he wished to take Dr Smith to see the men for the purpose of getting a certificate" I sent a boy to shew him their place, But he did not take Dr Smith as he should have done; but went to the house himself, & finding one of the men in bed with a chill, insisted on their going to the Doctor to be examined. they refused, saying they were too sick to do so. Capt O'Brien then informed me that the men had themselves admited that they were too sick to go to sea. I went to their room and aske them had they made such a statement & they said "Yes." I then told the Capt that he could have his papers, & they were delivered to him that afternoon, as he had to present his clearance from the Custom house first, which he did not do until pm. As regards the statement made by Mrs J Gray, I believe it to be true, & I am sorry she is too sick to enable me to obtain a written statement from her properly authenticated.   I do not believe Capt O'Brien wanted to take the men on board of his vessel, & after shewing so much annoyance at being compelled to do so; I think the men were afraid to go with him particularly "Henry Miller, who said he knew him to be a bad man at sea" Capt O'Brien could have obtained (through his consignees) a certificate from Dr Smith, worded just as he wished it, I think most every one in town will admit this.

As regards the Capts remarks about my not being a regular Physician; I am quite sue he can get all the information he desires on that subject by referring to the records of Harvard Medical School Boston Mass, or Dartmouth College N.H.

Had these men been affected with a contageous disease, I would not have asked the Capt to take them. But they were not thus affected, and in proof of this I inclose correspondence between the Colonial Surgeon, Dr Smith and   and myself on the subject. I beg to say in conclusion that the men were not too sick to go on board on the 21st Oct: for the distance from their boarding place to the warf is much shorter, than the distance to the Hospital, to which place they walked on the 23rd when they were admitted: the day Capt O'Brien sailed. I have no complaint to make of Capt O'Brien's private conduct; but as Capt of an American ship, in this particular case his actions were very inhumain: And as I have adopted the policy to inform the Department of all that occurs in this Consulate during my incumbency, I felt it my duty to state the facts in his case just as they occured

I have the honor to be
Sir
Your Obt Servant
John A Parm, U.S. Consul

Copy 3rd

U.S. Consulate S. Leone
April 9th 1879

Dear Sir,

Will you please inform me whether the two America Seamen Henry Miller, & Charles Thompson, who were admitted into your Hospital on the 23rd October 1878, were sufering from a contagious disease or coast fever; I beg you will please state what in your opinion was there malady & whether it was a severe attack.

I remain Respectfully
Yrs Jno. A Parm
U.S. Consul

Dr W. H. Hart
Colonial Surgeon
 
reply 3rd

Colonial Medl Department
Sierra Leone 14th April 1879

Sir

In reply to your letter of the 9th inst relative to Charles Thompson and Henry Miller who were under treatment in Hospital in October last, I have to inform you that they were suffering from Fever of the ordinary type.

I am, Sir
Your obedt Servant
Wm Hume Hart
Colonial Surgeon

Jno A. Parm Esq
U.S. Consul
