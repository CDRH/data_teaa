New York, October 31, 1898.

The Hon. John Hay,
Secretary of State,
Washington, D.C.,

My Dear Sir:-

I have the honor to submit to you an additional document to my communication delivered to you in person in your office Wednesday, October 19, bearing upon the present condition of the Republic of Liberia. This document is a copy of an article that has recently appeared in an English paper, and presents so pertinently additional matter in reference to the crisis in which the Republic of Liberia now finds itself that I deem it important that you should have it.

The Hon. Isaac Townsend Smith, Consul in this City for the Siam Government, is called to Washington on official business, to meet the Minister of that country, and I take the liberty of asking him to hand it to you in person.

Mr. Smith has given large attention to the history and present condition of Liberia, and will also be prepared to give additional information if so desired.

I have the honor to be, dear sir,
Yours sincerely,
JC Hartzell
Special Envoy to U.S. from the

Liberia

From our Liverpool Correspondent.

The sad murder of two French travelers in the territories of the Liberian Republic has provided the French Government with a powerful and singularly opportune weapon in the furtherance of its policy of gradual encroachment upon that Negro State. The German Emperor has recently shown to the world to what excellent use, in the shape of territorial aggrandizement, the assassination of a few missionaries may be put; and although Messrs. Bailly-Forfillere and Pauly were not missionaries, their deaths may be turned to an equally good account, for, both with the Chinese Empire and the Republic of Liberia, the possibilities of a successful resistance to preposterous demands for compensation are not perceptible.

I do not know whether the French Government has demanded compensation from the Republic. But the designs of France on Liberia have become so palpable during the last few years that it would be displaying a crass stupidity not to take very serious notice of this incident in light of the consequences to which it may give arise.

As most people know, Liberia is an independent Negro Republic which owes its inception to the philanthropic instincts to the people of the United States, following in the wake of our own somewhat similar efforts in Sierra Leone. A colony of freed American negroes was gradually formed at Monrovia and various other points on the Grain Coast, and in 1847 the colonists were endowed with the constitution of the United States. Ever since, the Liberian Republic has eked out a somewhat miserable existence, falling every year deeper into debt, and conclusively providing in a thousand ways that the American negro lacks the elementary principles of self-government, being deficient in the very essentialties of constructive administration. Certain foreign powers have endeavored to take advantage of the utter incapacity of the Liberians, and their motives in doing so are not far to seek. Not only are the territories owned by the Liberian Republic quite exceptionally rich in West African products, but it so happens that the Liberian coast is the home of the very best labor which West Africa can produce. The fame of the Krooboy is proverbial; without his magnificent muscles and his cheerful willingness to work, our colonies would suffer most severely; it would be almost impossible for merchants to carry on several important branches of their business, and British steamers would be deprived, to their disadvantage, of native firemen and stokers.

French and German Intrigues.

It must be perfectly obvious to all those who are in any way interested in this question that for years past two great Powers, France and Germany, have been silently, and without undue ostentation, working to get Liberia under their control. Whether there be collusion between them in this particular case is uncertain, but as Franco-German interests work harmoniously together in other parts of Africa, the hypothesis is by no means an unlikely one. However, that may be, France as is customary with her, is seeking to attain her ends through political action; Germany principally by commercial transactions on a large scale. The former hopes to establish, if, indeed, she has not already established a sort of right of pre-emption over the whole of the Liberian territory, filching here and there a slice when the opportunity presents itself. Germany is endeavoring to get the finances of this unfortunate State into her own hands, and, what perhaps is more dangerous still, trying to obtain a monopoly over Kroo labour throughout the territories of the Republic.

 

French intrigues in Liberia are not of yesterday. In 1879 a very definite rumour arose that France had offered protections to Liberia, which would probably have been accepted by the latter had not the American Government, ever mindful of its inexperienced offspring stepped in and intimated to the French Government that such a course would be viewed with disfavour in the States. In 1884 the rumours were renewed, and we find United States informing M. Roustan (French Minister at Washington) that "Liberia, although not a colony of the United States, began its independent career as an offshoot of that country, which bore to it a quasi-parental relationship, which authorized the United States to interpose its good offices in any contest between Liberia and a foreign State, and that a refusal to give the United States an opportunity to be heard for this purpose would make an unfavorable impression on the minds of the Government and the people of the United States." Again, in 1887, on the reported French aggression in Liberian territory, the United States were compelled to draw attention of the French Government to the fact that their relations with the Republic of Liberia had not changed, and that they still felt justified in employing their good offices on her behalf.

The Treaty of '92.

For five years the French desisted from their efforts. But on December 8, 1892, they succeeded in inducing the foolish legislators of Liberia to accept a treaty by which France, while making some so called concessions in the interior, which the French knew would be no more use to Liberia than to the Emperor Menelik, grabbed a large slice of Liberian territory between the San Pedro and Cavally Rivers, substituting the latter river for the former as the boundary between the French possessions on the Ivory Coast and Liberia. By this means a valuable portion of Liberia territory became French. And, to accentuate the irony of the situation France is now repudiating her "Concessions" in the interior to Liberia, on the plea that added geographical knowledge has materially altered the conclusions upon which the arrangement was based.

But the treaty did more than that. There can be no doubt as to the motives of the policy which dictated Article V. of the Franco-Liberian Agreement, nor that M. Hanotaux, in getting it accepted, showed his usual diplomatic skill. The translation of Article V. (the text is in French) reads as follows:-

In recognizing to the Republic of Liberia the limits which have now been fixed (after stripping Liberia of part of her dominions France graciously allows her to retain the rest) the Government of the French Republic declares that it only intends binding itself towards the Republic of Liberia, free and independent, and makes all its reserves in case this independence were to be impaired or in the event that the Republic of Liberia should alienate any part of the territories which are recognized to her by the present Convention.

The designs of France are at once disclosed in the above Article. If Liberia wishes at any time to dispose of a portion of her territory to another power, France tears up the Convention, and reserves herself the right to take any action which may seem fit to her. In other words, it is a distinct threat to Liberia never to cede any of her territory to anybody but France, under penalty of diplomatic remonstrance, followed by physical action, which would obviously entail more land-grabbing.

 

German Concession-Hunters.

We must now turn to German proceedings in the Republic. Her merchants and her traders have been worming themselves for years past into the very vitals of Liberia. Rubber concessions, coffee concessions, territorial concessions of all kinds obtained by the judicial expenditure of "palm oil"; loans contracted; a gradual but steady grip closing round the finances of the country; and now the boldest stroke of all, the purchase by a German firm of the monopoly of recruiting and shipping Krooboys all along the Liberian coast. Small wonder that this latest barefaced attempt to capture the free labour market of West Africa has evoked a spirited protest from the Liverpool Chamber of Commerce.

Now it is necessary that the Governments of Great Britain and of the United States should look this matter squarely in the face. I have shown, I think conclusively, that the future of Liberia is being gravely compromised by French and German action. "Her very independence is at stake, and the freedom of her subjects imperilled. Are the statesmen of the United States, who in 1879, in 1884, and again in 1887, forbade outside interference with Liberian affairs — are they prepared in 1898 to acquiesce in Liberia becoming an extension of the French Ivory Coast? As for Great Britain her duty is clear. She should refuse peremptorily and at once to countenance any sort or kind of arrangement which places restriction on the free recruiting of Krooboys on the Liberia Coast. That done, London and Washington must arrive at an understanding which shall save Liberia both from the incompetence of her own legislators, and from becoming a prey to the cupidity of the Continental Powers.
