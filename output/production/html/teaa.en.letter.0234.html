Department of State Received Sep 12 1887
No 13

United States Consulate
Zanzibar, August 1, 1887.

To the Hon.
Third Assit. Secretary of State.
Washington D.C.

Sir:

I have the honor to inform the Department of some recent changes in the Consular Service of the different European Nations represented at Zanzibar.

Dr. Arendt late Imperial German Consul General at Zanzibar has returned to Britain and has been relieved by Dr. Michahelles. It is presumed that Dr. Arendt has been recalled by his Government.

It is also understood that Mr. Holmwood, lately H. B. M.   H. B. M. Acting Consul General of Zanzibar, who has been relieved by Maj. MacDonald, has been recalled at the instance of Prince Bismark.

In this connection the resignation of Sir John Kirk H. B. M. Political Agent and Consul General is significant: since it would appear to be in the direct line of British non-interference with the project of the German Government for the colonization of the East Coast of Africa.

Diplomatic relations having been resumed between the Portuguese Government and His Majesty the Sultan. The Portuguese   Portuguese flag was again hoisted at Zanzibar on the 15th ultimo. Le Vicomte de Castilho being Consul Gl. and Chargé d'Affaires.

M. Hermanigilda the special Envoy of the King of Portugal is at present in Zanzibar accompanied by two ships of war. His mission is the delimitation of the Northern frontier of the Portuguese possessions in East Africa.

I have the honor to be,
Sir,
Your Obedient Servant, Seth A. Pratt.
U.S. Consul.

 

This market has for some time been over-supplied with quantities of English and Indian goods.

American Imports.

The imports from the United States may be said to consist nominally of Refined Petroleum, Unbleached Cotton and Soap. These being the staples handled by the American houses engaged in this trade. The former of these by its phenomenal cheapness has completely taken the place of the vegetable oils, which in former times were used exclusively for illuminating purposes.

 

Can[?] petroleum is often sold in this market as low as one dollar and a quarter. United States Currency. which fact would not command it to the serious consideration of exporters in America. The yearly consumption of East Africa -- Zanzibar being the distributing point -- is upwards of 75,000 cases.

American cottons have for many years been the standards in this market but their use has been much curtailed within the past ten years by the introduction of the cottons manufactured in India.   Standard thirty-six inch sheetings cannot be sold here at better than five rupees per piece of thirty yards.

Brine[?] Soap commands a ready sale but at unremunerative prices.

Besides the foregoing a limited business is done in Bleached Cotton, Biscuits, Lumber. Bar[?] Shacks[?], Hoop[?] Iron, Nails, Resin, and Turpentine.

As is usually the case, the imports from the United States for the past year are very small in comparison to the exports for the same period.
