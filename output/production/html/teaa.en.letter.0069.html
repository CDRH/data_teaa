Sir;

In my despatch No 13, I had the honor of informing the Department that an American Seaman calling himself Edward Baxter, presented himself to me, on the 24th of September, requesting protection as a destitute seaman. He represented to me that he departed from the American Barque "Governor Carver" Captain[?] Charles Mason of Westport, R.I. at Mossamedes (Little Fish Bay) for the reason that said Barque had taken in at that place slaves and that he assisted taking on board the first seventy five and on returning for more, ran away and after the Barque had proceeded to sea, he came down to the town and made arrangements with Captain [?] of the British Mail Steamer "Lincolnshire" and came to this place and presented himself to me, as stated. I listened to his Hon William H Seward
Secretary of State
Washington, D.C. his story with apprehension and doubt, but not wishing to show, that I entertained such a feeling, I treated the subject, for a time with seeming indifference, and set Baxter to work, about my house with some light jobs. My anxiety to sift out the truth of this case increased from day to day and I questioned and cross-questioned him frequently on the subject with a view to compare one statement with another and I was surprised to find them always correspond. On one of these days, I met at the house of a friend, Watson Vredenburg Esquire, H B M's Commissioner for the Slave trade. I, Edward Baxter, a native of Liverpool, twenty-five years of age, do solemnly declare, and state as follows—

Towards the end of July, I shipped at St Helena, on board the Barque "Governor Carver" belonging to Westport, United States of America, of which vessel, Charles Mason, a native of Nantucket, is master. The first port at which the ship touched, after leaving St Helena, was Little Fish Bay, the arrival there on a Friday afternoon about three weeks ago. At about twelve Oclock that night, four boats, in one of which I was employed, were sent ashore to the North Point of the Bay, and there embarked seventy five Blacks, whom they conveyed on board. The boats were then sent a second time to the same place, and as I was afraid of what they were doing, I ran away in the dark, as I shipped to go on on a whaling voyage, and not to run slaves; I cannot say what number of Blacks, they took the second time. The ship was fitted out like a whaler, but when we were three days out from St Helena, I saw Sperm whales, all round the ship and said to the second Mate "how is it we are not lowering after those whales." "Oh", said he, "we are not after these kind of whales." I then asked him what kind of whales we were after; he answered "We are after two or three hundred Black birds if we can catch them." Then the fourth day they lifted out the bodies. The Americans call them "Tripods", and there was a kind of Bulk head. They knocked the bulkhead away and I saw an iron rod, nailed down to the deck, with footcuffs, a sort of handcuffs worn on the feet. Then the Captain said Boatswain do you know what these things are for? "this is where we shall put the first seventy   seventy niggers we get." and it was there they put the seventy-five they brought on board that Friday night. After leaving the boat, I remained all night in the mountains, the Barque went away the following morning, steering towards the north. I then went down into the town, and saw the Captain of the steamer "Lincolnshire" whom I asked to give me a passage to Loanda; He told me to help to get the Bullocks on board, and then to go on board myself; I did so, and arrived at this port on the 24th of September.

(Signed) Edward Baxter

I certify being the clerk in court during the proceedings in investigating the truth of an accusation made by the American Seaman Edward Baxter before the judicial authorities of this place, of a shipment of slaves in the Bay of Mossamedes, in which record of page 45, an inquiry is seen as follows. Terms of Inquiry. In the year of our Lord 1865, on the 4th day of November, in the City of Loanda and in the Criminal Court of the district, present Dr. Carlos Pacheco de Bettencourt, Judge of the Criminal Court with exercise in both sections of this tribunal together with me Feliciano Gonçales de Espirito Cinsha[?] his clerk, and the Prisoner Edward Baxter for the purpose of being re-examined. This was performed in the following manner. In In the presence of the witnesses Luis Pedro Ferreira and José Antonio de Santos, Public Officers of the Court who gave oath before the Judge to verify the [illegible], in writing the questions and answers are through the interpreter Francisco da Silva Marquis Porto[?], a Merchant of this place whose oath was taken by the Judge to faithfully translate and transmit to the prisoner, the questions made by the Judge and to me the answers given by him, which he promised to fulfill. Thus through the said interpreter, the Judge inquired of the prisoner, his name, place of birth, age, name of father, condition in life, profession, last situation, if he had ever before been in prison, and if he enjoys the liberty of his occupation; to which, he replied that his name was Edward Baxter, Native of Liverpool, age 25 years, a son of John and Mary Baxter, married,   married, a seaman, and that he enjoys the liberty of his profession, that his last residence was at Mossamedes from where he came to this city, having remained there only a short time, and that he had never been in prison before. Being further questioned by the judge as to his nationality he answered that he was an American Citizen, having been naturalized as such about nine years ago; He, the said prisoner then addressing the interpreter said that the accusation made by him both to the American and British Consuls and to the Government of this Province, of a shipment of slaves made in the Bay of Mossamedes on board the American Barque "Governor Carver" was false and that he declared it to be such to the Judge; whereupon being asked by the Judge for what purpose he had made such a statement. He replied, that having deserted the said Barque, and   and fearing future punishment, he had invented that means, not only to avoid it but also in the hopes of obtaining a passage gratis to England and it was with those deeds that, on his arrival here, he applied to his Consul Mr Silva.

All the above statements having to be made known, the Judge caused this record to be drawn out and after being read and explained to the prisoner, by the interpreter was signed by both, together with the witnesses and myself, the clerk signing for the prisoner, he not knowing how to read or write. and I, Feliciano Gonçales de Espirito Santo Cinsha[?], Bettencourt, Francisco da Silva Marquis Porto[?], Luis Pedro Ferreira, José Antonio de Santos.

No more was contained in said interrogation, of which I have caused a true and faithful certified copy to be taken from the the original documents, in my possession, which has been compared and signed by me and filed according to law. Loanda November 8th 1865. I, Feliciano Gonçales de Esperito Santo e Cinsha, Clerk who have written and signed it, and confirmed by us, José Solomão de Lewis Perveira Batalha, and Feliciano Gonçales de Esperito Santo e Cinsha[?]

A true Copy

(Signed) Antonio de Mello Varajãs

United States Commercial Agency.
St Paul de Loanda Novr 20 1865.

Sir;

I have the honor to request of you the kindness to deliver the enclosed letter to Captain Charles Mason of the American Barque "Governor Carver", when his vessel enters your port and who, I understand, is cruizing off your Coast.

The recent unfortunate circumstance connected with the false accusation made by Edward Baxter against the [?] inhabitants of your quiet village and the reputation of Barque "Governor Carver", was received here with doubt by everyone and his subsequent confession was no more than was generally anticipated.

It To The
Illustrious Senhor
Fernando da Costa Leal
Governor General of Mossamedes It must have been extremely unpleasant to the inhabitants of Mossamedes and particularly to yourself to have your character defamed by the base accusation of having the slave trade revived at your very door more especially at a time when it had become a fixed fact that for many years no instance of this nefarious nature has been recorded but on the contrary, your district has flourished by increased Commercial and agricultural prosperity, to the great admiration of all interested in the welfare of Angola.

I have already exchanged congratulations with the Honle Conselho de Governo for the happy termination of this disagreeable case; I also expressed to them my thanks for this cordial cooperation and promptness of action, which had the final results to force from Edward Baxter his voluntary confession; I am not unmindful that that a portion of this credit belongs to you and you will please accept the acknowledgement of my warmest sentiments.

I have written my government a minute description of the whole case and I am confident they will appreciate the able manner in which the authorities of this province defended the innocence of the American Barque "Governor Carver".

I have the Honor
To be
Your most obedt Servant.
(Sg) Augustus Archer Silva
Commercial Agent

United States Commercial Agency
St Paul de Loanda Novr 20th 1865.

Sir;

On the 24th of September, Edward Baxter, styling himself an American Seaman, presented himself to this Commercial Agency for protection, and on oath, before witnesses, declared that, he ran away from your vessel at Little Fish Bay, for the reason that you had shipped, at that place, a cargo of slaves.

This report, very naturally created much excitement and anxiety with the Local Portuguese Authorities and myself, who proceeded, at once to investigate the truth. Baxter was examined by the Authorities of this place and by the British Consul also, and at my request was sent to Little Fish Bay on board of the Portuguese Corvette "Infante D. João," which Captain Charles Mason
of the
American Barque "Governor Carver" which vessel left here October 12th and returned on the 30th of the same month, on which day, I received a communication from the Commander, informing me that Baxter failed to prove, on the alleged [?], the accusation he had made against your vessel and the inhabitants of that industrious village. Baxter was then, by order of the Portuguese Government, imprisoned. A few days after he was brought before the Judicial Tribunal of this City and indicted with the charge of having made false accusation and ordered, again, to prison, to await his trial. Baxter, before the court, became alarmed and to a Portuguese gentleman he confessed that everything he had said was false and a pure invention of his own. He was brought again before the Judge and made a formal confession, of which I have the honor to enclose a copy. Baxter is still in Prison, waiting his trial. The acting Governor General told me, a few days ago, that the   the penalty of his crime, by the criminal laws of Portugal was very light and that he would only be reprimanded and set at Liberty. As soon as this takes place, I shall order Baxter back to Prison and there confine him four months, on the charge of having deserted your vessel and I hope, in the meantime, you will come to Loanda to claim him. Your presence here will also do much good. I have been requested by the Governor General of this Province to invite you to visit this Port.

I have the honor
To be
Your most obedt Servt.
(Sg) Augustus Archer Silva
Commercial Agent.
