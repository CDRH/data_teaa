Department of State Received Jan 29 1881

No. 41 Consulate Sierra Leone Dec 24th 1880.

Judson A. Lewis To Dept State

Captain Roberts Bark Hazard refuses to take two destitute Seamen and leaves his Papers in Consulate

 

No 41 U.S. Consulate at
Sierra Leone Dec. 24th 1880.

Hon John Hay
Ass't Secty State
Washington D.C.

Sir

I have to inform you that Captain D. A. Roberts of the American Bark Hazard cleared from this Port yesterday for Boston Mass. U.S.A. consigned to Mansfield and Roberts of Boston laden with Dry Hides.

Captain Roberts refused to take Two destitute American Seamen David Freethey and Walter Duval on board for transportation to the United States under the law and in accordance with Consular Regulations Par.   123. and 124. I retained his Papers. and he proceeded to sea leaving in this Consulate his Register Shipping Articles and Crew List, together with Bill of Health, Hide Certificate and Certificate given to Masters when required to take to the United States destitute American Seamen - all of which I had prepared for him.

One of these men Walter Duval lately arrived from Grand Bassa although fit to go to sea would not be fit for any labor as you will see by the enclosed Certificate of the Colonial Surgeon.

The Surgeon and myself thought it would be far better for him to go to sea than to remain here for Six weeks waiting for the arrival and departure of another   American vessel - and reasoning if one of the ships Sailors was in the same condition as the said Walter Duval the Captain would most certainly have taken him: consequently I considered it quite proper to send him at this time, and read the Captain the Surgeon Certificate and furnished him with a copy of same.

Captain Roberts is a man who flies off at a tangent on the slightest provocation either imaginary or real and cannot be reasoned with: and I would most respectfully recommend and urge that the most stringent measures which the law provides be taken in this case, as there was no good reason why the men should not have gone.

 

The Captain being in his usual noisy and explosive state abused and tried to brow beat the Sailors and the Consul.

I put the men together with their effects on board the vessel and he sent them ashore again in the ships boat as you will see by the deposition of the two sailors enclosed.

In the absence of any instruction or information in the Regulation touching such this a case I send all the Papers enclosed to the Department viz Register, Shipping Articles, Crew List, Hide Certificate, and Bill of Health.

I also enclose Consular Certificate marked A.

I am Sir
Very Respectfully
Your Obt Servt Judson A Lewis
U.S. Consul.

I hereby certify that I have this day examined the seaman Walter Duval.

He is in a delicate state of health and unfit for any work at present. If it can be arranged that he could travel to America as passenger merely until he regains his strength I would recommend that he be sent by the first vessel leaving this port for America, but he is not fit for any work on board and will require two or three weeks and as good food as can be provided before he will be fit.

At present he has no signs of any organic disease but is only suffering from debility the result of Coast Fever

Signed W Hume Hart
A. B. M. B[?] & M Ch
Trinity Col Dublin
L M Roy Hosp. Dublin
& Colonial Surgeon Sierra Leone

Dec 22. 1880.

 

U.S. Consulate at
Sierra Leone Dec. 23, 1880

I Judson A. Lewis Consul for the United States of America at Sierra Leone do hereby certify that the foregoing Certificate is a true copy of Certificate given me by Dr W Hunt Hart Colonial Surgeon of this Colony

Judson A. Lewis
U.S. Consul

 

We Daniel Freethey and Walter Duval destitute American Seamen do take oath to the following statement

One Thursday Dec. 23rd 1880. we were sent aboard the American Bark Hazard Roberts Master lying in the Harbor at Sierra Leone together with all our affects by the U.S. Consul Judson A. Lewis.

The Captain and the first Mate were on shore when we came on board - in about an hour - the Captain and Mate came on board - The Captain swore he would not have us aboard of the vessel & that we must go ashore - and addressing Walter Duval said even if I had you aboard when I got out to sea where you have no Consul   to protect you - I'll fix you.

He made us go in the ships boat & put all our effects into said boat & sent us on shore - saying he'd be d-d- if he have us aboard & when we got ashore we could go and report to the Consul & he didn't care a G-d- d-d for the Consul any more than he did for us-

The last thing he said as we were leaving addressing Daniel Freethey was - if you can steal aboard I'll take you-

Daniel Freethey
Walter Duval

U.S. Consulate Sierra Leone Dec. 24th 1880.

Sworn to before me at my office this day Judson A. Lewis
U.S. Consul

 

U.S. Consulate at
Sierra Leone,
Dec. 24th 1880.

To whom it may concern.

I, Judson A. Lewis U.S. Consul at Sierra Leone do hereby certify that I requested and ordered Capt. D. A. Roberts Master American Bark Hazard of Boston to receive on board his vessel and to transport to Boston two destitute American Seamen named Daniel Freethey and Walter Duval and that the said Captain D. A. Roberts refused so to do and that when I put the men on board his vessel he compelled them to go into his   boat and sent them on shore leaving them in Sierra Leone and went to sea contrary to my orders and protestations, and without his Register, shipping Articles, Crew List, Hide Certificate, and Consular Bill of Health.

Given under my hand and the seal of this Consulate on the day and year above written.

Judson A. Lewis
U.S. Consul.

(Seal.)
