No 31

US Consulate, Goree-Dakar
July 13th 1885

To the Honorable
James D Porter
Assistant Secretary of State
Washington

Sir

Before discussing the subject of the appointment of a successor for Mr Armand, I desire respectfully to call the attention of the Department with a view to some explanation of the enclosed copies of letters received at this Consulate form the Legation at Paris. It will be seen thereby that my request for a leave of absence to visit America preferred in my Despatch No. 21 and dated February 18th met with an unqualified refusal and until I know the grounds of that refusal I am at a loss in preferring another request which in the not distant future may be absolutely necessary for me. If it was thought that I asked for a visit too protracted, I have only to urge Universal Custom, the extreme precautions taken by naval vessels while navigating [?] in the good season on this coast, and above all the melancholy fate of my colleague Mr Armand, whose death was caused solely by blood poisoning from malaria. Though habituated to extreme Southern Latitudes for the last twenty-five years I am suffering some from the same cause. I have not been six months away from Africa in eight years, and with this fact in view it can   hardly be esteemed unreasonable by the Department that I should be held too closely by a Consulate which scarcely pays for the writing it entails.

Regarding a Successor for Mr Armand I know of no person in Goree who would exactly answer. The nearest approach to it would be Charles de Montfort Esq the present Mayor of Goree who understands the English language sufficiently will and is faultless as to business qualifications and Character but holding many colonial offices he is compelled to be much of the time absent. The only other suitable person that I know of is my son George Strickland such is now on a visit home to bring out my family and I expect him here early in September. It is not perhaps delicate for a father to recommend his own son, and I shall not do so only to state facts which under the circumstances may be acted on or not as the Department shall think proper. He is 21 years of age, was born in New London, Connecticut, has been liberally educated both classically & commercially, writes an elegant hand and can both speak and write the French language with facility. He was with me here all of last season and has altogether lived as many as three years in Africa. He has also been in France & England and several voyages   with me at sea, mostly however when he was very young. As to political opinions neither of us have ever expressed any by means of a rate; not from apathy perhaps but principally from lack of occasion. My father however was a life-long Democrat, and I have always been conscious of some bias that way: not enough however to make me follow that or any party in measures which our being fairly examined my judgment or conscience would condemn.

My position with regard to the Consulate is this: as it came without seeking on my part so, I am prepared to part with it as lightly if it shall so seem expedient for the Government. If however it is still intrusted to me I shall continue to render the best service which my ability with some due preparation to the means will allow. For me to make occasional business visits about the Coast is imperative, and it is also necessary that I should sometimes go home. It is easy however to arrange so that the Consulate will not be neglected, and with an apology for these extended remarks

I am Sir with respect
Very truly Yours
Peter Strickland U.S. Consul

Inclosure

Copies of Letters concerning "Leave of absence" forwarded from the Legation at Paris
