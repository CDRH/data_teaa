I have the honor to forward herewith Duplicate copies of "Commercial Report for 1879". Ending Dec 31st

I regret that I could not get information at the proper time to enable me to make said Report as per Par. 380 of the Consular Regulations.

This Report is incomplete in many respects but I have done the best I could under the circumstances.

Your attention is specially called to the fact of Imports and Exports from and to the United States being respectively 13 per cent and 8 per cent of the total Imports and Exports of this Colony.

It seems to me that the West Coast of Africa offers at this time an inviting field for American Merchants and Manufactures to introduce their wares. In my Depatch No 20 dated March 15th to the Department I [?] a few[?] articles which have been introduced in this market during the past year with good success.

Comprising more novelties[?] probably than have been brought to to this market on trial, during the past five years.

I have the honor to remain
Your Obedient Servant

Agriculture.

Is in a very primitive state. the cultivation of the soil is all done by hand with stout hoes as no plough, nor modern implements of any kind such as used in the states have ever been even seen by the natives of this Colony.

Almost all attention is given to trading by men, women, and children and only Ginger, Benniseed, Ground nuts, Palm Kernals and Cola Nuts are raised for Exportation and Cassala, Rice, Sweet Potatoes and Yams are the principle vegetables raised for [?] consumption.

The following fruits grow spontaneously and in great abundance, Bannanas Mangos, Plantains, Coco nuts, Pineapples, Pracada[?] Pears, Oranges, Limes &c. which are sold about the streets and markets very cheaply.

There are a few Gentlemen of of means who are cultivating some land, for their own satisfaction but without any great profit thus far. It is generally thought by the intelligent portion of the community, and much is published in the local papers that more attention should be paid to agricultural pursuits and less to trade.

Of late several public meetings have been held, committees appointed, reports submitted and subscriptions called for to form a company for the purpose of establishing a sugar mill and to encourage the cultivation of the sugar cane.

This scheme if vigorously pushed by a couple of Europeans and a few intelligent natives could be consumated at this time, but without this energetic and persistent push which is so characteristic of the American American or what is sometimes termed Yankee - it will end like all other things of a similar nature which have been brought forward for introduction in this Colony - failure.

The Committee appointed to investigate the feasibility of the sugar cane business, say in their Report as follows.

Your committee are unanimous in the opinion that the soil in and around the Colony of Sierra Leone with anything like proper attention and cultivation will produce the sugar cane successfully.

x x x x

The question of Labor has been one of considerable perplexity[?] to your Committee, but after considerable discussion and deliberation it was pretty generally thought that suitable labor could could without doubt be obtained and instructed in the work, by patience and proper payment of the same.

x x x x

By judicious and proper management of this said company, its finances and interests, we believe the absolute risk to the shareholder, will be reduced to the very minimum of any enterprise which is organized at the present day for legitimate business.

x x x x

The report closes as follows

You will not forget that great streams from little fountains flow, and that every large and valuable enterprise which is successful to day was once an untried experiment.

 

Education

Receives considerable attention both from local authorities and missionary societies in England.

The Fourah Bay College connected with and under the supervission of the university of Durham in England. The Principal and one professor are white men, balance of Teachers black. The building is a large three story brick and stone structure, on a high point of land overlooking the Sierra Leone River about one mile from town.

Next in order is the Grammar School, located in "Regent Square" about half a mile from the center of the town, a boarding school for Boys. next in rank is the Wesleyan High School also a boarding school for Boys next the Model School for Boys and Girls.

 

Most of the Churches have day schools held either in their chapels or in buildings separate from, but under their supervission.

The Catholic Church maintains two schools, one for boys and one for girls. All of the Catholic Clergy and Sisters of Charity are white persons. There are quite a number of private schools for girls and are attended by the children of families, who are able to pay their tuition and prefer to do so rather than to send to the public schools.

There are also two Institutions for young ladies. One the "Annie Walsh Memorial School" which is under the supervission and support of the Church Missionary Society of England. it is a boarding school and the buildings are quite elaborate and and very comfortable. Enclosed in grounds of about four acres: presided over by a Preceptress and an Assistant both white ladies, balance of teachers native.

The other Institution has just been opened and is under the supervission of the Wesleyan Society.

There is an examination held in all the public schools once a year and the pupils are advanced from the Grammar school to College &c.

Of Churches there are Seventeen divided as follows: Eight Methodists, Six Protestant Episcopal, Two Baptist and one Catholic Church, besides several Chapels not included in the above number, all of these churches hold regular services on Sunday and various meetings throughout the week.

 

Harbor &c

The Habor with perhaps one exception (that of St Paul De Loando) is the best on the West Coast it is most easily entered evey by strangers and is really admirable place for vessels to anchor only a few hundred yards from the "Queens Wharf", which is solidly built of stone and on which several large cranes are arranged for the hoisting of all kinds of merchandise from the Lighters, which are [?] by two natives and are invariably used in unloading all steamers and sailing vessels.

The landing of all Merchandise both dutiable and free is under the immediate charge of the Landing Surveyor Mr A. B. Hanson who is also Deputy Harbor Master and Superintendent of Quarantine[?] and Lighthouse. These Departments so far as the head is concerned are managed with remarkable promptness and intelligence, whose object seems to be to assist trade rather than impede it.

The Harbor and Port charges are as follows viz:

Pilotage inward 5/- per foot draft - outward half the above for moving a vessel in the harbor which must be done by a Pilot £1 - Tonnage dues 1/- per ton. Light dues 3d Harbor dues £3. if vessel is above 200 tons register. All Merchandise [?] Lumber and building material pays a wharfage tax of 3 pence per cubic foot - except very heavy articles which are taxed 10/- per ton by weighing.

The following articles[?] are subject to pay customs duties as as Imports viz:

Tobacco (natural leaf and manufactured[?]) Spirits of all Kinds, Powder, and Guns.

Of the Produce shown in the tabulated statement herewith enclosed and marked A. only the Hides and about 1000,000 pounds of Ginger and less than 50,000 lbs of Rubber are sent to the United States the balance goes to Europe. Nearly all the ground nuts a portion of the Palm Kernals and Benni Seed go to France the balance to Liverpool - same the Cola Nuts which find a ready market at about £60- per per ton at the Gambia.

The number of American vessels which have entered and cleared at this Port during the year ending Dec. 31st 1879. was 12 with an aggregate Tonnage of 3731 tons Register not including the United States Flagship Toconderoga.

Imports and Exports.

The total amount of Imports to this Colony during the year ending Dec. 31st 1879 was $1,825,858.10

Exports $1,397,186.66

Of this amount the Imports from the United States for the same period were $231,526,78 and exports to the United States $111,354.98

 

The Imports to the United States being about 13% and the Exports about 8% of the total Imports and Exports.

in view of the large number of American products and manufactured articles so admirably adapted to this market, this is hardly a satisfactory exhibit of American intelligence and enterprise.

This condition of trade is susciptible of great improvement in favor of the United States. I feel compeled to make one charge against the Manufacturer and merchant in the states - they seem unwilling to sell a Single article desired sample[?] & for trial (in working up trade) at the same rate they would in selling a quantity, forgetting the important fact that that with a little concession on their part, the result might prove greatly to their advantage

I would further suggest to those parties having goods which seem adapted to this market but which have not been introduced that when they have an opportunity to consign them to a responsible person they do so, sharring part of the risk and await the result.

Ice.

We have no ice either imported or manufactured - occasionally we can get a small piece from the Mail Steamers - on their way out from England - but on their return passage they have more even for their own use. On first coming to this Colony the writer[?] gave this subject considerable study and believed it would pay to to establish an Ice House (in connection with other business) and import the ice from the United States. It might not pay the first year but afterward I have no doubt it would be profitable, as the people once become accustomed to its use they would not readily relinquish it. The price would no doubt range from 3 to 6 cents per lb according to quantity consumed.

In the same vessel with the ice - could be shipped American Lard, Butter, Cheese, vegetables and many things which are not almost excluded from this market on account of length of passage and climatic causes.

 

Manufactures.

The manufacturing business of this Colony is so very limited as to be hardly worth naming in this report.:

There is no power mill of any kind in the Colony. there are a large number of Cabinet Makers, Tailors, Watchmakers and Jewelers, Shoemakers and Blacksmiths who do the work required for home use only.

General Observations.

The healthfulness of the Colony has greatly improved during the past 3 or 4 years. This may be owing to improved drainage and better sanitary measures - also to the fact of the remarkably good water which is now universally used by all, being brought in [illegible]

 

Population

The European population is very small, there seldom being any time when one hundred white people can be counted. There has been no Census taken for the past 6 years, but those best able to judge estimate the population at about 40,000.

Climate.

Being in 8° north latitude is of course tropical, but there is no extreme of heat. during the past year the thermometer has not indicated above 91° and there is usually a fine sea breeze at least two thirds of the time. during the rainy or cool season the Thermometer does not indicate lower than 75°.

From the middle of May til the the middle of October is the hot[?] season and by reference to the tabulated statement herewith enclosed and marked "B" it will be observed that the Amount of Rainfall has steadily and largely increased, during the past three years, being for the year 1879. a total of 168 inches as against 131 inches in 1877 and 163 inches in 1878.

Garrison and Fortifications

There are Soldiers Barracks, Officers Quarters, Hospital, Commissariat &c. for the accomodation of one Regiment of troops and the 1st and 2nd West India Regiments alternate. The 2nd Regt. came here the first part of 1877 and will leave early in 1880. being releived by the 1st Regt. Alternating between the West Indies and and Sierra Leone.

This is an important Military and Naval Point as the Depot of supplies is here and it being the principle coaling station on the West Coast for all English Men of War.

Steam Ship Lines.

There are two regular lines of Steamers (carrying the mails) from England going South as far as Old Calabar and giving one Steamer each way weekly. Occasionally an opposition line of trading Steamers come forward but up[ to this time, with no great degree of regularity or persistent endurance, it has the affect however of reducing the price of freights for the time being as the old companies fight the new, with energy and regardless of of present profits.

Quite a number of Steamers beside sailing vessels belonging to private parties come out from France and England on trading voyages.

By reference to tibulated statement herewith enclosed and marked "G" the aggregate tonnage of vessels entering this Port during the years 1877, 1878 and 1879 is indicated but nationality not given but mostly English and French

Finance and Currency.

There is one Bank in operation in Sierra Leone, the "Oxford Bank" owned and managed by private individuals they cash drafts draw checks, and loan money at 18% interest, though many times charging as high as 1/- per £ per month, for small sums (60%) There are some parties in town who do a thriving business in a small way loaning money in small sums and on short time at as high a rate of interest as 2/- per £ per month (120%)

The currency used throughout the Colony is all English & French coin together with Mexican Dollars, the latter during the past year have been imported largely by private persons, as they can be purchased in London for about 3/9 (90¢) and pass readily here for 4/2 (100¢) American money is not current here and only the Gold coin passes & that at a discount.

Judson A. Lewis
U.S. Consul
