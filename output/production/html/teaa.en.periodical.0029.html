The Ethnology of Madagascar

By Rev. B. F. Wheeler, A. M., D. D.

Read Before the New York Ministers Meeting

Madagascar, as all are aware, lies off the south-east coast of the continent of Africa. To be a little more definite or explicit, we may say that by exact measurement it lies between 12° and 25° south latitude, and 42° and 51° east longitude.

With the exception of Borneo, Madagascar is the largest island in the world. It is about 970 miles long and between 300 and 400 miles wide. To get a better idea of its size, it contains an area of 200,000 square miles, which would make it as large as England, Ireland, Scotland, Wales, and the States of New York, New Hampshire, Massachusettes, Connecticut and Rhode Island combined.

The land is low along the coast, and this low, marshy land extends, inwardly for 30 or 40 miles. In the interior there is a broad table land. A range of mountains rises from this table land and extends North and South nearly the entire length of the island. It does not appear, however, as far as we have been able to find out, that the structure of these Ankasatia mountains is such as to make it possible for Madagascar even to be proven to be a continent, as Australia has been shown to be. Some of the mountain peaks rise 8000 and 12000 feet above the level of the sea.

There are abundant beautiful lakes and rivers throughout the island, but unfortunately none of the rivers are navigable. Undergrowth and shrubbery all over the island make traveling exceedingly difficult. There are no roads and it is the policy of the government never to have any constructed. This shrubbery and undergrowth furnish a good natural defense they claim, and make it impossible for an invading army ever to reach Tananarivo the capital of the island. Some of the largest forests in the world abound in this island.

 

Tropical fruits of all kinds are found in abundance there; some of these, however, are not indigenous, but have been introduced by travellers or naturalists. The orange, for example, now grows in abundance, but it has been introduced by travelers.

Like most tropical countries there are only two seasons in Madagascar, the wet and the dry,—the former lasting from April to November and the latter from December to March. Two crops may be raised during the year, the one gathered in June, the other in December. As might be expected, rice, sweet-potatoes, coffee and sugar cane are the principal products, and of these great quantities are raised annually. The island is full of wild cattle. In fact Mauritius and all the neighboring islands are supplied with beef from Madagascar. But let this suffice for a brief description of the island is general and let us now consider more particularly the more important subject, namely:—

The People of Madagascar.—The People of Madagascar have received special attention at the hands of ethnologists and naturalists for a great many years. The great question which puzzled these learned men most was to prove the race identity of these people. Had they gone at the matter in the right way, seeking truth and being governed by the light of that truth, I suspect they would have had a comparatively easy time in settling the question. But because they have gone at it with convictions or prejudices of their own, by which they were determined to stand, they have had a rather hard time in settling the race identity of the people of this large island.

When the island was first made known to Europe by Marco Polo in the 13th century, and as late as the latter part of the 17th and beginning of the 18th century, about 200 years ago, when Robert Drury wrote of the inhabitants as such savage cannibals that he was afraid that they would leap upon him and eat him alive, it was pretty generally conceded that they were Africans. In fact Madagascar was only thought of as an African island and its inhabitants Africans. But during the present century, and especially during the last forty years, when the people have made such marvellous strides towards civilization, the Caucasian historian, ever true to his prejudice, has striven to   prove that the inhabitants of Madagascar are of Malayan origin, at least, that they were not of African descent. And it is astonishing to the calm, unbiased thinker, to notice some of the strange and far-fetched arguments some of these ethnologists and historians have employed to substantiate their theories.

We lay down the broad statement that the aborigines of Madagascar are Africans, or of African descent. We believe that a careful study of the subject, on the part of any who have not a bias or penchant toward race prejudice, will enable them to reach the same conclusion. Unfortunately so far, the only ethnologists or historians who have written on this subject have been Caucasians. And every body knows that the Caucasian race, as a whole, with all their prejudice against the Negro, is incapable of treating the Negro, or any Negro descendants fairly, in anything they may say or write about the Negro. To prove this statement, we have but to look at the histories of these United States, the war records of the Nation, the flippant and after reflections, daily and weekly reports of Negro assemblies, the unceremonious way with which crimes perpetrated upon Negroes are dismissed by the white press. It will be seen that when praise ought to be rendered by the Negro, the Caucasian historian or press is silent, when mercy ought too be shown him, no voice through the press is heard, When justice ought to be demanded, no justice is asked for by the press, Thank God here and there a noble exception to this rule is found. And we take pleasure in mentioning foremost among the noble exceptions, —The New York Independent.

There are in round numbers 5,000,000 souls inhabiting this island. They are short of stature, have beautiful white teeth, black eyes and range in complexion from a dark olive color to jet black. The hair is black in color, but in texture varies from the purest wool, or the hardest kink, to almost straight with a slight wave. Many of them have excellent foreheads and intelligent countenances. The oft spoken of retreating forehead as a distinguishing Negro characteristic will not be found in many cases here. For long ago has that description of a representative Negro type been abandoned by all except the most prejudiced   of historians. Especially has it been abandoned since Stanley found such a high type of Negroes in certain portions of the continent of Africa. The people of Madagascar, especially the females, are said to be some of the most beautiful people on the face of the earth. They call themselves Malagasy. Just how the name Madagascar originated is not easy to tell. But as far back as the 13th century, Marco Polo, in reporting the discovery of the island in Europe said that it was called Magaster.

Like all heathens, in the early part of their history before they became christianized, they worshipped all kinds of idols, and were superstitious. They marry at the early age of from 12 to 14. Polygamy and slavery prevailed to within the last few years, in fact polygamy prevails to some extent even yet. They are now nearly all not only civilized, but largely christianized, and have schools and colleges, and engage in commerce with the nations of the earth with marvellous success. In 1888 their exports to the United States alone amounted to $806,555, while the imports from this country to that island amount to $823,856. The government is an absolute monarchy and they count back to 37 monarchs who have sat on the throne.

Now, as we have said, when it was discovered that these people were making such wonderful strides in the arts and sciences, and all that goes to make up a very high state of Christian civilization, historians and ethnologists set about to prove that they must be descendants of some other race, rather than the African race.

I. And the first argument advanced was that aborigines were Malayans. This view was especially advocated by Dr. Sclater an eminent naturalist and ethnologist, and to prove this he claims that the island of Madagascar itself was formerly a part of the Malayan peninsular—that the Malayan peninsular at one time extended from where it now is, to Madagascar, and that Madagascar was a part of the peninsular. To strengthen this argument they claim that the continent once extended over this vast area has since been submerged and become the Indian Ocean. of course if it could be proven that the Malayan peninsular once extended from where it is now is to Madagascar, it   would be pretty conclusive that the aboriginess were Malayan— except that we would not know how to account for the woolly hair of the majority of them.

But let us examine this theory more carefully.

1. In the first place, there is no evidence anywhere that there was ever a continent where the Indian Ocean now is.

2. In the second place instead of a slight depression between the Malayan peninsular and Madagascar, as would natturally be in case of a submerged continent, the Indian Ocean is known to be one of the very deepest of the five great oceans. In fact, in some parts of this ocean it is so deep that soundings cannot be made at all. This theory then of a submerged continent or continental extension is purely speculation, to use the mildest term in describing it.

II. Another argument advanced to show that they were of Malay descent was that the trade winds and currents would be favorable to the Malays. This is the view of Mr. G. A. Shaw, once a missionary to Madagascar. It is claimed that a few Malays who are a great maritime race, were cruising in their canoes, when they finally lost their course and were driven by the trade winds toward Madagascar. That they finally reached the island taking their families with them, settled on the south-eastern shores and lived there for several generations until they got sufficiently strong, when they made war on the neighboring tribes, the Vazamba conquered them and fought their way to the high lands of the interior where the capital Tananarivo now is, and established themselves as the rulers of the island. This is the account of the Hovas, now settled in the Aukova district.

1. But against this theory it may be said it is difficult to conceive of persons at that remote date, long before Columbus came to America, with small craft, scarcely larger than our canoes, venturing on such a voyage, and even if they were driven out of their course and blown by the winds to Madagascar it would be almost absurd to think of them living long enough with their families, to be drifted between two and three thousand miles, especially when it is remembered that waterspouts, sea   squalls and the like are of such frequent occurrence at all times in the Indian Ocean.

2. But even if this could be proven it would only account for the Hova, or one of the twenty tribes of the island which Mr. Shaw claims to be different from the rest.

3. The Vazimba, (Mzamba,) were there when the Hovas came, even according to this account, and after all the only persons autochthonores in the island were these Vazimba, which may be a general term to designate all in the island besides the Hovas. There is no effort made to prove that these Vazimba were Malays, but it is difficult to see how it can be proven that the aborigines were Malays, when it is admitted that when the first Malays came they found these Vazimba already autochthonores to the soil.

We doubt very much whether any Malays ever reached the island in its early history, especially in the way they are said to have come. But, granted that a few did come in that way, they could never become any more than one of the twenty other tribes.

III. Another argument used to show that the aborigines are not of African descent, is the fact that some of the words in their language are Polynesian.

1. But while it will be admitted as the most natural thing that some of the words of the Malagasy are Polynesian, the Island lying within the great sweep of the Polynesian language, some of the words would naturally be incorporated into to Malagasy language, but we shall show presently that the Polynesian language finds but little place in the Malagasy language.

IV. Effort was made by Mr. Straniland Wake, in 1880, to prove that the Malagasy were of Siamese descent.

1. But we will not stop to consider that theory, since there is scarcely anything in common between the Siamese and the Malagasy.

Now, instead of going so far out of the way to prove that the Malagasy are not of African descent, how much easier to accept the plain and natural view, that the aborigines of the Island are of African descent. Reason and common sense are certainly in favor of this view.

 

I. In the first place, the close proximity of Madagascar to Africa is a strong argument we think in favor of this view.

From Madagascar it is only two or three hundred miles to Africa, across the Mozambique channel.

II. The position of the Island, long and narrow lying parallel with Africa, shows that it is a continental Island.

III. Besides, this channel is very shallow and dotted with many islands. This would show that Madagascar is but an off-shoot of this continent, and that, therefore, the inhabitants of the Island are Africans.

IV. But even if the Africans were not autochthonous of the soil of Madagascar, it would still be a very easy thing for them to make their way from Africa from one of the small islands to another, until they had finally reached Madagascar.

V. This is all the more possible when it is admitted by Mr. Geo. A. Shaw, who labors so hard to prove that the Malagasy are of Malay extraction, that although the trade winds are generally from East to West, yet that there are times when the wind is from West to East—from Africa to Madagascar.

It is far more reasonable to think of the aborigines in this way making their way from Africa to Madagascar—only about 250 miles—than to think of them drifting three thousand miles across a wild and tempestuous ocean.

But Mr. Shaw argues that it is almost impossible for the early inhabitants of Madagascar to have come from Africa, because the abruptness of the coast of Africa nearest Madagascar would prevent the Africans in those parts from ever becoming a maritime race.

1. To this argument it is but necessary to say, that all students of classical history know that the Greeks around the high mountains of Achaia, Orgollio, and other portions of the Peloponnesus, coming down from their mountain fastnesses, engaged in sea faring life and made themselves, by daring and adventurous exploits, terrors to all mariners passing by those parts. And why should it be thought incredible that the Africans on the east coast of Africa could do likewise.

2. But even if they did not venture to go there of their   own accord, since, as Mr. Shaw claims, it was customary for the Malays in those days to carry their families with them on their voyages, so the Africans might have had their women in their boats with them and been drifted across the channel to Madagascar.

VI. But there are other arguments in favor of the aborigines of this Island being Africans.

Dr. Hildebrandt, the great German traveller, ethnologist, naturalist, and expert in craneology, often having made many cranium and facial measurements, with the best scientific instruments, and noticing carefully the physiognomy of the people in southern and central Africa, and comparing them with many which he made in Madagascar, gave as his opinion, that the inhabitants of the entire island of Madagascar were of African descent. And Mr. Shaw, who holds radically different views from Dr. Hildebrandt, admits that "In a conversation which I had with him (Dr. Hildebrandt) in the early part of 1881, he said that as far as his research in the matter went, he was led to the conclusion that while there was some slight indications of a connection in race between the Hovas, and the South Africans, the similarity between the coast tribes of the island and those of Central Africa was most marked." This is a strong argument, for Dr. Hildebrandt is probably the best authority of all the ethnologists who have visited the island. This is important, too, because he does not even grant that the Hovas are any other than Africans.

VII. Mr. Dahle, also an ethnologist of some distinction, gives his opinion of the early inhabitants of Madagascar as follows: "The Vazimba may have been a collective name for several East African tribes, growing gradually into disuse as these tribes became more separated, and from this common stock the original inhabitants of Madagascar, the Vizamba, were an off-shoot."

VIII. Then the language of the Malagasy resembles very much the language of the Hottentots and other tribes inhabiting the central and south-eastern coasts of Africa.

Take a few words for example, and notice how much more closely the Malagasy and African resemble each other, then the   Malagasy and the Malaysia. These words we take from Prof. Keolle's splendid "Polyglotta Africana:"

So that we see that even in language these people are Africans. And what is true of one portion of the island relative to their language is practically true of the entire island. Mr. Wallace admits that when he says that "the language throughout Madagascar is essentially one, divided certainly into a number of dialects, but differing no more from one another than that of Lancashire from the English of London." Thus it appears from the language that the Hovas even are African, for they have no other language than the frizzle haired Sakalavas or Betsileos.

IX. Again the Flora in Madagascar is almost identical with that of Africa. The Man-eating and one or two other plants which are peculiar to Madagascar, are about the only exceptions.

X. Then what is true of the flora is also true of the fauna. There are a great many animals in Madagascar, but comparatively few different species. But what few species there are, are all, with one or two exceptions, such as are found across the Mozambique channel in Africa.

But Rev. Mr. Geo A. Shaw makes one more argument to prove that the aborigines of Madagascar were Malayans. He resorts to certain traditions among the Hovas, to the effect that "they (the Hovas) came to the island and found a few black faced people called Vazimba, short of stature, which they (the Hovas) subdued and finally exterminated, and took possession of the island."

1. There might be something in such an argument, but for the fact that nineteen of the twenty tribes now inhabiting the   island have black faces. The blacks, instead of being exterminated are the predominant class throughout the island, numbering almost nine to one of the brighter olive colored ones.

XI. Then the tribal life which prevails in East Africa is almost identical with that of Madagascar.

XII. To a Negro of America it is not strange to hear of these Hovas trying to disconnect themselves form the rest of their Malagasy brethren, when we remember who have been their teachers. Caucasians alone have been their teachers, and Mr. Douglass announced a very truth when he said, "that the first thing white missionaries teach heathen of any race, especially African, is that the white man is superior to all others." If the white missions taught this lesson to the Malagasy, it is but natural that those of the lightest complexion should try to make themselves the descendants of any race rather than the African. And in doing so, they do nothing more than many Negroes in America do when they try to pass for Spaniards, Creoles and the like.

And if we were driven to it, we would account for the light complexion of the Hovas very much as we account for the light complexion of certain of the Negroes of America. Especially when we remember that the Hovas for centuries claim to have resided on the east of the island, near Tamatave, the principal sea port town, where Frenchmen, Portuguese, and Englishmen have been landing for the past six centuries.

Again it is natural for Mr. G. A. Shaw to try to prove that the Malagasy are other than of African descent, since he ha married one of the beauties of Madagascar himself. And it would be an unpardonable breach of race loyalty for him, a white man, to have married an African girl.

Thus it appears that although in this country and almost every other country white men look with disdain on wooly heads and black faces, as being characteristics of the despised Negro alone, yet in these far off civilizations, where Negroes have accomplished such wonders, these same white men even lay claim to wooly hair and black faces, in order to deprive these Negroes of the honor due them.

 

We conclude with the remark that these noble, intelligent, enterprising, sable sons and daughters of Madagascar are Africans— a part of the great race with which we Afro-Americans are identified. And we hail with joy the phenomenal success they have made in all that goes to make up a high state of Christian civilization. Their success is only so much success of the entire African race.

Jersey City, N. J.
