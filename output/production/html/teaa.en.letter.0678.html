London
February 7th 1883

To,
The Right Honorable
Earl Granville K.G.
Her Majesty's Principal Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs,

My Lord,

Having carefully considered the report of our affairs with the French Government which has lately appeared in its "Documents Diplomatiques," we beg to submit to Your Lordship's friendly attention the following few brief notes which we feel are alike demanded by the interests of truth and the welfare of our cause.

With regard to the correspondence of Monsr Baudais, we shall not trespass upon your time by denying in detail the many misrepresentations thereon made. It was not to be expected that his letters would give a unprejudiced account of the state of affairs between France and Madagascar. But we did look forward to finding a fair historical record of our dealings with French officials at Paris in the Government document already referred to, and are sorry to be compilled to assert that such record has not been given.

Permit us to remind Your Lordship of what we have already had the honour of stating in our communication of 9th December 1882 with regard to the manner in which our business was transacted in Paris.

Except at the very beginning of the negotiations, our affairs were not dealt with by those who had been   been officially appointed to discuss them on the part of France. In all the attempts to come to an understanding the French Government was represented by intermediaries who we have reason to believe conducted the business with their own personal advantage chiefly in view. By these gentlemen we were given to understand that there was not the slightest hope of avoiding immediate hostilities with France except by making some concession to the National pride. We had clearly refused to acknowledge the claim to a Protectorate in our Note to Messrs les Commissaires (No. 23 of Documents Diplomatiques) on the subject, and continued to do so throughout. But on being promised by the intermediaries that a temporary removal of our new Custom Houses from the North West Coast, and an offer to grant long leases of land would lead to a withdrawal of the threatened protectorate, we verbally expressed our willingness, thus to meet the French Government halfway; our conversations at this time we taken by the intermediaries & made up by them into the Notes Nos. 24 & 26 of "Documents Diplomatiques," which we do not admit as correct interpretations of our meaning.

Our last words to the intermediaries on this subject were to the effect that the removal of the Custom Houses was to be distinctly regarded as only temporary. We were also assured that after these verbal agreement had been placed upon paper we should have an opportunity of reconsidering them before being expected to affix our signatures.

The next event was our being asked by Monsieur Ducluc through a Government Clerk to sign   sign a document which has since received the name of "Ultimatum" to which we specially desire to call Your Lordship's attention. It is not reproduced in its original integrity in No. 27 of "Documents Diplomatiques" but is given in a manned calculated to create an unfavorable opinion of our refusal to sign it.

In the published report a whole paragraph has been omitted, the very portion to which our refusal attached itself as being contrary to what we had been led to expect as the result of making concessions to National pride. The omitted paragraph reads "Il est bien entendre etc," and will be found in the copy of the original document which we have the honor of now enclosing for Your Lordship's inspection.

In the face of such an unjust misrepresentation, unprecedented we would hope in the transactions of civilized Government, it seems a small matter to call Your Lordship's notice to the incorrect statements made by Monsieur Baudais to which we have already referred.

But the more we examine his misleading language, the more we discover the unworthy ill-feeling which prompted its use, and hence we venture to ask Your Lordship to give a moments consideration to the comments we have made thereupon in the copy of the "Documents Diplomatiques" which accompanies the present letter.

We have the honor to be, with the highest consideration.

My Lord
Your Lordship's most obedient servants
