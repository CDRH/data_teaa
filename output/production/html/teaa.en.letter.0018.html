No 12.

United States Commercial Agency
Loanda January 22 1861

Sir.

Herewith I enclose Returns from this Commercial Agency for the Quarter ending December 31 '60. The Journal Accounts & Certificate I have directed to the Treasury Department & have drawn on the Hon Secretary of the Treasury for $219.12Â¢ being Balance of my Salary for that Quarter.

Under date of October 23rd I had the honor of addressing you & enclosing Relations for the Quarter ending September 30 '60. The Fever then prevailing here I am now pleased to say, was of short duration & did not affect the old residents much, being mostly confined to newcomers.

I have now to inform Your Honor that your despatch of August 10th respecting the "disposition made by the British Government of the Africans captured from Slavers by the British Navy", & also as to the "disposition made by that Government" of the officers & crews of these Slavers was Hon Lewis Cass
Secretary of State. was not received of this Agency 'till November, & as I could not send an answer in season for the next session of Congress. I have delayed it 'till the present time.

As regards the "disposition of the Africans", I cannot give you any reliable information, but presuming you have sent the same circular to the US Consuls at St Helena, Sierra Leone & English West Indies, they will be able to give such information to the Department as is now required.

Respecting the "disposition of officers & crews of Slavers", I have to inform your Honor that when a suspected vessel is boarded by the English, such inducements & threats as given that the Master suspects more is known of him then in fact really is, which generally leads to the giving up the vessel, when the officers have their choice of giving to St Helena or Sierra Leone, or being landed at some convenient place on the Coast. The Crews nine times out of ten are put on shore immediately. If they are fortunate enough to be landed near the Congo River they can find friends among the Traders there, who keep them 'till an opportunity offers of Shipping, when they take this chance again. but many have been left in desolate parts of the Coasts & if they escaped being killed or robbed by the passing tribes they have generally died of African fever or hunger. Although it sometimes happens that one or two Americans are found among the crews of these vessels, yet generally they are comprised of Portuguese & Spaniards.

Your despatch of November 7th informing me that "some Consular forms & a Boat flag have been sent to my address" is received. The vessel being the above is now in port & on receipt of the package I am in hopes to find that instead of a "Boat flag" I shall find one for the "Consulate flag staff," that being what I requested in my despatch No 10 under date Sept 15th.

The Portuguese have again been fighting with the Natives North of this, burning several Towns &c. but as the Towns are built of Mud & Straw, the damage done did not amount to much, except helping draw rather largely on the Angola Treasury, which is & has been for a long time in a very undesireable condition.

Very Respectfully
Your Obt Servt
John G. Willis
