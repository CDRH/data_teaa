Mr. Heli Chatelain
To the Department of State.

Subject:

Miscellaneous News.

Abstract of Contents.

I - The first rains have been abundant. Hence: sickness, perspective plenty, lack of free labor, and greater demand for unfree labor, and increased traffic in same.

II - The Congo-State recruiting agent, tho' offering high prices, has not yet obtained any men. The idea of Angola's independence gaining ground both in Portugal and here. The three principal political convicts continue to escape. The commissioner for boundaries with Congo State still here. The government immigrants a curse rather than a blessing.

III - A new line of steamers has been started by a Jewish firm.

IV - A young man from Congo District, belonging to Baptist Mission there, is released, owing to my unofficial steps intercession

V - A missionary on the Congo wants to know whether he has not the right to fly the stars and stripes, as he pleases.

VI The King of Garenganze rejects the British Protectorate

 
No. 17

Consulate of the United States,
St. Paul de Loanda, Dec. 31, 1891

Honorable William F. Wharton
Assistant Secretary of State,
Washington, D.C.

Sir:

Desiring to inform the Department of various occurrences, each of which is not important enough for a special Report, but may be of some use, especially to the editor of the Consular Reports, I collect them all in one Despatch of Miscellaneous News.

I. Rains, Sickness, Plenty, Lack of free labor and demand for unfree labor. After two years of drought, the first rains (Sept. to Jan.) have been very copious and this fact is attended by the three following consequences: (1) With the unusual dampness of the season, fevers and other tropical diseases have increased to an alarming extent. Nobody escapes the effects of the miasma; one by one, all are laid on the sick-bed, some to rise no more, the great majority, to ail on for some time and then resume their normal state and usual occupations. I have been suffering   these two months from a kind of rheumatism and hand have had two review attacks of fever.

(2) With the drought, the dearth is coming to an end, and 1892 promises to be a your of plenty. How great the dearth was, may be inferred from the fact that large quantities of Brazilian manioc-meal ("farinha de pás") were imported into this land of manioc and sold for about half the price of the best native article, while foreign (American) corn, rice and beans successfully competed with those of the country. One good result of the dearth is that the natives have learnt to eat bread, and having once acquired the habit, are not likely to give it up with the return of cheap native produce. This enlarged consumption of bread means an important increase in the importation of American flour.

(3) As soon as the showers soften the soil, the natives leave the exacting service of the whites and take the cultivation of their own fields. This causes a lack of carriers for the rubber and wax of the far interior as well as for the coffee of the Cazengo plantations and a general insufficiency of free hired laborers for the railroad and all other work depending on them. This shortening necessarily entails an increased demand for unfree,   or contract, labor. Within a short time, the prices of "servicaes" at Novo Redondo (Novo Redondo) and Benguella, which are now the principal markets, have risen from 40 to 70 milreis (one milreis = $1.09) per head, and the prices may rise to double that amount before long. The dealers say that the Congo State recruiting agent offers them 750 milreis per man, and make this a plea for screwing up the prices of course the greater the profits realized in the business the greater also the activity developed in procuring the merchandise, that is in "redeeming" slaves from the interior, which is simply a revised edition, of the old slave-traffic.

II Political News. The Congo State officer, Mons. Braconnier, who has taken the place of the unfortunate Vandevelde, as recruiting agent of 500 Angola men for the Congo State army is still here at a hotel waiting for the Portuguese residents to relax their opposition to this exportation of unfree labor. The resistance of the colonists to the home government in this matter has met with the approval of the whole colonial and liberal press in Portugal. Some papers even go so far as to advise Angola to demand self government and prepare its independence. The prospect of bankruptcy in Portugal inclines the Portuguese residents in favor of the native ideal of independence.

Rather separation than shame is the motto of many. Meanwhile, here as in Portugal, men of action are   not to be found.

Aided by friends and officials, the three principal rebels of the late insurrection at Oporto, have managed to escape from their exile. One went to Gaboon; the whereabouts of the two others is known only to the friends who saw them off.

Senhor Dantas Baracho, the Portuguese Commissioner for defining the Eastern boundary of Angola with the Congo State has been here since August doing nothing but earn his salary, the total of which is said to amount to twelve Pounds (60 dollars) per day. Nor is there any sign or probability of his going to his destination before next May or June. As his salary is charged to the Provincial Treasury, the dissatisfaction of the colonists may be imagined.

Every Portuguese packet continues to bring from 50-60 emigrants sent to the West-Coast by the Portuguese Government in order to deplete the prisons and get rid of dangerous and starving tramps. While rational colonization would be a blessing to the Province and the home-country, this kind of assisted emigration is a curse and a source of endless troubles.

III New Line of Steamers. Nothing proves better the immense resources and growing prosperity of the Province then the rapid and steady development of steam navigation. While our   Philadelphia Board of Trade and enterprising men of Baltimore and New York have talked for years about a steam ship line to connect America with Africa, and have not yet reached the point of sending a trial steamer, the humble Jewish firm of Benchimol & Sobrinho (Lisbon & Loanda) has within five months bought, repaired and run between Angola, S. Thomé, Lisbon and Liverpool, four old English steamers, a far trips of which will suffice to pay their cost. Without lowering the freight-charged by the other company without Government aid, the enterprize has proved a grand success.

There is some talk of a third Portuguese Company running steamers between the same ports.

IV - Vita case. About a year ago, a pupil of the Baptist Mission at San Salvador (in the Congo District of this Province) called Vita, accidentally shot one his comrades. He was examined by the Portuguese Resident (who knows neither English nor the Kongo language) and sent, a prisoner, to Loanda, there to be judged by the competent authority. As, after months of waiting, the missionaries heard nothing about the lad, two of them came at different times to Loanda to find out his whereabouts and to have him bought to trial Mr. Ed. Bannister (then acting British Consul) did what he could for them, but in vain

 

Hearing of my arrival, the missionaries thought my popularity might be more successful, and wrote to ask my intervention on the lad's behalf. I answered, that, as the lad was a Portuguese subject I could do nothing officially, but would use my private influence[?] in favor of their protégé. Not only was I immediately told where the prisoner was, and allowed to see him, but the "Delegado" who was to judge the case, and to whom I explained the particulars, released the poor fellow with the statement that the papers concerning the case had disappeared. The young man had been kept eight months in jail without trial and (forgotten as his case was) might have rotted away there, had not his missionary protection persevered to the end.

V Stars and Stripes in Congo State. On Dec. 8 Rev: J. Chester Hyde, of the American Baptist Mission[?] wrote to me as the nearest consul, since the absence death of Lieut. Taunt, desiring some information on the right of flying the Stars and Stripes in the Congo State. "In Nov. (1891)" says he[?] "Governor General Wahis; returning from the interior, visited me, and I flew the stars and stripes in his honor. The next day the commissary of our district informed me that only consuls were allowed to display their national colors beyond the limits of Matadá and I was requested to comply   with the laws. Perhaps you may have some instructions concerning the stars and stripes. I answered that (1) the Congo State is outside my consular sphere, (2) but I would communicate the matter to you, and (3) in my private opinion the display of flags is a matter of courtesy, not of right, but that if, in identia[?] circumstances, other nationalities were allowed to display their colors, an American might expect the same privilege.

Complaints against over regulation and taxation, and irksome red-tape in the Congo State are general among traders and missionaries of all nationalities.

VI I have just seen, on his way home, Mr. Faulkner, a Canadian missionary, who spent a few years in Katanga, or Garenganze, as it is known through Fred. A. Arnot's book. He told me how Mushiri, the King of Garnganze, was alarmed when he saw Mr. Sharpe, the agent of the "British South Africa Company" approach his capital from the East, "by the back door" as he styled it, and how he absolutely rejected the proposed British Protectorate and bade Mr. Sharpe leave his country at once, as soon as he heard that the British wanted his copper mines. As is well known, the Congo State has already conceded all those mines and the whole of Mushiri's Kingdom to the Belgian Katanga Company, though no Belgian had ever set there a foot or got permission from Mushiri to exploit   his land and mines. Quite recently the Belgian expedition of said Company and the Congo State have been at Mushiri's capital and may have been more fortunate than Mr. Sharpe.

I am, Sir,
Your obedient servant
Heli Chatelain
