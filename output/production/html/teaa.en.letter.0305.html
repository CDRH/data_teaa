No. 3

Consulate of the United States of America at Sierra Leone
22nd March 1866.

Sir

By the mail packet, in a despatch No 2. dated the 16th inst. I informed you of the change in the Government of this place, or rather of the British colonies on this part of the West Coast of Africa.

Taking into consideration the above change, which Her Majesty's government has deemed necessary, on account of the growing prosperity, and importance of of the British settlements about this coast, I would most respectfully suggest it be a fitting opportunity to appoint a higher consular officer than that of a consular agent.

In making this suggestion I beg leave should it meet with the approval Hon W. H. Seward
Secretary of State
&c &c &c   approval of the department to request that I might receive the appointment, and will state my reasons for feeling some confidence in so doing.

I am a citizen of the United States, (Salem Mass) of native born parents, I have been a resident of this colony for seven years, arriving in April 1859 the commencement of the epidemic of that year which carried off about 80 of the white inhabitants among others John E. Tylor Esq the Acting Consul of this place, for the United States, and as the colony lost 40 whites during the rains of last year I may safely say I have passed through two epidemic seasons since my arrival, so with so long a residence and passing through two epidemics safely I feel I have reason to consider myself thoroughly acclimated.

Five years and six months of the   the seven years I have held the position of Consular Agent of the United States, and as such I have had to do all the work of a consul, without the full powers, and priviledges of such an officer.

The three years before the war our commercial transactions with this place were very large and it was common to see from Eight to ten American vessels in port at the same time but in the latter part of 1860 our difficulties commencing interfered very much with business and both imports and exports rapidly decreased, although it improved a little during the years 1861 and 1862 it was however, carried on almost entirely under the British flag.

Now that the war is over I confidently expect to see the business of this colony reach   reach its healthiest average of about $300,000 or $350,000, and this with the American trade with Gambia would swell[?] the transaction of business between the United States, and the Coast under the the new Governor General to about $550,000 to $600,000 it is really an interest the U.S. Government should consider and give it all the protection in its power.

The unfortunate civil war having closed, American vessels are already beginning to arrive and others are cleaning for the place from the United States, this will increase the work of the office which during the war has certainly been rather slack, an other addition also to the the work now, will be the looking after the interests of U.S. vessels of war in the port; their arrival I am informed by Commander   Commander Harrell of the "Kearsarge", which came into the port to coal, and sailed on the 14th for down the coast, will be frequently hereafter.

Aside from our commerce requiring a higher Consular officer, by the provisions of the slave treaty with Great Britain necessitates the station of a consul if not a higher officer here, as this is one of the places named[?] as the position of the mixed commission court, and in event of absence if judge and arbitrater being absent the present position will not allow of acting for them. Such a contingency has arisen as the judge is I believe in Washington and the Arbitrater been obliged to leave on amount of severe illness.

There are in the place a French Consul, Spanish consul, and our Consul, Portuguese Consul, Consul   Consul for the Netherlands, and Liberian Consul.

The French Government knowing of the change here have given their Consul authority to appoint Consular agents in all places under the new Governor Generalship, and also are about to send him out a small steamer to carry him to the different places under the new government, their commerce cannot arrive to that importance, excepting in exports, which ours holds it its prosperous periods.

Earnestly desiring the reasons herein set forth will convince the department, that this is a Consulate which should be represented by a higher consular officer of the United States

I have the honor to be
Sir
Your obed't Servant
Henry Rider
U.S. Consular Agent
