Subject. Loss of the ship "Joseph Nickerson"

Abstract of contents.

Ship lost at Sharks Point, River Congo, 20th December 1876. Vessel attacked by Natives. Crew saved & sent to England per s. s Ethopia. Master's declaration & Register of ship enclosed.

No. 120.

United States Commercial Agency
St. Paul de Loanda
29th December 1876.

Sir,

I have the honor to bring to your notice the loss of the ship "Joseph Nickerson" of New York, owned by Messrs Yates & Porterfield of New York, on the 20th December 1876, at Shark's Point, Congo River.

From the statement of the Captain, a copy of which is herewith enclosed, it appears that she took the ground in entering the River in consequence of the strong current that was recurring[?], attempts more made to get her off, and the vessel would in To the
Assistant Secretary of State
Washington, U.S. in all probability have been saved, had not the natives come down in numbers and cut the stern hawsers by which she was being warped[?] into deep water.

Valuable and friendly assistance was rendered by the Agent and other members of the Dutch House (Afrikaansche Handelsvereening) from Banana, about eight miles distant. Their Agent here in Loanda, has applied to me for information as to their compensation or salvage, neither the vessel nor any portion of the Cargo was saved, and I have referred them to the owners in New York.

Her Britannic Majesty's steam vessel "Avon" has gone to Shark's Shark's Point, at the request of the English consul to enquire into the matters. Last year a similar act of piracy occurred in the Congo River, and the natives were very severely punished by the English Commodore.

In granting the seamen a passage to Liverpool, England, I had to take into consideration that there were no American Vessels in this Port, and that their detention here at this time, the most dangerous and unhealthy to unacclimatized persons, was not advisable.

I beg to bring to your notice the name of Captain J. W. Davis, of the African Royal Mail Steamer "Ethiopia", who kindly gave the Captain and shipwrecked men of the "Joseph Nickerson" Nickerson" a free passage from Banana Congo River, to Loanda, and, he has also promised to put two of the men on his articles if he can find a vacancy for them on the passage home.

I have communicated all the foregoing circumstances to the United States consul in Liverpool, to whose charge the men have been sent.

I further enclose the Register delivered to me by the Captain of the "Joseph Nickerson" although I am unable to find instructions to do so.

I have the honor to be
Your most obedt Servt,
Robt S Newton

Declaration made before me, Robt. Scott Newton, Vice-Commercial Agent for the United States of America, this Twenty-seventh day of December, one thousand eight hundred and Seventy six, by Captain A. C. Yates of the Schooner "Joseph Nickerson" of New York, Official No 13252, letters H. L. K. J. and owned by Messrs Yates & Porterfield of New York.

I left Ambriz on the 18th December and shaped a course for the Congo River. On the 20th December I arrived off Shark's Point. The Cargo consisted of some Tar, turpentine, paint oil, and black heart, and about 18 tons of coffee shipped in Ambriz. The wind was light and the and the current very strong running out of the River; so in order to make the Port of Banana I was obliged to hug the southern shore, and the forefoot of the ship took the sand about 12 o'clock in the day. I immediately had a hedge anchor carried out by the "Yawl"[?] under the charge of the chief officer, and was succeeding in warping the vessel off, when a number of natives came of from Shark's Point. I should estimate them at about between 400 and 500, in about 70 canoes. They swarmed about the ship, and they cut the Hawser I had out to my Ridge[?]. I attempted to get another Ridge out, but the natives prevented me. Three men came on board and tendered me   me assistance. they called themselves "King Peter's Son," — "Jack Wash" — "Walker".

After my Hawser was cut I endeavoured to make sail on the ship, thinking that as the ship was so nearly afloat, the sail could drive her off, but the natives swarmed over the ship's side, and with their knives cut the sails to pieces, rendering them perfectly useless. Up to this moment I had received assistance from these three men before mentioned, but after this all seemed to join in the plunder, cabin doors were torn off, all the provisions and spare rigging were taken out of the tween decks. I cannot say for certain that these three men took part in the plundering but but afterwards I saw many things belonging to the ship in the native huts, particularly, I wish to mention, a trunk belonging to Mr. Swanson the mate, in the King's house.

The pirates cut the coffee sacks open and carried away as much as they could. Four of my men and the chief officer took the yawl and went to Banana to the Dutch House for assistance, which was rendered on the following day. the Agent Mr Bloëme and Mr Yungblood and about 10 other Europeans or White men all well armed, came over with about 100 Kroomen, and drove the natives out of the ship. in fact the natives ran away when they saw saw the assistance coming. I left the ship about 6 o'clock in the evening of the day she took the beach and going in a native came on shore was upset and nearly drowned. I consider this was intentionally done, as I heard one man say, "if I was drowned there would be an end of the palaver". The Dutch men after driving the natives out examined the remainder of the cargo and found the coffee so saturated with oil and tar, that had been spilled by the natives, as to be utterly worthless. I must here state that in the arrival of the Dutchmen I went back on board of the vessel and found her completely gutted   gutted, bulkheads gone, copper torn off, sails and rigging cut, in fact a perfect, useless wreck. I left after this, and shortly afterwards discovered the vessel to be on fire. I cannot say by whom she was set on fire. I saved the ship's papers and chronometer, but lost all my own personal effects. I wish also to state that when I saw my trunk (I mean Mr Swanson's trunk) in the King's house, I called his attention to it, and said, "if you are not playing me false: how comes it that this trunk is in your house?" He said in a sharp tone of voice, "I know nothing about it. some man must have brought it in here   here". I make this statement just to show how much one can believe in the sincerity of their friendliness. The King also showed me a Paper made between himself and Sir William Hewitt R. N of Great Britain, in which he was recommended as a trustworthy man

Signed. A. C. Yates
Master of late Schr Jos. Nickerson
