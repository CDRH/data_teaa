Department of State.
Monrovia. January 23, 1891.

His Excellency
H. R. W. Johnson

Mr. President,

When I arrived in the United States, on the 14th September 1890 I found the Country in a great state of excitement consequent on the elections, which were held on the 4th of November last.

I found it would be useless to go on to Washington then, as Mr. Secretary Blaine was away on an election tour, and he did not return until after the elections. After the elections were over which resulted disastrously for the present Administration in nearly all the States. I went to Washington, on the 10th of November, and the next day, Hon John H. Smyth, with whom I stopped while in the City, accompanied me to the State Department, when we found twenty or more persons, all waiting to get an interview   interview with the Secretary. We waited about half a day, and were then informed that the Secretary would not be at his office that day. Mr. Smyth and I called the next day, but after waiting some hours he had to leave, but I remained, and about 1 o'clock the Secretary came, and I was admitted to an interview for about ten minutes. Mr. Blaine then excused himself, as the new Spanish Minister was then waiting by appointment to be taken to the White House to be presented to the President. Mr Secretary asked me to call on him the next day, and instructed the usher to bring me "right in", as soon as he got to the office. I called the next Saturday but after waiting until after 12 m, we were told, to the great disappointment of all of us then waiting, that the Secretary would not be at his office that day.

I called at the Department the next Monday and Tuesday; but with the same result. One of the gentlemen that I met, waiting to see the Secretary, told me   me that he had called daily for fourteen days, and when I left, he had not yet been favored with an interview.

I then left Washington, and from Philadelphia I wrote a letter to Secretary Blaine, copy of which I now present. After waiting several days without an answer, I wrote Mr Coppinger and requested him to call upon the Secretary and inquire whether my letter had been shown to him, and feared that it might be laid down somewhere, and not come under his notice. Mr. Coppinger wrote me that he had called several times at the Department, but without success, but that he would still try to carry out my wishes.

Mr Blaine received me very courteously, and but for the fact that the Spanish Minister was then waiting by his appointment, I think he would have given me a much longer and favorable interview. I requested the Secretary to send me an answer to New York   New York, and perhaps I may get it by next mail.

This matter having been initiated allow me to suggest, Mr. President, that it be followed up through the State Department, as I deem it of great importance, at this time, that we have a demonstration of friendship from one of the Great Powers.

I have the honor to be Mr President
Your obedient Servant
W. M. Davis
Commissioner &c.
