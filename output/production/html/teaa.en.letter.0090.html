St. Paul de Loanda
15th November 1869.

Messrs Grant Brodie &ca
London

Dear Sirs

On the 15th of last month, while at Dondo, I met a merchant by name José Gomes da Silva, who related to me a most interesting account of his travels through the interior of Africa, as far as the upper bank of the Zambeze river, on a mission for the purchase of slaves, for the estate of Albino José Soares da Costa Magalhaes, a well known coffee planter of Cazengo.

On my enquiring if he had heard any news of the whereabouts of Dr. Livingstone, he told me that he had heard he was killed, and related a very startling account of the circumstances connected with, and leading to his death.

Although interested at the time, I paid little more attention to the subject, until my return to Loanda; a few days afterwards I read an article in the "Times" of Sept 7th[?], which convinced me that no reliable information had reached England, regarding[?] the existence[?] of Dr. Livingstone   since 1868.

Sr Gomes da Silva, arrived in Loanda two days after me, and I embraced the opportunity to envite him to my house, with a view of obtaining from him all the information he possessed regarding the unhappy fate of Dr. Livingstone. He readily complied with my request, and I was so convinced of the truth of his statement, that at my request he addressed me a letter on the subject, a copy of which I beg to enclose, knowing that any thing which may tend to throw light on the fate of that distinguished traveller must be of the greatest interest to you and your countrymen.

Snr Gomes da Silva states that after a dangerous journey he crossed the River Congo on a bridge made of trunks of trees, in the territory of Lucuna. near to King Catende; three days afterwards, taking the sun as his guide, he proceeded in a south-easterly direction, and after a March of twelve days through many large and wonderful plains, he arrived at the town of King Manguangua on the banks of the River Zambesi, having expended ninety five days in travelling from Malange in the province of Angola.

 

On the 15th June 1868, two months after his arrival there, it came to his knowledge that an ambassador (Cacuata) with five hundred natives had arrived at King Chindo's, who reigned on the opposite bank of the Zambezi, in order to pay the customary tributes to his superior Muata[?] Invo, the ambassador being from the King of Cazembe.

His curiosity induced him to invite to King of Cazembe's ambassador, to come and see him, and as it was on his way the ambassador accepted the invitation, bringing with him the five hundred natives who had accompanied him.

In the course of some conversation which he had with the ambassador, regarding the roads which lead to Mozambique, it accurred to him to enquire concerning Dr Livingstone, when the ambassador immediately replied "dont speak to me of the great fetish" and then enquired if he were any relative of his. Being alarmed at the manner and tone of this remark he did not think it advisable to continue the conversation.

The ambassador however remained with him several days, and Snr Gomes da Silva's kind hospitality soon won the confidence of his guest, who one evening invited him to his   hut, where were prepared two calabashes of cacollo, an intoxicating drink made from fermented honey, and then he was enabled to obtain further information regarding Dr Livingstone. The ambassador, having sent away his servent, and speaking in a manner calculated to impress the greatest secrecy, gave Snr da Silva the following information regarding the death of Dr Livingstone.

Referring to Dr Livingstone, he said that the Calunga was a great fetishman that he spoke every day with the sun, never slept in houses, was not afraid of wild animals; that he kept one in a box, and never gave it any thing to eat, but would open the box, always when he wanted know the right road, and never asked this information from any one else; at times he would open large rolls of paper, and would talk to them with great earnestness; he would cross rivers without a canoe, and did many other things equally astonishing.

In his previous travels when he crossed from Puto-Ambaca (St Paul de Loanda) he had already, among the natives, the reputation of being a great calunga or wizard, as the ambassador said about that time no one dared look him in the face from the impression they entertain of his   supernatural powers.

It seems that on his journey from St. Paul de Loanda to the East Coast he encamped for the night in the district of Cazembe, twenty day journey from the spot where they now were, and a few days after his departure, one of the King's sons took ill suddenly, and died; the king attributing his death to the evil influence of Dr Livingstone whom he accordingly sent after to destroy. Dr Livingstone was too far advanced be overtaken, and thus escaped, although this circumstances was in the end the cause of his death, although long afterwards.

It was on his present expedition, that he again came into this district, and the King being informed of the approach of the great Calunga, sent out at once to meet him, and presented him with a cup of "muange," a poisoned drink, the effect of which, in the belief of the natives, decides the guilt or innocence of the accused, and thus certain death awaits the suspected, such was the unfortunate result to Dr Livingstone, and while suffering from the effects he was [illegible]   since denied, but received no further information.

Horrible as this story may appear, yet from the several interviews I had with Snr Gomes da Silva, and the earnest attention I paid to repeated narrations of his story, and the exactness of each repetition, the locality from which he derived his information, the intense anxiety exhibited by the ambassador to ensure secrecy from his own tribe, together with the fact, that Dr Livingstone was supposed to be in that vicinity at that time, from all these facts combined I am inclined to attach credence to the story of Snr Gomes da Silva, and to believe that a sorrowful light of truthfulness has been thrown on the fate of this illustrious traveller.

You will observe from the foregoing that Dr Livingstone's reputation of being a Calunga or wizard among the native tribes of the interior of Africa, dates from his journey from the Cape of Good Hope to St. Paul de Loanda. All African traders are aware that a horrible death awaits the unfortunate native who is accused of witch-craft. Travellers on the Quanza [illegible] with the famous [illegible]   been hurled, after having been dragged bound hand and foot for many miles, therefore it is not improbable that Dr Livingstone ended his valuable life a victim to native superstition.

It is a matter of regret to me to be the medium of information of so sad a nature, but while deeming it my duty to give publicity, to so important a revelation, I trust that intelligence received from other quarters will prove the statement I have written to be unreliable, and that Dr Livingstone is still spared and destined some day to return to his country.

I am
Dear Sirs
Yours Faithfully
(Sgd) Augustus Archer Silva
