U.S. Consulate, Goree-Dakar
March 25th 1897

Mr Peter Strickland
To the Department of State

Subject

Sending two destitute missionaries to Liberia, at a cost of 202.50 for their passages.

 
No 200

U.S. Consulate, Goree-Dakar
Mch 25th 1897

To the Honorable
Assistant Secretary of State
Washington

Sir: I have the honor to bring to your notice, that on the 27th of January last I paid the passages from here to Monrovia, in the German Str Karl Woermann, of two destitute American Missionaries, T.[?] Arndt aand his Wife, which amounted to 202fcs.50c, and I desire to know if I am entitled to any compensation from the Government for this seeming necessary act, as I do not know what could have become of the parties if I had not thus befriended them.

Mr Arndt appeared quite unexpectedly before me on the 19th of January last, while I was receiving a farewell visit from the English Consul, then on his way to Teneriffe, and at once stated the position that himself and wife were in. He said that he and his Wife were last from Tacoma, where I believe they had been engaged in Mission Work   in the Methodist connection, but that while there he had thought he should be more usefully employed in Liberia, he having had experience in mission work among the colored people in Louisiana. Upon application to the Society however he was told that owing to a lack of funds caused by the "hard times" he could not at that time be sent out, and that the society was in too great straights financially to think of adding to its expenses. Nothing daunted however Mr Arndt still kept his project in his mind, until by one of the strangest of chances a Norweigan Barque called the Elisea arrived at Tacoma chartered to load timber for this place. Mr Arndt being himself of Norweigan extraction, made the acquaintance of the Captain (Olsen) who it appears at that time sympathised with the objects he had in view, and offered to take him and his wife as far as Dakar free of expense. They accordingly embarked, and after a pleasant passage of 3 1/2 months around[?] Cape Horn, they arrived duly   at Dakar, where they met with their first disappointment in not finding the place near to Liberia as they had anticipated that it was. They had at the time of their arrival only fifteen dollars left between them, and as the Captain of the Elisea had observed before the end of the voyage that Mrs Arndt was likely soon to be confined with a child, he was betraying the utmost anxiety to get them out of his vessel before the event should occur, claiming that he was worthy of the greatest credit for having brought them so far for nothing.

Here was a dilemma I thought which exceeded the wildest dreams of romance; are worthy to take place in the strange African Continent. The narrator of all this appeared to be a perfectly sane, fairly well educated, and in all other particulars a very respectable person, and his Wife, whom I saw afterward, seemed perfectly adapted to him. What, under the circumstances, was the proper thing to do?   The English Consul, who was soon obliged to leave me for his steamer was so astonished that he could hardly recover his speech, and before going away slipped me half a guinea in gold as a contribution for assisting in whatever I might find necessary to be done.

My first impulse was to defend the Consulate, which I did by telling the man that as I understood my duty I did not feel authorized to assist him officially; that as he and the captain he came with were responsible for his being here, he had best arrange with the captain for staying in the vessel until he and his wife could reach a port in some country where they would have the sympathy and assistance of their co-religionists. I told him that Monrovia was more than 500 miles distant from Dakar, that second-class passages for himself and wife would cost $64.00, and that in the condition his wife was in, I thought it would be imprudent   for them to leave the vessel until they could see the way clearer before them.

He left me for the vessel at Dakar somewhat depressed but still calm & dignified, while I consulted with the authorities to see if destitute passengers could be prevented from landing. I found that as there had been no precedent here the authorities were not decided, and learned soon after that the captain by assuming an attitude of sternness had got them to go ashore at Dakar, where they had taken refuge, in a cheap, dirty, over-crowded boarding house, the occupants of which were from the lowest class of vicious Italian peddlars.

I could stand this no longer, and going to the agent of the German Steamers with whom I was well acquainted, I found that on relating the principal parts of the story to him he was in full sympathy with me although a Catholic, and agreed on a reduction of a deck passage to Monrovia for Mr Arndt from 60 fcs to 40 fcs. For   Mrs Arndt, considering the condition she was in and what might happen on the voyage I did not ask for anything less than second-class, which cost 162.50. I then sent for them to leave the wretched den in which they had taken refuge from the vessel which brought them, and from which I knew they would be ejected the moment their money was finished, and come over to Goree which was to be the calling place of the steamer. They came to my house as directed, and as the steamer came along ahead of time they were not detained at Goree quite the whole of one day. The captain of the steamer received them very kindly on their introduction to him by the kind-hearted agent, and although only a deck passage at a reduced price had been paid for for Mr Arndt, the captain at once directed that they should make the passage together in a state-room without any reference to what had been paid for.

 

The Missionary, Mr Arndt, had frankly told me before I did anything for him that the Methodist Missionary Society was not responsible for his enterprise of trying to get to Liberia on his own resources, but I nevertheless thought it best to communicate with Bishop Harzell, whom I supposed to be still in Liberia, before writing the Government. He replied as follows. "I had no knowledge whatever of Mr Arndts coming to our work here, and at present have no funds to employ him * * * Hiss wife & he are at white plains near here. * * They have a little son. * * * They seem like good and reliable people".

Such is the way the case stands. The persons assisted were Americans, and without doubt in distress, but they were not "sailors". They resembled the man of sailors only in being imprudent. They were not even vicious like the most of sailors, but impressed an eminent discriminating Christian like Bishop Harzell, as being   good reliable people. They would very likely never have heard of me nor I of them if I had not been Consul, and so it is probably because I am a consul that I was singled out to help them. I could not help assisting them in the circumstances in which I found them placed because it was not in nature that I should let such people suffer when I had the means to help them. But as I have remarked, after all, they were not sailors, and I am therefore simply recounting the incident to the Department, without being certain as to whether anything can be done about it or not

I am Sir
Very truly Yours
Peter Strickland
U.S. Consul
