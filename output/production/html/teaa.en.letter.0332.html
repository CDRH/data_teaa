Civil Commandant's Office
Bulama 22nd March 1869

Sir,

On Saturday the 6th March I was waited on by David C. Miller late Master of the American barque "Gem" who informed me that his vessel had been lost on the reefs on the West side of the island of "Orange" on the morning of the 4th March that he with 3 of his Sailors had arrived at this port in a small boat and that he was desirous of making a declaration before me relative to the loss of his vessel. A copy of the declaration I herewith enclose marked A. Knowing that a brig called the "Potomac" belonging to the same owner as the "Gem" was at Bissão I dispatched the 3 Sailors to that place the same evening with the Hon:[?] Colonial Secretary a Freetown   a letter to the Governor of Bissão requesting that the 3 men might be allowed to go on board the "Potomac" lying in Portuguese Waters. This request was granted, but on the night of the 10th instant the three men were sent back to me accompanied with the enclosed note marked B from the Master of the "Potomac".

On the 8th instant the other boat and men mentioned in the declaration reached this port. In all the 2 boats contained 10 persons, of these 10 individuals I now send on to Freetown to be handed over to the American Consul by this Mail the undermentioned namely

1. W. H. Tracey 1st Officer
2. Alexander Foreman 2nd Officer
3. John Brown
4. Henry Clark
5. G. F. Linscoff
6. E. J. Jones 7. John McDonald }AB

The   The Steward by name John Lass has gone with Captain Brown of the "Potomac" and also a native who was engaged whilst on the rivers.

The late Master of the "Gem" is still here and intends returning to America by the first Steamer via Liverpool at his at his own expense.

The Crew saved their clothing - no lives were lost and the Ships papers were also saved.

The Charges incurred by the Colonial Government on behalf of these distressed seamen will be forwarded in my Vouchers by the next opportunity.

I have &c
Signed "J. Craig Loggie"
Acting Civil Commandant
Bulama

Civil Commandant's Office
Bulama, West Coast of Africa
6th March 1869

David C. Miller, late master of the American Barque "Gem" states -

On the morning of the 4th March I made the land coming from the Isles-de-Loss, which I afterwards found out to be that of the Island of "Orange" bearing E. N. E. I saw a line of breakers and then altered my course and stood to the southward and Eastward, at this time I was in to fathoms water, after altering and standing to the S. and E. I still found shoal Water until the Vessel struck in 3 fathoms, after she had struck I saw it was hopeless to think of getting her off as she continued to beat into one fathom. I then (ordered) had the 2 Boats ordered out, into which I put provisions and Water, the boat into which I got I had 3 men, the other boat under the Chief Officer had 5 men. The vessel struck about 9.30. a.m. and the weather was   was very thick - after we left the vessel we all saw that she was on fire. smoke coming out from about the Foremast and Kitchen - after we had placed about a 1/4 of a mile between the boats and the vessel, an explosion took place on board occasioned by the fire reaching the powder which was on board. I had I think about 3 Tons of Powder on board at the time of the fire, several explosions took place blowing the whole of the after part away, The main and mizen masts going at the came time. As it was out of all human power to do any good in staying, I gave the boats a course to steer by so that we could make the Island of Bulama, which I made about 3 o'clock on the afternoon of the 6th March 1869. The boat under charge of the Chief Officer has not yet come in, The last I saw of the Chief Officer's boat was about 6 P.M. on the 4th March 1869

Signed and Declared before the undersigned one of H. M. Justices of the Peace and Civil Commandant of Bulama by

Bissau March the 10th 1869

Commandant of Bulama

Sir, I beg to sent you back the tree seamen of the Barque "Gem" sent to me as the do not want to shign in the Potomac the want to go to Sierra Leone to the U.S. Counsel. the do not know what the want first the wanted to ship one Hour afterwards the oldest their mind it is a great truple and Humbuck[?] to me. I beg [?] waiting fore Capt Miller to comm over as he promist me. I dont know what hinder him from coming As it is of the greater importance fore him to come over to arrange his Account with me (otherwise) regarding what I beg receit from him and he from me otherwise he will not be able to settle, when he gets home until I get ther also. I sent you a Box containing 8 Bottles Limmon Syrup by my boat I also sent you a letter fore Mr Rosenbush U.S. Counsul at Sierra Leone Please Bee so Kind and send it by the Steamer.

I remain your obd Serv:
signed "Charles E. Brown"
