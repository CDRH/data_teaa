Department of State Received Jan 18 1881

No 38. U.S. Consulate at Sierra Leone Dec. 17th 1880.

Judson A. Lewis To Dept of State

Regarding Walter Duval a deserter from the American Bark "Jennie R. Deverty" - with deposition enclosed.

Also two sailers forwarded per F. I. Merryman to New York

 

No. 38. U.S. Consulate at
Sierra Leone Dec. 17th 1880.

Hon John Hay
Asst. Secretary State
Washington D.C.

Sir:

I have to inform you that Walter Duval, an able seaman - a deserter from the American Bark "Jennie R. Deverty" - came upon this Consulate from Edina or Grand Bassa per S.S. Lualaba Dec. 11th 1880. and made oath to the enclosed deposition. The said sailor was sent to this Consulate by Lt. A Williams U.S. Commercial Agent at Grand Bassa - with an order on me for his passage - which order amounting to £1-2-10 I paid.

This is the 4th man who has come upon this   Consulate from the said Bark "Jennie R. Deverty" - Weed Master from Boston

I cannot tell why the Master of the said "Deverty" or the U.S. Commercial Agent at Grand Bassa should allow so many sailors to desert and make no effort to prevent it and would respectfully recommend an investigation.

The said Jennie R. Deverty I believe is still on the Coast South of here and I do not know when she will sail for the United States The aforesaid Walter Duval will be sent to the Unite States by first opportunity.

I have also to inform the Dept. that I sent Frank Withers and Chas Worrell two destitute seamen from "Jennie R. Deverty" to New York Dec. 13th 1880.   Personally appeared before me at my office this day Walter Duval who makes oath as follows.

I shipped on or about the 29th of July at the Port of Boston Mass. as an able Seaman in the Bark Jennie R. Deverty - Weed Master for the West Coast of Africa The Bark first touched at St Vincent Cape De Verde Islands - where we remained for about three days and discharged 1400 ft of Lumber to the American Consul - thence to Monrovia where we arrived early in September - remained about two and a half days - sold considerable Rum at this place - business other Cargo - At Monrovia I refused duty because I had no place to sleep and had none during the passage and nothing fit to eat - all owing to the Cook - as there was plenty good provissions on board - but the Cook   Cook was a thoroughly a bad man - and the Captain believed in him - The Captain therefore made out in his official log - one whole page against me and charged me with mutiny. He read this charge to me in Grand Bassa Bay and went ashore in Edina and reported me and one other man and to the American Consul Mr H. A Williams Mr Williams sent a Sealed letter directing me to report to him on Shore - which I did - when I saw him I told the Consul who I was and he read to me the official Log - which statements I denied - I asked the Captain how he could make out a charge of mutiny for refusing to do duty - He replied for influencing the men - He could not sustain the charge for all the men testified that I had not in any way tried to influence them -

He then took up the Case of provissions   food from the Consul - being sick some of the time -

On the 9th Instant the Consul Mr Williams put me aboard the steamer Lualaba giving me a letter to the Captain also one for the Consul at Sierra Leone where I arrived Dec. 11th 1880.

Walter Duval

Sworn to before me at my Office Sierra Leone this 11th day of December 1880.

Judson A. Lewis
U.S. Consul
