Department of State Received Jun 12 1871

Edward J. Roye President of Liberia
To
His Excellency U S. Grant
President of the United States

Executive Mansion
Monrovia
31st March 1871

Great and Good Friend

You will please pardon my intrusion upon your time and patience I hope however you will consider the nature and importance of the subject which I wish to present for your consideration a sufficient apology.

My   My principal reason in writing you at this time is to present to you, as briefly as the as the nature and importance of the subject will admit the present condition of the Republic of Liberia, and what she needs and must have at the crisis, to enable her government to become established upon a permanent basis, and to allow her to fully develope her almost inexhaustible resources.

We have a country containing between six and seven hundred miles along the sea coast and extending far back into the interior. The civilized portion of the population is small only comprising about twenty or twenty five thousand. Of native Africans we have about five hundred thousand within the limits of the Republic, many of whom have made more or less progress towards civilization   The history of this Colony must be familiar to every intelligent Americans. Its rise progress and present state are well known to the statesmen of all countries.

For nearly fifty years, we have been struggling to establish a free government and to build up Republican Institutions, in the land of our forefathers. How far we have been successful, we must leave the civilized world to judge. We have done all that we could with the limited means at our command to accomplish the great object of our mission here.

The first emmigrants arrived on these shores comparatively poor   and ignorant having been born and reared amid the institutions of slavery where they had but few opportunities for mental improvement and for the accumulation of wealth

These colonies were originally established under the auspices of the American Colonization Society. Since then they have been united under one Government called "The Republic of Liberia"

But our Government is, like all other new governments poor and is therefore unable without aid   from other sources to progress as rapidly as the interests of the people and the progress of the age require.

This government even with its hands tied by the want of adequate means and heavily oppressed by poverty has still done much to commend itself favorably to the consideration of the civilized world. It has done that which for many years baffled the united efforts of America and England; viz: it has demolished the slave factories on the land and broken up the slave trade on the sea-coast, within the jurisdiction of this Government.

It may be deemed [?] in me to add that Liberia possesses vast[?]   and almost unlimited resources. if her government and people only had the means necessary to fully develope them. Her valleys and hills, forests and fields abound with the choicest products of the earth. Our forests are filled with the most valuable timber, dyewoods, and gums. We possess a soil of unsurpassed fertility suitable for the growth of everything that will thrive written the tropics.

All we need is the necessary means to open roads, build bridges &c, that ere may be enabled to cultivate and improve our lands and bring our products to market. To establish a system of internal improvement; and facilitate our progress in agriculture, we need labor saving   saving machines. For the moral and mental improvement of the Americo Liberians and also the native tribes around us, we need money. Without money the resources of Liberia cannot be developed, our vast illimitable fields cannot be cultivated, and without this material aid schools and churches cannot be established among the native population. Unless we can get money the wheels of government must be stopped or move on with very slow progress.

In order then that we may rise rapidly from our infant state towards national manhood, and to enable us to keep pace with the progress of the age, to prevent the great experiment of founding a government of colored men on the soil of Africa, from   from proving to be a disgraceful failure, we are compelled to solicit pecuniary aid from the friends of human progress and few institutions wherever they may be found.

While it is true that Africa is our fatherland; yet we regard America as our mother country and we sincerely believe that her government and people feel an interest in our welfare, and the success of our undertaking.

For this reason we make our appeal to the government and people of the United States, or through such other persons as you may deem proper, to negotiate a loan of five hundred thousand dollars in coin   in coin [?] of the Principal and Interest [?] we will give Government Bonds and [?] of the Republic. I am authorized by an Act of the Legislature to borrow between three and five hundred dollars. A Copy of the Act is herewith enclosed. Any statistics that you may wish to see concerning the present Revenue of Liberia, I will be pleased to furnish you at any time.

Should we be enabled to effects this loan, we desire that the cost of the munitions of war with the Interest thereon and the amount due for the "Rescue" including the cost of sending her out, shall be deducted from the amount loaned. Our   Our Chargè d'Affaires is fully authorised to negotiate with the United States Government.

From the very pleasant and agreeable interview that I had with you during my recent visit to the United States, I am strongly induced to believe that you will feel so great an interest in the rapid progress of the only Republican Government of colored men on the West Coast of Africa, that you will do everything in your power to secure this loan for the benefit of Liberia - and thus cause it to be recorded upon the page of impartial history, another substantial proof of the fact:

That the great Republic of the   United States is a true friend of the Negro Race

It is my duty to inform your Excellency that James E. Moore Esqr has so acted against the government that he is objectionably holding the Office of U.S. Vice-Minister Resident near this Government.

With feelings of the most profound respect.

I remain
Yours truly, E. J. Roye
