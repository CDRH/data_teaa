No. 31. U.S. Consulate at Sierra Leone

Judson A. Lewis To Dept. State

Peace Meeting at Sierra Leone. Settlement of the Quiah war and signing articles of Peace.

 
No 31.

U.S. Consulate at Sierra Leone Sept 30th 1880.

Hon. John Hay
Ass't Secretary State
Washington D.C.

Sir:

I have the honor to report to the Department regarding a great meeting between the Sierra Leone Government and the Queens, Kings, and Chiefs of the Timneh Country and their followers, held at Government House on the 17th Instant, to conclude and sign a Treaty of Peace, which by the mediation of some of them, His Excellency the Administrator in Chief had undertaken to effect among them all, Chiefly for the settlement of the Quiah War and also to secure from future disturbance by native wars, the territories adjacent and forming the highway to Sierra   Sierra Leone for the trade of the interior.

A preliminary meeting had been held at the Court Hall at which the terms of the Treaty had been fully discussed by the Chiefs among themselves, so that the work of Friday afternoon, consisted in the final act of signing, after due explanation of the terms of the Treaty already agreed to.

The meeting at the Government House which was held on the Eastern Lawn was attended by about three hundred (300) Chiefs and followers. His Excellency the Administrator-in chief and Staff together with Officers of the Regiment and invited officials to the number of about Seventy five (75) were in attendance in uniform or full evening dress, some of whose names appear below as having witnessed the ceremony of the signing of the Treaty of Peace

 

The meeting which was one of the largest of the kind every witnessed in Sierra Leone, was very imposing. The Band of the 1st West India Regt. was present, and a guard of honor of about two hundred (200) troops of the same Regiment, carrying their colors, arms with fixed bayonets were so placed upon the [?] esplanaid on the north side, that[?] they not only added effect to the scene but must have impressed the assembled chiefs, that with the ready disposition of British power to seek for and make peace, its capacity for war was also unbounded.

The meeting having been opened[?] His Excellency the Administrator-in Chief made to the Queens, Kings, Chiefs and their followers an address which was interpreted to them in the Timneh language by Mr. T. G. Lawson, Government Interpreter.

His Excellency in addressing them said, that he had very little to   to say to them as he had already had two interviews, with them and he had heard what they had said in the Court Hall.

He had seen Lahie Bundoo and Gbannah Sehrey shake hands and had heard a universal desire expressed for peace.

They had asked him to make an agreement, which they should all sign before him, so as to peace on record this desire. This he had done and they had already had it read and explained to them, and they said it was the expression of their desire wish. He said he had asked a great many officers and gentlemen to be present at the signing of this agreement and if any of themselves should forget what they signed that day, they might be sure that he and his officers and friends would not do so.

He believed from what he had heard from them, that they had not come   come for play, but were sincere in their desire for peace; and if this was so they and their children after them would be glad they had signed that instrument, but it would be an [?] day for any one who made light of it. They saw the soldiers that were present, but the officer who was sitting by him, had many more up the hill and at other places on the coast. They had been to Quiah: but he trusted they would never go there again in that way. The wish of the British was to see peace and to teach the natives how to become prosperous.

His Excellency asked Bey Mauro King of the North Bullom, how many people they met at Benkia, when he accompanied them there some time ago, during the war, and he replied none. he then asked how many were there now, and he replied plenty, the country was peopled again.

 

His Excellency said this was the effect of Peace and that if they continued peaceful, their country would soon become rich, through the security which their crop and all their property would enjoy. He expressed the hope, that he would shortly have the pleasure of seeing their people, again in Sierra Leone not as now, to be talking of war, or even to be making peace, but to be trading together and enjoying the fruits of the present peace, which he trusted would be permanent.

At the request of His Excellency the Colonial Secretary read and Mr Lawson interpreted the Treaty which is written below. All the Chiefs having expressed that they understood and agreed to the terms thereof the Treaty was signed and witnessed.

The following is the Treaty

 

Treaty.

We the undersigned Queens, Kings and Chiefs of our respective countries, being met together at Government House Sierra Leone, desire to record and do hereby mutually agree as follows:

Whereas wars have for some time since been raging, in the Quiah Country, whereby said country has been impoverished, and the inhabitants driven away: and Whereas, such wars have been mainly carried on by or at the instigation of Gbannah Sehrey a chief under Bome Rufa and Bome Warrah, Queens of the Quiah Country in disobedience to the command of such Queens: and whereas Lahai Bundoo another chief under the said Queens, has also recently been engaged in war with the said Gabannah Sehrey whereby the Quiah Country has been devastated: and whereas the   the said Gabannah Sehrey and Lahai Bundoo, have expressed their sincere regret, for their past conduct, and the said Gbannah Sehrey has particularly inticated the Administrator in Chief of the West Africa Settlements to convey to her Most Gracious Majesty Queen Victoria his penitence for having insulted His Excellency Sir Samuel Rowe K.C.M.G. Governor in Chief of the West Africa Settlements by attacking Bey Sherboro King of Yonnie and his Chiefs and attendants, when on their way to a peaceful mission to the said Governor-in-Chief; and the said Lahai Bundoo has begged to be forgiven, for renewing disturbance in the Quiah Country, after His Excellency the Governor in Chief had at great expense, and personal exertion left such Country in quiet: and

Whereas Seckoo Camarah,   Alikarlie of Port Lokkoh and Boccary Bombolie are alleged to have encouraged Gabannah Sehrey in making war and thus disturbing the peace of the country: and whereas great damage to life and property has been occasioned by the late and previous wars: and whereas the Administrator in Chief of the West Africa Settlements has been interceeded by Bey Mauro King of North Bullom and Allimamy[?] Sannassi Chief of Medina, Bullom Shose as mediators on behalf of the said Lahai Bundoo, Gabannah Sehrey, Seckoo Camarah and Boccary Bombalito allow them to make peace in the presence of himself, as the present representative in Sierra Leone of Her Most Gracious Majesty Queen Victoria and in the presence of the various officers and persons whose names are attached as witnesses hereto and to place on record in the most solemn manner their promise to   to keep the peace forever hereafter towards one another and all other peaceful chiefs and people: and whereas the various other Queens, Kings, and Chiefs signing this agreement have expressed their earnest wish to take the present opportunity of entering into an agreement for peace and also for submission of any disputes which may hereafter arise between them to the decission of the Governor-in-Chief of the West Africa Settlements.

Now being convinced that no country can be prosperous, whose inhabitants, can not enjoy in security the fruits of their labor we wish to take such measures as shall secure amongst us a lasting peace, feeling that as people of one country nation many of whom are joined together by ties of marriage and blood relationship, it is fitting that we should live together in amity, our interest being the same   same, and having no real hatred one against another, knowing also that if we can succeed in subduing these tribal feuds in which our children are taken prisoners in large numbers and sold as slaves, to a slavery from which many of their never return, we shall be stronger as a united people, and better able to progress in civilization and wealth.

With a view to procure this result we the undersigned Queens, Kings Chiefs, and people and people who have set our names to this agreement, hereby make peace and solemnly promise, for ourselves and our successors to keep the same, and to submit such disputes, as may hereafter arise between us and them, and as can not be settled by our general assembly, to the arbitration of the Governor of Sierra Leone or of such fitting   officer as shall be by him appointed to undertake that duty, if the Governor will consent to act in the matter, and we promise that we will abide by his award or that of the officer appointed by him.

We pledge ourselves that should any of us, refuse to abide by his decission or that of the officer appointed by him, and have recourse to war in opposition to it. That we will uphold the decission of His Excellency the Governor or of such officer appointed by him to act for him and that we will consider any person refusing to abide by such decission, to be a common enemy to the whole of us, and we will if requested by the Governor of Sierra Leone hand over any such person to him in order that he may be removed from our country and be dealt with by the Governor   by imprisonment or otherwise in any such way as shall seem to him to be good.

We request the Governor to take this responsibility and thus aid us in maintaining peace in our country; for although we know we have nothing to give in return for this, yet we feel from the interest that Her Most Gracious Majesty the Queen of England has been graciously pleased to take in our welfare, and from the council given to us by Her officer, that the Governor will still be willing to help us in this which we believe is the right way to ensure peace among us.

Signed by

Alimamy Lahie Bundoo. principal Chief of Fourodugu in Quiah. Gabannah Sehrey, a chief of Magbainie in Quiah: Seckoo Camarah, the Arlikarlie[?] of Port Lokkoh: Boccary Bombolie, Chief of Bankah[?]-Loll in Quiah   Bome Rufah, and Bome Warrah, Queens of Quiah: Bey Mauro, King of the North Bullom or Lokkoh Marsamma: Alimamy Tenda Samassi Modoo, King of Medina Bullom Shore: Canray Mahoi, Chief of Riblue, Sherbro: Santiggy Nonkoh Lahai, and Santiggy Ansum abah Yeamy[?], principle chiefs in Port Lokkoh: Sorie Camara, a young chief in Port Lokkoh: Santiggy Dibluath, a Chief of Port Lokkoh: Malliggy Langley a Headman in Port Lokkoh: Lamah, Husband of Bome Rufah: Pa King, Ansumammah[?] Quiah, Saddoo Bonkay, Santiggy Malonkoh, Foda Camarah, Chiefs of Quiah Santiggy Momoh, Sulimamy Bundoo, Chiefs of Quiah, Fourah Bundoo brother of Alimamy Lahai Bundoo, Thompson brother of Bey Mauro, a chief of North Bullom Santiggy Cabbah, Ansumanah Foray Chiefs of Bullom,   Musa, Chief of Medina, Hannal Modoo brother of Alimamy Sannassi Foday Yanub a chief with[?] Allimamy Sannassi, Canray Lenkeh, and Tuah Bundoo Chiefs in Riblie: Boccary Sehrey cousin of Gbannah Sehrey; Sogo Fellah sister of Gbannah Sehrey.

Signed at Government House Sierra Leone this 17th day of September 1880, by the above mentioned, Queens, Kings, Chiefs and Headmen.

It is very important to the trade prospects of Sierra Leone, that all the various native tribes should be at peace so as to allow one another to pass back and forth; bringing produce to Sierra Leone and taking Merchandise in exchange, so whenever anything can be done to secure this result, it is quickly performed by the Sierra Leone Government and Merchants.
