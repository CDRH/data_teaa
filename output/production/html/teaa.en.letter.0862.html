Department of State Received Dec 2 1880

No 35. U.S. Consulate at Sierra Leon Oct 27th 1880.

Judson A. Lewis - To Dept of State

Distressed Seamen.

 

No. 35. U.S. Consulate at
Sierra Leon Oct. 27th 1880.

Hon John Hay
Asst Secty of State
Washington D.C.

Sir:

I have to inform you that on the 25th inst. Daniel Freethey late 2nd Mate and Frank Withers and Chas Worrell Able Seamen Deserters from the American Bark Jennie R. Deverty[?] came upon this Consulate per S.S. Ethopia from Grand Bassa Liberia - as destitute American Seamen - and made oath to Statements inclosed.

You will observe by the statements that neither the Master of the Bark nor the Commercial Agent at Grand Bassa took proper measures to present said dersertions but on the other hand rather encouraged the same.

 

The second mate paid his own fare to Sierra Leone and the Commercial Agent at Grand Bassa gave the Steamship Co. an order on me for the fare of the two Able Seamen for £2-11-8 which amount I have paid.

They will be returned to the United States by the earliest opportunity

I remain
Your Obedient Servant
Judson A. Lewis
U.S. Consul.

(Inclosures).
1. Statement of Daniel Freethey
2. Statement of Frank Withers and Chas Worrell

No 1 Inclosure

U.S. Consulate at
Sierra Leon Oct. 25th 1880.

Personally appeared before me at my office this d ay Daniel Freethey who takes Oath as follows viz: I shipped as 2nd Mate of The American Bark Jennie R. Duvrty[?] Weed Master at the Port of Boston on or about July 31st 1880. for the West Coast of Africa The Bark touched at Cape St Vincent Cape De Verde Islands thence to Monrovia Liberia where we arrived on or about Sept 10th thence to Grand Bassa and Cape Palmas.

At the latter place the Captain being on shore one night attending to business the Mate Harry Miller being in command had plenty of liquor which he drank & gave the men he went forward, drank sang & danced with the men in the forecastle until I finally succeeded in getting from aft into the cabin - when shortly he became crazy - tore every particle of clothing off broke glass ware, clocks, overturned various articles in the cabin and had to be carefully watched for 3 or 4 hours until he finally became exhausted and sank on the floor of the cabin - perfectly nude This took place in the night with a heavy wind blowing and only one anchor down - & I alone the Steward half drunk - not knowing when I would be obliged to go forward & let go the other anchor - fearing also to cease guard over the mate in the cabin for fear he might get over board or do some damage Several Sailors also drunk from Liquor furnished them by the Mate and [?] disorder   prevailed - About Six days after this a similar occurence took place - finally I told the Captain all that had taken place and told him I wanted to leave the vessel. The Captain said he could not discharge me and if I left the vessel he must not know anything about it - but afterward he took me aboard the S.S. Ethiopia in his own boast at Cape Palmas Oct 21st and bid me good bye. I paid my own fare to Sierra Leone

Daniel Freethey

U.S. Consulate at
Sierra Leone Oct 25th 1880.

Sworn to me before me this day

Judson A. Lewis
U.S. Consul

 

Inclosure

No. 2.

U.S. Consulate at
Sierra Leone Oct. 25th 1880.

Personally appeared before me at my office on the day above written Frank Withers and Chas Worrell and made oath to the following viz:

We shipped on board the American Bark "Jennie R. Deverty" as Able Seamen at the Port of Boston U.S.A. July 31st 1880. bound for the West Coast of Africa Weed Master.

Arrived at Monrovia Liberia (having touched at St. Vincent Cape De Verde Islands) on or about Sept 10th thence to Grand Bassa and Cape Palmas.

The food furnished us was not properly cooked after me left St. Vincent as the Steward was nearly all the time under the influence of Liquor as a large portion of the Cargo consisted of Rum we informed the Captain that the food was very bad - not properly cooked & he said he would attend to it - but was overheard to tell the Steward - the men want their food cooked better but you can do as you are a mind to.

Withers was Sick two weeks before he left the vessel - & when he got better he told the Captain he could not stand it & must go ashore and the Captain & 1st Mate Harry Miller took ashore at Grand Bassa Oct. 5th and there we saw the U.S. Commercial Agent Mr Williams who advised us to return to the Bark - but we replied we would rather die on shore than go back aboard the vessel. We remained ashore and saw the Captain frequently and neither he nor the Commercial Agent Mr Williams took any positive measures - to compel us to return to the vessel and we remained ashore until the arrival of the S.S. Ethiopia Oct. 23rd when the Commercial Agent Mr Williams procured our passage to Sierra Leone

Frank Withers
Chas Worrell[?]

U.S. Consulate at
Sierra Leone Oct 25th 1880.

Sworn to before me this day at my office

Judson A. Lewis
U.S. Consul
