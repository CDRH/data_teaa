Subject.

Disposition of prisoners of war, by the Liberian Government

Synopsis.

Children taken from parents and placed in Mission Schools and private families. Adult male persons banished to distant parts and hired out.

 

No. 10 Consular Series.
Consulate General of the
United States, Monrovia
Liberia, Oct. 29, 1898.

Hon. John B. Moore,
Assistant Secretary of State,
Washington.

Sir:

I have the honor to transmit to you the intelligence, that as a partial result of the continued Tribal war, between the Government of the Republic of Liberia, and the native Golah Tribe of the aboriginal, referred to in my Diplomatic Dispatches, #6 of May 26, #20 of Aug., 9, and #29 of Oct. 22, 1898, that a few days since, His Excellency, the President of Liberia, brought down 50 small native boys and girls, ranging, probably   from 3 to 10 years of age; which he had captured, in war, from the hostile Tribe. These children were brought to Monrovia and placed in the Mission Schools or distributed among the Liberian families, to be taught and trained into civilized habits.

On the 27th instant, a squad of 49 male prisoners, of war, accompanied by His Excellency, the President, of Liberia, was brought down to the city and placed under guard, upon the Liberian gunboat, Rocktown to await the arrival of a German Streamer, en route, for south Africa; upon which the said prisoners, of war, are to be transported to the German settlement of the Camaroons, to be hired to the German settlers of the Camaroon   Camaroons, for a stipulated amount to be paid to the Liberian Government.

I have the honor to be your obedient servant;
Owen L. W. Smith.
