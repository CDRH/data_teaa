No. 70.

Consulate of the United States,
Goree-Dakar, October 9, 1887

Honorable James D. Porter
Assistant Secretary of State
Washington, D.C.

Sir,

I have the honor to here-inclose copy of a letter by the Honorable Charles H. J. Taylor which reached me at 9.30 A.M. yesterday from the British S. S. "Fez" anchored off Dakar. This steamer arrived at Goree the morning of the 7th at about 8 o'clock from Monrovia, Liberia; obtained permission to go alongside the hulk coal-hulk which is anchored on the side of the bay nearest to Dakar; had a visa attached to her bill of health; and continued on to the hulk while taking her coal, the health-boat came alongside (neither the Medical Officer nor Harbor-master appearing) and the negroes in her asked for the bill of Health which on being given to them they carried on shore. From that moment the "Fez" was subjected to many annoyances according to all on board and some of the procedures   and non-procedures of the Authorities were serious, more especially as she had the Belgian mail on board. Our Honorable Minister of course suffered these trials with the rest and I at once repaired alongside where I had the pleasure of seeing and speaking with him and of hearing the Captain's story which was corroborated by all. I could not go on board for she was in quarantine, although there was no Guard there from the shore as is usual.

The Captain, Augustus Kent, appeared to me to be a thorough gentleman and as his story coincides with that inclosed I will not use time to repeat it for the mail leaves in half an hour. He sent on shore four times for his bill of health in order to continue on his voyage but it was each time refused (this is denied on shore) Neither was the vessel formally declared in quarantine (also denied) This fact produced an unfortunate occurence There is at this season for arrivals from the south a quarantine of five days. Two   French passengers, unaware of the fact, went on shore and all over the town where a fever of excitement was produced as it was claimed that the steamer had wilfully violated the quarantine regulations and the affair was laid before the State prosecution.

To make matters worse, the British Vice Consul, who, by the by, is a Frenchman, and despises the English as much as any one else here instead of trying to arrange the business did everything to complicate matters and to discomfit the Britishers. He had already made up his mind before having even spoken with the captain.

Therefore, to assist Mr. Taylor, I availed myself of the good relations which I have always been fortunate enough to keep with the authorities and aid all in my power without interfering in British matters and the bill of health was finally given up to the ship. There are a variety of lesser charges each with two sides   sides, but it would be useless to speak of them all as none are proven. What I have written as far as I am able to know as this moment I believe to be correct. No doubt that some part of the difficulties were caused by misunderstandings bu they were just the same unfortunate.

The "Fez" left for Teneriffe last night at nine o'clock with Mr. Taylor in good health. All claims against the vessel captain & owners will be settled in Europe.

With an apology for this hastily written dispatch,
I am, Sir, with esteem
Your obedient servant
George Strickland
Vice-Consul

 
No 1.

Off Goree
S.S. Fez.

Hon. Peter Strickland U.S. Consul for Goree and Dakaar

Sir:

I have the honor to inform you, that I am on board the S.S. "Fez" delayed by a procedure which in my opinion is wholly illegal and without precedent, unless said precedent has been laid at this place. On Oct 7th 1887 at 8.20 A.M. we arrived off Goree, our bill of Health was sent ashore and was [unclear, one word] and returned, Capt Kent, who is in command of this steamer being out of coal and desiring sufficient to arrive at Teneriffe got permission from Goree to go alongside the "Hulk" the same being a coal vessel, in the Stream off Dekar. The Capt. proceeded to load twenty tons of coal the amount necessary, to bring us to Teneriffe. A lot of negroes came alongside and asked for papers, the captain believing   there would be no difficulty sent the "papers" when lo and behold, that whilst we were allowed to pass Goree, coming 2 miles further caused the unfortunate predicament upon us, of meeting a little French Doctor, who pretends by word of mouth from 3rd parties to have us under quarantine for (5) five days. To me this appears very outrageous. We got no notice of said quarantine until the hour of 8 P.M. of the 7th inst. The Doctor who institutes[?] the quarantine has never been alongside the vessel to take observations in refference to our healthful condition we have no communication whatever with the land, we cannot send telegram, get provisions, receive more than 200 pieces of washing which was left at Dekar nor nothing else. We have been subjected to nothing but insults. If we starved and famished for   food and water, it would excite no aid or even pity from the Dekarites. But this is not all & as the Consul General and Minister Resident of our liberal Government, to foreigners, the United States, am on leave of absence from my post Monrovia Liberia, making my way to Washington D.C. Doubly[?] armed with "passport official" and behind me the International Law, which presents to me perfect immunity, from molestation of any kind. I am told by a little French Commissionair of Police, that he cares not who I am, I shall have no communication with land. I shall be extremely obliged to call upon you to intercede at once in Dekar on my behalf. You will please do me the kindness to come aboard, that I may see you and fully give information, and take such other steps, as may secure   secure my rights, yours and our successor.

I am in haste to arrive in the states, hoping and trusting that you may at once take immediate "steps" in this matter, I am your obedient servant

Chas. H. J. Taylor
U.S. Minister Resident and Consul General to Liberia
