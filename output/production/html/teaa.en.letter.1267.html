Hon. David J. Hill,
Assistant Secretary of State,
Washington.

Sir:

I have to say, that upon receipt of your No. 15, "Consular," of Feb'y 8, 1899, through the third Assistant Secretary; with the inclosure of a letter from Wm. R. Morris, Esq., the guardian of the indigent Todd children here, I had decided to say nothing further, relative to the matter; but as the Secretary of the Legation, here, in overhauling the papers and files, in his office, has just come across, what seems to me, to   to be, a very important letter, relative to the Todd estate, which upon its face, seems to contradict, in part, the statement of Mr. Morris, relative to the sustenance of the children, if at home; and would probably have the appearance that he does not desire the presence of the children in Minneappolis, in order, that the property may be otherwise disposed of. For this reason, I thought it my duty to inclose the said letter of Gale & Co. of June 12, 1894, with one from Wm. R. Morris Esq., of Nov. 1, 1895, which I found in my office, on taking charge, for your information. I have the honor to be your obedient servant; Owen L. W. Smith
C. General

Consular Bureau. July 29 1899
Consular Bureau. Memorandum.

Mr. Cridler:

On February 3, 1899, William R. Morris informed the Department that there were no funds available to bring the Todd children to this country and that an effort to raise the money had resulted in failure. Morris is the guardian of the children's property. We sent the letter to the Consul General who replied under date of May 18, 1899, enclosing a letter from Messrs Gale & Co., dated June 12, 1894, who stated that the father of the children had died and left them a home valued at $3,000 to $4,000. A letter from William R. Morris, dated November 1, 1895, was also enclosed. Morris had at that time been appointed guardian of the children. He stated that they were trying to raise money to bring the children to the United States; that the personal property left   by their father amounted to but few dollars; and that the real estate could not be sold until the youngest child became of age; that it might with the consent of the court be mortgaged for $600 or $700, but there would be no way of paying the mortgage off when due, etc. We sent a copy of this correspondence to Gale and Company on June 29, 1899, and Mr. Edward C. Gale now replies that Morris is still guardian of the children; that on account of a shrinkage of value the property is worth little more than $2,000; that the only way to raise money would be by mortgage; that the children if here would have to be supported by charity after the money raised by the mortgage was exhausted; and that there is now $124 in cash in the hands of the guardian over and above the   taxes, etc., which have all been paid.

I fail to see what we can do in the matter except to copy Mr. Gale's letter to the Consul General. The property is in the hands of a state court, and will be disposed of in such manner as that court may direct; and we have no funds at our disposal with which to bring the children to this country.

W. J. C.

Minneapolis, June 12, 1894.

United States Consul,
Monrovia, Liberia,
West Africa;

Dear Sir:-

Some time in the autumn of '93, Mr. Julius C. Todd, a worthy colored man long a resident of this city, left here with his family with the avowed purpose of joining some Christian mission in Africa. We do not understand that he went out under the auspices of any society, but at his own expense. A Philadelphia paper published by the colored Methodists on April 19 published a letter from a missionary stating that word had been received from Brother Johnson of Monrovia that Mr. Todd and wife had both died in or near Monrovia soon after reaching that country. If true, their deaths must have taken place some time in February or March of this year. When they left here they had five children. All sorts of rumors are afloat among their acquaintances as to their condition, but no direct word has been received from any one. Mr. Todd left a comfortable home here valued at $3000. to $4000, and before leaving leased it for five years at $15. per month, and left it in our care to collect rents, pay taxes, etc. We have heard nothing directly from him, and nothing indirectly, except as above, since he left New York. In order that we may protect the interests of his children, look after his estate, etc., will you kindly make diligent inquiry as to the facts in this case? If you find that both are dead, it will be necessary to have an affidavit as to the time and place of death of both Mr. and Mrs. Todd; also, any information as to the present condition and whereabouts of the children will be very important for us to have. Awaiting your early reply,

Very truly yours,
Gale & Co.

  Attorney and Counsellor at Law,
927 Guaranty Loan Building.

In re Estate of J. C. Todd, deceased

Minneapolis, Minn., U.S.A. Nov. 1st 1895.

Mr. A. D.[?] Williams, Guardian Todd children,
Monrovia, Liberia, Africa.

Sir:-

Your communication of Sept. 16th 1895 to Edward C. Gale Esq. is before me, and the contents have been carefully noted. It is the desire of the acquaintances of Mr. Todd that his children should be brought to this city reared and educated. Were they here homes could be provided for them and they be suitably educated. The estate has been settled; there is no money left and nothing that can be turned into money that could be used for their nurture. We are now doing our utmost to raise money enough to pay the passage of the children to this land; there are several out with subscription lists for this purpose. At present I am unable to say whether or not we will be successful. No compensation can be given any one for the care of the children as there is none to be derived from the estate that can be used for this purpose. The final report of the administrator shows in personal property the sum of $15 56/100 giving the sum of $3 89/100 to each child, but ten dollars of this amount is in the shape of a worthless promissory note, thus leaving the sum of $5 56/100 to be divided among the children. There is a house and lot left to them but each having   an undivided one fourth interest in the same. This cannot as the matter now stands be converted into money nor could it be until the youngest child becomes of age. The property was leased by Mr. Todd for a period of five years at a rental of twelve dollars per month making a yearly rental of $144 00/100 deducting commissions we have a balance of $129.60, out of this. The sum of $75 00/100 more or less must be deducted for taxes and there is left the sum of fifty or sixty dollars to be divided yearly amongst the children. If the property were mortgaged, if the court permitted this, not more than the sum of six or seven hundred dollars could be obtained on it and as there would be no money to pay off the mortgage when due and no certainty of keeping the interest paid up it would result in the loss of the property and the children would derive very little benefit therefrom. There are friends of Mr. Todd here who would take the children and rear them without charge if they were here. Mrs. Sykes is not a guardian for the children. I am the guardian appointed by the Probate court of this Hennepin County, Minnesota. When Mr. Todd left here he took with him considerable personal property and quite a sum of money. Were there any personal effects left by either Mr. or Mrs. Todd? If so in whose possession are they? I would suggest that if we are able to raise the money for the payment of the passage of the children to this country that they be sent here and that whatever personal effects there are left in your country go toward compensating those who have so kindly cared for the children since the death of their parents.

The youngest child is how old now?

Trusting the children are in good health I am most respectfully yours,
Wm. R. Morris,
Guardian of the Todd children.

I would be pleased to hear from you at your earliest convenience.
Wm R. M.
