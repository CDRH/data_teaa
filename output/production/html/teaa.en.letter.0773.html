Department of State Received Mar 2 1891
Number 128

U.S. Consulate, Goree-Dakar
January 31st, 1891

Mr Peter Strickland
To the Department of State

Subject

Imbrobability of being able to find a suitable person to serve as Vice-Consul or Deputy at this Consulate on the present "Salary by Fees":—Also.— The inability of the Consul to at present supplement his "Salary by Fees" by doing a paying Commission business: this being caused principally by an increase of duties here on foreign merchandise which came into effect at the commencement of this month: these and other considerations herein mentioned being respectfully urged as reasons why this Consulate should be put at once in a salary basis.

 
Number 128

U.S. Consulate, Goree-Dakar
January 31st 1891

To the Honorable
William F Wharton
Assistant Secretary of State

Sir

In my Dispatch to the Department No 123, and also in some of my other Dispatches. I have had the honor to suggest that the Fees collected at this Consulate afford but a meagre compensation for the services which actually have to be rendered, and as experience has proved, they do not make it possible that the Consular business can be conducted uninterruptedly and efficiently, as would seem to be desirable in a colony which in so short a time has become so important as this.

Twenty-five years since there was not a single steamer trading here, and a small sailing vessel required more than three weeks to discharge and load. Now there are four regular lines of steamers from France, three from England, two from Germany, and a multitude belonging to French   mercantile firms which are nearly regular on their trips, besides the usual complement of transients and sailing vessels. Altogether, I judge we are now having not less than fifty steamers a month some of which exceed 6000 tons in size, their equipments being of the finest description.

But twice now within two years I have been obliged to close the Consulate for months, because for the present allowance "Salary by Fees" not even a foreigner could be found willing to serve as Vice-Consul and be subject to the Regulations which our service imposes. Several American Vessels "arrived and departed" during my enforced absence one of which having cargo damaged needed very important Consular service but there was no one on hand to render them. Even now that the Consul is open for business, and four American vessels are known to be on their way here[?] there is neither Vice-Consul nor Deputy to serve in case the Consul from sickness or any other cause should not be able This seems a state of affairs which call for a sufficient remedy.

 

But there are reasons more potent perhaps than the foregoing why something should be done immediately for this Consulate.

Hitherto I have occasionally pointed out the growing importance of the trade relations between the manufacturers and shippers of the United States and the millions of appreciative consumers who inhabit this country. with a suggestion now and then that it might be well for all interested in increase the importance of this Consulate in at least a corresponding ratio. But until no I have been under no necessity of regarding the Consulate as a Scource of income, and perhaps I never should if the French Government had not lately adopted measures which will give American trade here for the moment a set-back, and stop me I am afraid from prosecuting with advantage my commission business. I allude to the augmentation of the duties here on the Goods of foreigners, which will be mentioned more at length in another Dispatch.

These duties if continued will check importations from foreign countries   considerably, and should the same policy be pursued by the several European nations among which the greater part of Africa is now divided up American Commerce on this Continent will not have so promising a future as it otherwise might have. It would seem then that this state of affairs should be closely watched, so that instead of diminishing the importance of the Consulate, these very measures, if persisted in, will all the more call for its continuance, and by some one[?] capable of making the requisite observations.

But if the Consulate affords no adequate salary how can this long be done? With no business to depend on for an income, a family at home and not fees enough from the diminished number of vessels which will now perhaps be coming here to pay my rent, there can be but one course for me to pursue in case I do not get timely assistance. I shall be compelled to close up and go home.

If the Government however can interfere in season with a salary to prevent this   I suppose I can say without egotism that if education and experience avail anything I ought not to be ill-qualified for a more advanced Consular position. I have served here now for more than seven years with scarce a criticism from any quarter against my work. I am in good health, thoroughly acclimated, and know all the principal merchants and of their wants in the American line of trade between the Great Desert and Sierra Leone, a distance of more than 600 miles. Hitherto I have not done much in the way of giving in reports which might be useful and interesting to Americans, because, without a salary and being occupied with the care of a large mercantile business, I could not compass means to attend to it. But with suitable compensation from the Government, I could now give information which would be of the utmost value to all desiring an outlet for Goods this way, and communicate with such specially when they desired it. It will be understood that the new duties cannot very long check here   the importation of such American staples as Lumber, Petroleum, and Tobacco because these and many other articles which Americans have cannot be furnished by France. Also, those Articles in the manufacture of which Americans greatly excel, it is not to be supposed that the present discriminating duty of seven per cent will prevent them from being imported. But American manufacture will require information on this and kindred subjects which is at once accurate and practical, and such information if situated so I could I should always be able and glad to furnish.

Hitherto in making suggestions for increased compensation I have only named for a salary the highest sum which would allow the consul to [?] business. But in view of the explanations which have been made it may rightfully enough be thought best to place the Consulate on a basis which will enable the Consul respectably and without such extra assistance to devalue[?] his whole time to the interests depending on the consulate. Almost   every mail brings me communications from America like the one enclosed, and if employed in business other than Consular I could not of course give them the attention they seem to deserve. Large quantities of American lard have been sold where by American firms which ave run sailing vessels in this direction, but this method of doing business seems to be more and more going out of use, and how can parties like the one mentioned in the enclosure get the information they want unless it is furnished by their Consuls?

Consulates at points like this are it is true a tax to the Government, but if conducted in a manner to furnish outlets for large quantities of American Goods, they thereby make possible the imports from which the Government gains its principal pecuniary support.

In a place like this which furnishes neither unofficial fees nor other perquisites and where rents and the cost of living are dear, the largest salary which allows to do business   would better compensate a Consul than anything less than $2500 with business interdicted.

I hope my somewhat extended remarks on the subject I am presenting will not be deemed amiss by the Department, as it seems necessary that the situation now existing should be stated somewhat fully as well as frankly and fairly in order to be duly appreciated. With the facts thus furnished it will be the province of the Government to decide what under the circumstances should be done, and my suggestions are only intended to assist in making up the judgment, the same as the testimony of an eye-witness would assist in the operations of a court. And perhaps I should further suggest that as I am not long at my post after a "Leave of Absence", if the Government should now adopt the seemingly necessary means to keep me here. I should not probably care for another in less[?] than a year and a half. I have moreover the best adapted premises in the Colony for a Consulate, and everything which   has thus far been furnished me is in good condition.

Should the Government decide in accordance with my views, or otherwise, [?] us at present situated it is of the utmost importance that I should know the purport of the decision promptly. I am at present not meeting my expenses, and if it is not asking too much I would respectfully suggest telegraphing as a means of giving the desired information, the only address necessary being, Strickland, Goree.

I am Sir
Very respectfully Yours
Peter Strickland
U.S. Consul
