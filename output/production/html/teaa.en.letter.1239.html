Accra, March 3rd 1896.

U. S. Consular Agent
Elmina

Sir:

I wish to call your attention to an incident which happened to me in the Am. Bkt "D. A. Brayton" of Boston.

We Left Winnebah on Monday, March 2nd bound for Accra. Which sailing down the Coast and about five miles from Accra, we could see a vessel in the distance off shore, perhaps 8 or 10 miles away, and which I took for a telegraph ship. About half hour after, the mate came to me and said that the steamer was coming towards us and looked like a Man of War. I told him to hoist the ensign, which he did, and lowered the mizzen top said showing our colors plainly.

I saw he had signals up. The Steamer was H.M.S. "Blonde". She crossed our stern with signals still flying, and came across around on our lee quarter. Thinking him a stranger, seeking information, I hailed him by saying I was sorry, but we had no signals. He replied by asking why we did not hoist our colors, I replied, we   had, and he said. Why not before? I told him we did not hoist our colors to every ship which might pass 10 miles or so out to sea, but only to vessels which were near, we he said we ought to, and I replied that I did not agree with him, whereupon he seemed very angry, and said if he heard any more insolence he would make us heave to. I would not reply to his insolence, and he steamed away. What I wish to know is, Can any British Man of War come along side a merchantman belonging to a friendly Power in time of peace, and use threats of such a nature, for every one knows that a ship is forced to heave to by a shot being fired across her bows, and if she does not comply, the next one is supposed to go into her. Not only was the language used by the Captain totally uncalled for, but his tone too was most aggressive. I wish you to forward this to the Consul General and ask him to forward to the proper authorities.

I have written Mr. Brooks, enclosing a copy of this, and asked him to   forward to Washington.

I remain
Yours Respectfully
(sd) O. A. Datow
Master of Bakt D. A. Brayton
