Department of State Received Nov 7 1887
No. 71.

Consulate of the United States,
Goree-Dakar, Oct. 14, 1887

Mr George Stickland
To the Department of State

Subject:

Further Remarks on the Annoyances to which the Honorable Mr. Taylor was subjected while passing Dakar on October 8, 1887 on board S. S. "Fez"

Abstract of Contents

French Government meant no harm to United States Minister but to S.S. "Fez" "Messageries Maritimes Company's agent ungracious

One Enclosure.

Letter to Mr. Restoul

 
No. 71

Consulate of the United States,
Goree-Dakar, October 14, 1887.

Honorable James D. Porter,
Assistant Secretary of State,
Washington, D.C.

Sir,

My last despatch, No. 70, was hurridly written in order that it might catch the steamer which had already arrived, and I beg to add, now that I have had time to learn more, that the reasons given for detaining the S.S. "Fez" thirty hours and with her Minister Taylor are than on a former voyage the steamer had left the port of Dakar under compromising circumstances, and also that on this visit the captain and passengers used violent language to the Harbor-Master when the ships papers were refused them. The "compromising circumstances were that in getting under way in the night her anchor piked up the chain of a "Messageries Maritimes" coal barge and did some damage, and that she did not stop to rectify the matter. There   There are insinuations also of some small bills left behind.

The captain claimed not to be to blame for the broken chain for the lighter was in the public way without any light. If there were bills it is not unusual for it is understood that they are to be regulated afterwards by the consignee. In any case (for there are so many conflicting stories that it would be impossible to arrive at the truth), had the captain had pangs of conscience it is not brobable that he would have called here again. As for the "violent language" it might be excused considering the broiling sun to which they were exposed while waiting at the mole coupled with the persecutions which they suffered.

But be all this as it may, it only concerns the United States insofar as it bears upon the delay occasioned to its foreign minister and the wrongs he endured while passing Dakar on board the English steamer. The French Government can show that it had no intention of hindering a representative of the United States (which idea could no for a moment be entertained   entertained) for its Medical Director here has given me permission for him to go directly on board the Messageries Maritimes Mail Steamer for Berdeaux which was expected and did arrive before the departure of the "Fez", but was actuated only by a desire to have satisfaction for wrongs from the British Steamer by stopping her. But the Messageries Maritimes Company acted ungraciously through its agent here (The same who is Her Majesty's Vice-Consul) who refused absolutely to allow Mr. Taylor to go on board the Mail Steamer notwithstanding that the "Fez" had a clean bill of health. I thereupon addressed him a very polite letter the copy of which is enclosed and which he has not answered. He finds it difficult without doubt to put his motives in black and white; the more so that his company receives a tremendous subsidy for calling at Dakar and is probably responsible to a considerable extent to France. I did not mention this circumstance in my last, because I wished to transmit at the same time the justification he might give of his refusal. As he has given none I shall of course drop   drop the matter as it stands. Even were it my privilege to insist, I should not do so for I believe it to be highly essential not to allow one's self to be drawn into disputes and dissensions for there is rarely anything to be gained but on the contrary one's intentions are sure to be misconstrued and a stigma attaches to a Consulate which consequently loses prestige as well as dignity. This is more especially true in countries like Senegal where it is indispensable to preserve good relations with all. Therefore my suggestions and requests in this case were made with all the modesty and unobtrusiveness compatible with the occasion.

I beg to be excused for writing the details at such length but I remarked that Mr. Taylor was impatient of his detention and believing that he will speak of is upon his arrival, I have thought it preferable to err by sending too much rather than too little.

I am, Sir, with respect,
Your obedient servant
George Strickland
Vice-Consul.

 
Consulate of the United States of America at Goree-Dakar

Gorée, le 10 Octobre, 1887

Monsieur Restoul
Agent de la Compagnie des Messageries Maritimes á Dakar.

Monsieur:

Si je ne craignais pas d'être indiscret, je vous prierais de vouloir bien m'écrire pour quoi vous avez refusé de faire recevoir Monsieur Taylor, Ministre des Etais-Unis á Liberia, et passager sur le vapeur anglais "Fez", abord votre paquebot poste qui est arrivé le 8 inst. et par lequel il aurait pu arriver beaucoup plus lot eu Europe. C'est pour que je puisse repondre dans le cas que mon Gouvernement m'eu quande[?] de renseignements. N'est ce pas qu'il y a eu des precedents parmi les passagers qui sont arrivés au Sud sur les vapeurs "British and African" et qui se sont transbordés directement á un paquebot de votre compagnie sans[?] communiquer avec la terre?

J'ai   J'ai l'honneur, Monsieur, de vous saluer respectueusement, et de vous remercier d'avance

George Strickland
Vice Consul des Etats-Unis.

Without being indiscreet, I should be obliged if you would write me why you refused to allow Mr. Taylor, U.S. Minister to Liberia, and passenger on the English Steamer "Fez", to take passage on board your Mail Steamer which arrived the 8 inst and by which he might have arrived much sooner in Europe. I ask in case my Governments demands information. Have there not been precedents of passengers arrived from the south being transferred immediately to your line without communicating with the shore?

I have the honor to be
Yours respectfully,

Please note that the French is smoother and much more polite.
