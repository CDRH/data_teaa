Department of State Received Sep 10 1887

Franklinton
Franklin Co N.C.
Sept. 8/87.

Hon Thos F Bayard
Secretary of State
Washington D.C.

Sir:

I received a letter from the Treasury Department informing me that I was due the Government of the U.S. for service as Vice Consul General of the U.S. at Monrovia Liberia $337.27.   Your Government charged me for the burial expenses of Moses A. Hopkins that I am willing[?] to bear. Your Government also charge me for paying a bill of Mrs. E. J. Harmon of Cape Palmas after telling me to do so. Please look over your consular series and you will see where the Department told me to do what Minister Smyth and Hopkins did not do. The bill due Mrs Harmon was $48. and some cents and I paid in[?] the U.S. draft. Your Government also charged me $30.00 which I paid the U.S. Messenger who could not   give me a voucher; because he could not write his name. but you remember Moses Hopkins wrote you a Despatch about the messenger–stating that the boy could not write his name? The messenger could not write–that is the reason I could not send in a voucher for the $30.00 charged against me. I am now in the U.S. and I have no hopes of meeting the messenger again to get even a cross mark from him. Again I wish the U.S. government to remember I was in a position of   which I was unacquainted with the[?] rules and regulations they allowed. The[?] messenger has been paid and I pray[?] you in behalf of what I have lost in[?] carrying on the U.S. business at Monrovia Liberia that I be rewarded. Your Government also charged against me $30.50 drawen in favor of Moore & Sons. The State Department has voucher for that. Allow me to call the State Department to certain facts.

There[?] were many small items to be paid at the U.S. Legation at Monrovia for which I could not draw a   draft for G. Moore & Sons paid these small items and at the end of the quarter I would always pay them in draft; that is the reason they would would also give me voucher including messenger &c.

May the 7th 1887 the Department of State charged against me $20.00 which I paid out for wine. Now Sir–Indeed I could not prevent from drawing that $20.00 on the Department. The election of the Government of Liberia was over and the people of that place were going around visiting   the Consular officials of the various governments and the officials were giving them a treat and I did the same; that is the reason the $20.00 is against me.

It is I think useless to write further. Out-side of the $95.85 for burial expenses for[?] Moses A. Hopkins I think the Government of the U.S. should pay me for what I have done at my best[?] in its behalf. I was a servant of the Presbyterian church in that far off country and I did not   seek the position; but si being acquainted with Moses A Hopkins and knowing he was in a place unknown to him I thought I would be of use to him therefore I took the position and even held it long before ever receiving a certificate of recognition as Vice Consul Genl from your peer; therefore how now can your Government be so exact on me after laboring under many disadvantages in its behalf? I pray your government give me what due me. I know you will consider my case as I have   state here[?]. Please do all you can for me. I am not going back to Liberia; so I will not be able to give you any vouchers.

Hoping you will do justice to my case–and hoping to hear from you soon

I remain
Dear Sir
Your Obedient Servant
Samuel S. Sevier
Franklinton
Franklin Co. N.C.
