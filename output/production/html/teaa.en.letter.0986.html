Washington Star
June 28, 1879.

The American Polar Expedition.

Geographical Exploration–The Unknown Blank Around the Pole–Solving the Arctic Problem.

The departure of the American expedition from San Francisco for the Arctic regions, which takes place in a few days, gives a local interest to the subject of geographical exploration. The are of the earth's surface remaining unexplored is not great. Summed up, it embraces a portion of Central Africa, Central and Northern Australia, New Guinea and the vicinity of the Poles. The explorations of Livingstone and Stanley have harrowed down the are of the unexplored region in Central Africa to a very small compass, and what was less than half a century ago supposed to be in a great measure an arid and unproductive desert we now know to be well watered, fertile and densely populated. The absence of water and vegetation has baffled all attempts thus far made to explore the interior of Australia. That region is just about as little known now as it was at the time of the discovery of gold in Victoria and New South Wales. But little progress has been made during the last half century in Arctic exploration. Latitude 83 deg. 26 min. is the northernmost point reached thus far by any explorer. This leaves an unknown blank surrounding the North Pole, which at the narrowest point is about eight hundred miles across. The mission of the Jeannette expedition, like that of similar expeditions which have preceded it, is to ascertain, if possible, what this unknown region is. One of the most satisfactory of all recent Polar expeditions has been that sent out by the Swedish government in charge of Professor Nordensjold. The steamship Vega, in which the expedition sailed, was a few days ago reported to have finished the voyage along the northern shores of Europe and Asia; and she is now ploughing her way through the China seas homeward bound to complete the circumnavigation of the two contingents. But the highest latitude reached by the Vega was only 77 deg. 40 min.

The Jeannette will carry the first strictly Polar expedition, setting out from the Pacific and attempting to push forward to the North Pole by the way of Behring Strait. According to L'eutenant De Long no vessel has penetrated further north through Behring Strait than latitude 71 deg. Beyond that parallel the explorers will encounter an unknown region. A branch of the Kuro Shiwo, or Japanese Warm Stream, is known to enter the Arctic Sea through Behring Strait, and it is believed that it emerges on the other side of the continent through Baffin's Bay. The drift of ice is consequently northward on this side, while it sets to the south on the other side. Whether this will be an element of danger to the expedition or whether it will help it in the solution of the Arctic problem remains to be seen. It has been regarded by Arctic explorers hitherto as an element of danger, as it practically cuts off the retreat in case of any accident to the ship which may compel the party to take to the ice.

The California Academy of Sciences last evening held a meeting for the consideration of the Arctic problem, at which Lieutenant De Long, commander of the Jeannette, and other members of the expedition were present as invited guests. The academy expected these gentlemen would unfold their plans, but in this respect it was disappointed. Whatever these plans are circumstances may arise necessitating a material revision of them or even their total abandonment. The expedition starts, however, with the benefit of the experience of all other expeditions in the Polar regions to guide it, abundance of money to back it and the prestige of government direction. Even if the expedition fail to reach the North Pole–the goal of every Arctic explorer's ambition–it will no doubt add something to geographical lore and still further reduce the area of unknown territory for further geographical exploration.–[San Francisco Bulletin, June 17.

London Times,
July 15th, 1879
France.
Paris, Monday, July 14, 9-30 p.m.

A Commission has been appointed by M. de Freycinet to report on the feasibility of a railway between Algeria, the Soudan, and Senegal. The population of the Soudan is estimated, remarks M. de Freycinet, at 100,000,000. The Niger traverses half of it. The inhabitants are industrious. The moving sands, formerly considered universal, are only a local accident, and the soil is everywhere similar to that of European soils. A railway from Algeria to the Niger would not exceed 2,000 kilometres and would be much less costly than the projected Panama Canal. A preliminary commission has already recommended the scheme, one ground being that it would repress the internal slave trade; but it enjoins circumspection on account of the imperfect knowledge of certain parts of the Sahara. It therefore suggests a survey for a line of 300 kilometres between Biskra and Ouargla, to be connected with the Algeria and Constantine line, and that explorations should be made beyond Ouargla toward the Niger. The Budget Committee and a Senatorial Committee on Algerian railways have also pronounced in favour of France taking an active part in the opening up of Central Africa. The Commission now appointed is headed by General d'Andigné and Meudes, and includes M. de Lesseps, various senators and deputies, and a number of engineers.

M. Fouquet, deputy of the Aisne and a sugar refiner at Laon, has gone to London on a mission from the Minister of Finance to give evidence before the Parliamentary Committee on the Sugar Question.
