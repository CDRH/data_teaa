Office of the Penna. Colonization Soc.
Phila. March 19, 1862

To Abraham Lincoln
President of the United States

Dear Sir

We read that Alexander cut the Gordian Knot with his sword. Now I have a suggestion to make by which I fondly hope that the knot in regard to the acknowledgment of the independence of Liberia may be peacefully and quickly untied by a few strokes of your pen.

Hear me then, a few moments, in the midst of your pressing duties.

In London there resides Gerard Ralston, a Philadelphia by birth, but for thirty years a resident of Great Britain. He has negotiated, without charge, the treaties with most of the European governments. Before me is a letter, dated Feb. 26, 1862 in which he writes:

"I am to have a conference with Mr Adams about Liberian affairs and hope his influence with the American Government will aid in procuring a speedy recognition. I should like to   make a treaty of Amity, Commerce and Navigation with Mr Adams for the joint benefit of Liberia and the United States. I can probably manage this better than can be done at Washington as I have already made so many treaties and know the views and desires of the Liberian Government so well. My Colonization friends in America might probably promote this object at Washington. Mr Adams and I, could save Mr Seward all trouble in this matter. He has trouble enough already at Washington and he does not wish an increase of work in his department of State."

There seems to be great wisdom in this kind suggestion. Can it not be carried out. It avoids the chimera of colored ambassadors. Instead of seeming to go out of the way to acknowledge a colored Republic a treaty is sent, in this way, to the Senate, for their ratification.

Will you consult Mr Seward about this plan? Cannot the weights be lifted, thus, from the shoulders of the young and thriving African Republic? One of the best friends of America is the brave Flag Officer Foote.

 

You are daily remembered in earnest prayer. God has guided, and will guide you, I trust, in all the perils which we are encountering.

With much respect
Yours truly
Thomas S. Malcom
Co. Sec. of the Penna. Colonization Society
