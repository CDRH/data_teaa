C.
No. 3.

Legation Consulate General of the United States,
Monrovia, No. 20, 1878.

Mr. J. H. Smyth to the Secretary of State.

Subject:

The barque "Cupid."

Synopsis:

Protection & support furnished the destitute seamen of the barque, under impression that the ownership of the vessel determined its nationality.

 

C.
No. 3.

Legation of the United States,
Monrovia, Nov. 20, 1878.

Sir:

I have the honor to acknowledge the receipt of your No 9, dated Oct. 8, 1878 with reference to the barque "Cupid."

Ere you shall have received this, you will be in possession of the Judicial proceedings had in the Admiralty Court with respect to the barque in question.

You inform me that the vessel "cannot be looked upon Hon. F. W. Seward,
Ass't Sec'y of State,
Washington, D.C.   upon as a United States vessel."

Now if after seeing the decree of the Court, the Department still hods the opinion above quoted, I shall find myself in a dilemma; for the reason that after the burning of the vessel, with the exception of master, mate & steward, all the sailors were in a destitute condition, and as American seamen claimed and received protection & support from the Legation.

The men signed the articles under the impression that the vessel was an American vessel; it being the property   property of American citizens.

I was governed in my action, in giving support to the destitute seamen of the "Cupid," by the latter portion of Art. 13, Sec. 128, Consular Regulation beginning with the words: "and that every person &c., &c.

For the information of the Legation I would be advised whether the flag or property in the vessel determines its nationality.

With distinguished consideration, I am, Sir,
Your obd't serv't,
Jno. H. Smyth
