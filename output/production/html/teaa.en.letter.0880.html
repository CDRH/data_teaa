Boston 20 Jany 1862

To His Excellency
Abraham Lincoln,
President of the United States.

Sir

Please read the annexed copies of letters addressed to the late Prest. of the United States, no reply to which has ever been received.

And if your many pressing duties will allow you to consider the subject at this time, I beg you to examine the Statutes therein referred to, as well as the precedents, a list of some of which, is appended.

I have requested the United States Commercial Agent at Monrovia to communicate the facts in the case, in official despatches, and doubt not that you will find at the State Department such statements as will convince you of the propriety of affording immediate   relief to those of us who for so long have been endeavoring to build up a legitimate trade with the Coast of Africa, as we have reason to fear that discriminating duties will soon be levied upon our vessels, unless justice is done to Liberian interests in the United States.

I am, Sir,
Yours very resp'y
Charles P. Clark

Boston, 26th July, 1860

To
James Buchanan,
President of the United States.

Sir,

The writer begs to call your attention to his letter of 11 June, of which copy is enclosed.

The matter is one of interest to me and an early answer is solicited.

Very respectfully,
Yours
(signed) Chas. P. Clark

  [illegible]

being susceptible of proof, the writer begs leave to refer you to (U.S. Statutes V4, P308) an act approved 24 May 1828, as also to (Statutes Vol. 4, P 579) an act approved the 13 July, 1832, authorising you to admit future arrivals on terms of equality, as also to refund the discriminating duties already collected, and to inquire what proof you require and how authenticated to authorise you to issue the proclamation therein referred to and provided for.

The certainty of discriminating duties being imposed on American commerce in the ports of Liberia, by which I should be a loser, in case no relief is granted to Liberian vessels, impels me to make this enquiry, and to beg you to give the subject your immediate attention.

I have not the slightest interest in the "E. N. Roye."

 

With the greatest respect,
I remain
Yours &c.
(signed) Chas. P. Clark
