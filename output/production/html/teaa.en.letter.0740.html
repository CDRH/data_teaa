Department of State Received Mar 2 1885

United States Consulate
Loanda, 30 December 1884.

Sir

I have the honor to enclose translation of a despatch received from His Excellency the Governor General in reply to a despatch I wrote him, with reference to the American Mission lately expelled from Bailunda & Bihé. Since that date Mr Walters, Chief of the Mission has been here and I presented him personally to His Excellency the Governor, & the Governor has promised to do all in his power to assist the Mission and has given Mr Walters a document with the private seal of the Government attached, to the Chiefs of Bailunda & Bihé requesting them to do all in their power to assist the Mission and informing the said Chiefs that any persons presenting themselves for the future as representing the Government, must present a similar seal before they are to be recognised as such. Mr Walters has returned to Benguella and seems to think he will not have much trouble for the future. Mr Saunders another member of the Mission has returned to Bailunda and they appear to have great hopes of recovering the greater part of their property.

The Governor has also offered to give the Mission a grant of land to the extent of 1000 hectares or   or 2400 acres, should they wish to establish an Industrial School at Benguella or elsewhere within the province.

The U.S. Vessel of War "Kersage", I hear was in the Congo a few days ago, but did not come as far South as Loanda

I have the honor to be
Your most obedient Servant
Robt S. Newton
U.S. Vice Consul

To the
Assistant Secretary of State
Washington DC.

Department of State Received Mar 2 1885

Loanda
November 18 1884

Sir

I received your despatch of the 17th ult in which you not only enclosed the guarantees that the American Mission wishes to re-establish itself in Bailundo, but even an account of the effects and their value lost by the mission, which are supposed to be in possession of the Sobba who is held to be responsible for them. No one saw with greater regret than myself, that the Sobba of Bailudo could commit the same violence to the American Missionaries as he has at times committed on the Portuguese merchants and which certainly, neither you Sir, nor the Mission can think that such violences could have been ordered authorised or even insinuated either by me or any other Portuguese with the least investiture of authority. Of this   this the missionaries ought to be convinced as the governor of Benguella has more than once repeated it, and this I now confirm

In the interior the Sobbas far from the sight of civilised authority, commit thefts and extortions, that constitute one of the greatest risks of one employed in the civilisation of the African races who as savages are eminently suspicions and no one can certainly make the European Authorities responsible for this special character, at most times excited in their malpractices by political intention and some times ostensible, at others secretly manifested for the travellers and missionaries who parting from the scientific sphere or from preaching the Gospel give to their position in the interior an irritant situation of which anyone can imagine with more or less accuracy when he judges himself prejudiced by these intentions, in his interests, and especially that of   of commercial gain

By what is affirmed by the missionaries, it appears that they were given notice to leave Bailundo in eight days and even then they stayed six weeks, and it seems only rational that during that time they should have sent one of their members to Benguella to obtain a letter from the Governor to the Sobba to assure him that in whatever intrigues my name or that Governor's might be involved, had no foundation whatever and was utterly false.

This the missionaries did not do and consequently they have only themselves to blame. What part the Portuguese merchant Braga might have had in the matter that very much I lament to have been the determination of the Sobba of Bailundo, I cannot tell you, as I await information from the Governor of Benguella who left for the interior with the intention of complaining of the affair and to   to relate personally what had taken place to enable me to do entire justice. At present we have the simple declaration of the missionaries, there is yet to be heard the Sobba, and it is this that I enjoined the Governor to do.

The habitual complaint of the merchants of Benguella is that the American Mission tried by every possible means to deviate the flow of commerce from Bengulla, and this might perchance have influenced the spirit of one less cautious to have provoked the Sobba and his ministers to do what they did, thus giving them a pretext, which until to day they have not wanted, to behave in a like manner to some of the Portuguese merchants, under futile reasons of "fetish" that have only had their origin from the coveteousness and greediness of the heathen population, always incited by the knowledge that they may have   have of the existence of goods in the hands of the inland trader.

The experienced opinion of Silva Portó is that what has now occurred to the American Mission he had long thought one day would happen as he well knows the character of the Bailundos, not being able to state if the affair really occurred as the missionaries state or even if any Portuguese merchants directly or indirectly took part. The comportment of this illustrous Portuguese settler to the English Missionary whose letter you enclose in one of your despatches to which I reply wakes his statement altogether beyond questioning as he offered him his house in Bihé to stop as long as he pleased so long as he only preached the gospel and did not enter into politics, for from the moment he did he would have to leave the house in order not to compromise Silva Porto with the natives

Not less is the complaint of the Sobba against the American Missionaries concerning their   political interference than is the complaint of the Merchants of Benguella concerning the supposed meddling in trade to which I already referred and therefore it will be much better that at the missionaries on their return to Bailundo make a solemn declaration or oath according to their religion before witnessess and the authorities that they do not intend to influence in any prejudicial way the trade of Benguella, but that they simply go to preach the Gospel and the doctrine of Christ. The oath that I suggest would for my part be perfectly unnecessary but the public spirit in the country is irritated against the missionaries, and in as much as such a promise would be a means of pacifying and dispersing any doubts that may remain and which certainly can cost nothing to the conciliating and Evangelical spirit of the missionaries

As you Sir, can well imagine I cannot give   give a decisive opinion upon the subject without first having the report of the Governor of Benguella's voyage into the interior, nor can I guarantee the behavior of the Sobba and have not sufficient forces in Bailundo, and much less can I exact from him the payment that the mission claims.

The mission established itself in the interior without consulting the Portuguese government as to what port of guarantee it might have for its members and property, ran of its own accord the risk inherent to the kind of exploration in which it was engaged. If the government had been consulted it would most certainly have said that the natives of Bailundo are treacherous and that only by a miracle could the missionaries uphold themselves, more so as soon as the Mission had a means and especially that of goods, incentive to the habitual malpractices of the native.

The American Mission can then when it   likes, establish itself where best it thinks fit; what the Portuguese government cannot do is to guarantee that the Sobba shall not repeat his violences because that, no country could do to which he was a vassal, nor could it keep a competent military force the expense of which no mission could compensate

What the Portuguese government can and will do as soon as it has properly heard the question is to place regular authorities in Bailundo and Bihé to maintain the equilibrium of intercourse between the Sobba and Merchants or Missionaries, and so forewarn itself by trustworthy notices & reports against the affirmations of other contradictory parties, and in that case while the Portuguese Government act like this they will have to limit the guarantee that they offers to those establishing themselves in Bihé and Bailundo, which can reasonably be claimed taking   taking into consideration the state of the people of these two districts at the same time noting that the guarantee will be entire in the districts like Cacondo & Quillengues where the preaching of the gospel would be as useful as in Bailundo or Bihé, and that will have to be combined between the interested parties and the native authorities of the place before a Portuguese authority that will be placed there, when missionaries or merchants wish to establish themselves in neighbourhoods where only reigns native customs, simply restricted by vassalage sworn to the King of Portugal

As to the English Missionary the writer of the letter to which I have already referred, it appears to me that the prestige of the settler Silva Porto to whom he is much indebted and from whom he has derived great help to him it ought to be sufficient guarantee to affirm, that as much to him and his subordinates, or even others   independent of him, that they will not have to complain of any interference in their work of civilisation, so long as they keep themselves to that, and do not interfere in politics whether native or European, in which case the Portuguese government cannot be responsible in anyway whatever for the behaviour of the savage tribes, amongst whom they may establish their theatre of operation contrary to its ostensible ends.

God guard you
&c &c &c
Government Palace
November 18 1884

To the United states Vice Consul

(Signed) Francisco Joaquim Ferra do Amaral
