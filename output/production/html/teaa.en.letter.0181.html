Department of State
Monrovia, October 3 1864

Sir,

In consequence of certain dangers with which this Republic is suddenly threatened by the recent stranding of our only naval vessel and guarda costa, the "Quail" and the probability of our coast being, for a long time, in an exposed and defenceless conditions, I Hon. W. H. Seward
Secretary of State
&c &c Washington D.C.   I am directed by the President to make application through you to the Government of the United States for a small revenue cutter or gun-boat for which this Republic will stipulate to pay after a term of years to be agreed upon by the Chargè d'Affaires of Liberia in the United States, and whomsoever the United States Government may appoint as its agent to negotiate on its behalf

Among   Among the dangers to be apprehended are, 1st the invasion of our feeble settlements on the coast at a distance from the capital by predatory tribes who, taking advantage of the emergency the absence of a suitable vessel to throw forces readily among them would seek the slightest pretext for plundering the settlements.

Owing ton the influence destructive of the slave trade, which this Republic has exercised, not   not a few of the older aborigines who formerly enriched themselves by the nefarious traffic entertain bitter and resentful feelings against Liberia, which, whenever there is the least opportunity, they never fail to manifest. The younger natives, I am happy to perceive the superior advantages of lawful commerce over the slave trade, and we may hope that ere long not only the overt practice but every feeling favorable to   to it will be completely annihilated within our borders.

2 From what I have just said, it may be readily inferred that another danger to the Republic likely to result from the absence of a guarda costa would be the revival of the slave trade in isolated localities. Those sections of country would again be the scene of the horrible barbarities and cruelties of former years, and a deplorable check would   would be imposed upon the growing agriculture and commerce of the country.

3 The third and last danger, which I shall mention, to be apprehended by us from the naval deficiency in question would be the constant infraction of our revenue laws by certain European traders; especially as there will likely go into operation on the 1st of January 1865, our new law confirming all foreign vessels to the legally constituted "ports of entry." Many   Many of the European traders seem opposed to this law, not because it is not just and equitable, but because they think that their traffic hereafter will not be so lucrative as it has been; and, through their influence, by clandestine visits to native tribes on the coast, they would be able to excite and aid them to acts of hostility against the Government.

The Government of Liberia has never before felt at liberty to make application, in emergencies, to   to the Government of the United States. But such has been the kind and courteous treatment of the present Government to Liberia, that we feel emboldened to take this step, assured that we have your ardent sympathy in our efforts to build up and maintain an independent Afric-American nationality on this continent.

The President and people of Liberia have observed with emotions of sincere pleasure the enlightened patriotism and   and exalted philanthropy which, since the breaking out of the unhappy contest, have influenced the councils of the American nation. Among the decisions of that Government which Liberia and every negro community must hail with the most unfounded applause, are first, the decision that slavery shall not hence forth be extended under the American flag over territories now free; secondly, that the African slave trade shall never be revived or renewed; thirdly, that slavery shall be forever abolished within the federal District of Columbia

fourthly   fourthly, that slaves escaping or captured from disloyal masters shall not be restored to slavery but shall be free.

These and numerous other equally liberal acts fortify us in the belief that the United States Government will not fail to accord us any assistance in its power in view of the imminent emergencies to which I have referred.

In closing this communication, I beg to express the hope that the lamentable civil war now distracting your lately prosperous and happy country, may be brought   brought to a speedy termination, consistent with the honor and dignity of the United States, conducive to the welfare and happiness of all the inhabitants of that great country, and promotive of the cause of civil liberty throughout the world.

I have the honor to be, with the highest consideration, Sir, your most obedient humble Servant,
Edwd W. Blyden
