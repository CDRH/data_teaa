Consular Bureau Received Aug 24 1865
No 3

U.S. Comcl Agency
Gaboon, June 23d 1865.

Wm H. Seward,
Secretary of State,
Washington, D.C.

Dear Sir:

The vacancy in this Comcl Agency, caused by the resignation of H. May Esqr., which I mentioned in despatch No. 2, gives me an opportunity, without infringing the rights of any one, of saying a few words on the U.S. commerce with this port, and the small need there is for a consular officer.

The trade has been gradually decreasing for a number of years. For the last three years, on an average three vessels (about 600 tons in all) a year have come this way from the Bight of Benin, to land a few passengers & a little freight, and to buy a few thousand billets of Barwood (an article, I believe, not dutiable, and of which a few cargoes would glut the market) to complete their cargoes of Coffee, Camwood, Palm-oil &c. bought along the coast from Monrovia to Fernando Po.

There is no prospect of the Trade reviving. The Ivory trade is pushed to its utmost extent. It is in the hands of the English, who can produce the Articles bartered for it cheaper, & more inferior in quality than the Americans can.

The India-rubber id being rapidly exhausted. Barwood is still plenty; but the French authorities here prevent all vessels from going up the river to trade, & thus getting near the Barwood, and even small boats must procure & pay for permission to ascend the river, and carry the French flag. This causes delay and increases the price.

American vessels of late, for the sake of despatch, buy of the English factories here.

Thus Trade does not require the presence of a Consul here.

The officer at Gaboon can not know about cargoes, taken aboard at ports North of this, so as to prevent frauds on the revenue. Besides the greater number of vessels return to the States from ports North of this, at which no consular officers reside; and thus the officer here has not the opportunity of certifying, even to the best of his knowledge, to the cargoes shipped.

The number of American Citizen residents here has also decreased. Their relations with the authorities have been for the last fifteen years of the most friendly character, and are likely so to remain. On their part there has never been a desire for a consular officer at Gaboon.

There are a few Americans at Corisco, but they are under Spanish rule. They may be interfered with; but they could not be much assisted by a consul in a French port, if they are.

Should the United State again send a squadron to the West Coast of Africa to cooperate with England in supporting the Slave trade, a consul at Gaboon would be of very little use. For several degrees north & south of this port there is no foreign slave trade, except what is carried on in small schooners & boats from the Portuguese islands of Prince and St. Thomas. The squadron could have no business in a French port, whence the trade is prohibited.

Fernando Po, as being already a depot for coals, and the Southern terminus of the line of Mail Steamer, and also near the ports in the Bight of Benin, whence the trade is carried on, would become a rendezvous for our vessels.

In fine I do not see what American interest can be advanced by the U.S. Comcl Agency at Gaboon; nor what interest would be in any way prejudiced by its discontinuance.

I have done only what I consider my duty to my Country in communicating the above. If, notwithstanding these representations, you wish to continue it, I am ready to welcome and assist your appointed as far as I can; or I should feel myself honored in subserving the interests of the United States here, and would, if appointed Commercial Agent, discharge the duties of the office to the best of my ability. I am free to confess that I desire the appointment, but I have no claims to urge why it should be conferred upon me rather than another.

I have the honor to be, sir, your obedient servant,
J. M. Preston.

U.S. Comcl Agency.
Gaboon June 23d 1865.
J. M. Preston.

Advising the Agency to be discontinued, with reasons. 1st Very little trade 2nd Trade can not be increased 3d Obstacle to trade. 4th Consul can not know about trade from other ports 5th Few residents, they do not desire it. 6th Could not assist in suppressing the Slave trade, should U.S. vessel be sent But if continued, present occupant desires to be appointed
