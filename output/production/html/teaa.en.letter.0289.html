Consulate of the United States of America at Sierra Leone
23rd May 1864

Sir,

I have the pleasure to forward the Department of State, per British brig "Emma" a package of the "Bean of Calabar", about 10 lbs, which I obtained through the kindness of the Rev. A. Robb at Calabar, whose letter I enclose, should more be required I can obtain them from the same source You Hon. F. W. Seward
Secretary of State
Washington   you will notice the article cost 6/ per lb., sterling, which is, here, $1.44, there has been about 50 cents expense on them.

I have the honor to be
Sir
Your ob'd't Servant
Henry Rider
U.S. Consular Agent.

 

Creek Town
Old Calabar,
2d May 1864

Sir:

I duly received your letter of 12th April, desiring, on behalf of the government of the United States, a supply of Calabar bean.

I have much pleasure in being able to comply with your request, and shall send per Mail a small box containing 10 lbs. If Analysis is the object, there will probably be too few for any extensive experiments. But Mr Baillie says that "500 or so would perhaps be enough". There are more than that in 10 lbs. I should think there are at lest 1000. Should more be needed I shall be happy to supply them; such if may be well that you   give me notice without any long delay, lest my supply should run out.

By the present mail I learn that 10/- has been paid per pound in London but as these are for scientific purposes I shall give them for 6/- per pound.

It is not out of place to state that about four years ago, I supplied some of these beans to a Dr[?] Mitchell in Philadelphia who, I understood was engaged in an analysis of African ordeals.

The request came to me through one of the missionaries at Corsica[?] or Gaboon.

I may further state that the fullest[?] I believe the only, account of the bean is to be found in a "Graduation Thesis" published by Messrs Oliver and Boyd, Edinburgh. The title is "The Characters, Actions and Therapeutic Uses of the Ordeal Bean   Calabar (Physostigma Venenosum, Balfour): A Graduation Thesis, for which a Gold Medal was awarded by the Edinburgh University Aug 62[?] By Thomas R. Fraser M.D. Edin, &c . It is published in the form of a pamphlet and gives a full account of the experiments and their results, and also specimen cases of diseases in which the bean has been used as a remedy, with good results.

I have the honour to be
Sir
Your obedient servant
Alexander Robb

Henry Esq
U.S. Vice Consul
Sierra Leone  

P.S. I may warn you that the bean is a deadly poison, and should be kept out of the way[?] lest any accident happen. There is no danger unless it be eaten, of course; but I believe that less[?] than[?] one may be a fatal dose. Its native name is é-ser-e (e, as in met)

A.R.
