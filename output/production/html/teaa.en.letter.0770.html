No 103 ... U.S. Consulate Goree-Dakar
April 30th 1889

To the Honorable
Assistant Secretary of State
Washington

Sir

I have the honor to acknowledge receipt of your Dispatch No 46 which came to hand yesterday, and having a full knowledge of Saint Louis affairs I have to report as follows.

Saint Louis is a flourishing City of some 30,000 inhabitants, built mainly in the European Style, and being situated near the mouth of a large and navigable River has considerable commerce with the interior. Unfortunately this River is obstructed at its mouth by a bar which renders it usually not navigable for vessels of large size, so that until quite recently the place has been rather avoided by vessels coming direct from the United States. Such vessels have mainly discharged their cargoes at Goree from which place small schooners, principally American, have been employed to forward them as wanted to selling-agents at Saint-Louis. This American Coastwise Commerce has averaged in the last decade to 60 or 75000 dollars a year and amounts perhaps to nearly that now. The Messrs F H Hamlen & Son have attempted to do business with Saint Louis direct, and so far have been favored by an unusually good condition of the bar. A vessel chartered by them called the Edith called here in March 1888 to have surveys held, having damaged some of her cargo on the passage out, and I presume that the inconvenience of not having a proper functionary there to attend to American interests must be to them very great. The reason I have not urged the matter with the Government is that in Dispatch No 4 to this Consulate it was   expressly stated that "no agencies were then needed," and I have received no encouragement to push the matter since. The American trade at Saint Louis is certainly capable of being increased, and if the Government is now disposed to afford the facility of a Consular Agent there I will make an early occasion to see Mr Camille Larrouy, with whom I am not yet personally acquainted, and report in the course of a month on his qualifications. A brother of his has been President of the Tribunal here at Goree, and as he seems to be the trusted agent of the American firm who have made application for him, there can be no question as to his character. The serious trouble here is that no one seems to realize that an American Consul is required to speak and write in English or that he has anything in particular to do. Mr Larrouy   is I believe a French West Indian, many of whom know English very well, and I have a pretty strong impression that he will be found available.

Saint Louis is less than one hundred miles from here in a direct line, but about 140 miles by train, and a trip there takes an entire day. There are two American vessels expected here every moment, and having no Vice Consul or Deputy at command I cannot leave here for St Louis until both of these vessels are cleared. Goree-Dakar as I have pointed out in several of my Dispatches is probably the most important commercial point on the West African Coast, but its Consulate cannot be kept on a proper basis for lack of funds. I would respectfully request that while the necessary agency in question is being established that measures be taken to place this Consulate on the same salary basis as that at Santiago C.V.I and that at Sierra Leone.

I am Sir
Very truly yours
Peter Strickland
U.S. Consul
