Department of State.
Monrovia February 19th 1883.

Sir

I had the honor to forward you a despatch last year, asking the consideration of the Government of the United States of America to several matters in connection with the welfare and interests of this Republic.

Having up to the present received no acknowledgement of that communication and in view of the fact that our complications in connection with the North West Boundary Question have increased rather than diminished, I beg to enclose a copy herewith fearing that the original may have miscarried.

I have the honor also to forward you a copy of a Resolution of the Senate of this Republic adopted at its late session touching the question of the North West Boundary which it had under consideration

The fact that the government of the United States unsolicited by that of Her Majesty the Queen Great Britain and Ireland and this State to act as umpire on this question, and in case of any disagreement Hon. F. C. Frelinghuysen
Secretary of State
For the United States of America &c &c &c   on the part of the Commissioners representing the two governments the decision of the United States would be final. I beg to call your attention to this understanding, and to request that your Government will be kind enough to give some expression on the subject, so as to set the matter at rest.

I need scarcely mention that this perplexing question has been and still is the source of deep concern to this Republic, and a termination of the dispute by the kind intervention of your Government would be hailed with great delight and profound gratitude by the citizens generally.

Department of State
Monrovia February 2, 1882

Sir

I am directed by the President after careful and anxious deliberation in the Cabinet, to request you to lay before your Government the following:

The Republic of Liberia whose origin and history are well known to the Government of the United States has now reached a period in her history when she sorely needs and is fully able to bear a large influx of civilized descendants of Africa from the United States. An important addition to her civilized population is imperatively demanded if she is to go on extending her influence and pushing her free institutions against the encroachment of foreigners.

The Natives in the interior of the Republic are anxious for the planting of civilized settlements on the beautiful hills and in the futile valleys which diversify their interesting country. We are pushing on as fact as we can get the immigrants from the United States, and our new settlements of civilized industry as they advance.

Arthington and Brewerville founded about ten years ago in dense forests by freed men from North Carolina, Mississippi, Alabama   Louisiana and Arkansas are now beautiful coffee groves with their computable frame buildings Christian Churches, School houses and Workshops, and as the Representatives of the Christian religion and of American life are attracting the Aborigines drawing them both within and around the settlements. But in view of the vast work to be done, it is comparatively little that can be prosecuted or accomplished by the few emigrants sent out at irregular intervals by a private association depending for its means upon voluntary contributions.

For the last ten or fifteen years the operations of the American Colonization Society have been confined to one section of the Republic for the most of means to send immigrants as formerly to different points along the Coast. The consequence is that the civilized population in the three leeward counties is rapidly diminishing and leaving the Aborigines to the money of the intrigues of designing foreigners who are anxious to see the prestige of Liberia not only lowered but destroyed among the natives on the Coast, hoping that eventually the Six hundred miles of much coveted coast with its accessible and desirable interior may fall into the hands of some European power. In relation to the commercial possibilities of Liberia, and the dangers to which she is exposed from external agencies, I beg to refer you to the despatch of the Hon. John H. Smyth, the late Minister Resident here, published in the Foreign Relations of the United States for 1880. pp 691-707. The   The native wars which occur periodically among the Aborigines and which are the result in part of old feuds leaving their origin in the days of the horrible slave trade, can never be permanently suppressed on the Coast and in the adjacent interior without the presence in these territories of civilized settlements. Even in the immediate presence a neighborhood of so powerful a Government as that of Sierra Leone it is impossible to prevent the recurrence of desolating wars in the purely native districts to the detriment of trade and against the progress of the people.

The Sherbro and Sierra Leone rivers within British jurisdiction and having no settlements of civilized people are frequently the scene of ruinous tribal wars, while the St Paul's, the Junk, the St. John's, and the Sinoe Rivers within Liberian jurisdiction and settled by Colonists are entirely free from such disturbances. It is in the North western and South eastern frontiers of the Republic and in localities within our boundaries distant from the civilized settlements where periodical wars now occur, and where trade is interrupted.

The long pending question relating to the Northwestern boundary of the Republic is a source of continual annoyance and irritation; and the arrangement of it appears to be beyond the scope of ordinary diplomacy. The country must be settled by civilized immigrants

During the negotiations of 1850 with aboriginal authorities   for the cession of the Gullinas and adjacent territory which Liberia now claims as it Northwestern limits the Commissions of the Liberian Government agreed that schools should be established and settlements made in that part of the country, but the need of immigrants in other portions of the Republic, and the supply from United States being very limited prevented the Government from carrying out that part of its promise to the Gullinas people and the scarcity of men for the educational necessities of the Country, prevented the sending of teachers to them. This caused dissatisfaction among them and was used by avaricious foreigners to stimulate their hostility to the Republic. But the Government is satisfied set that the establishment of settlement in the country would set at rest forever all disputes about boundary, and bring the Aborigines peacefully under the unquestioned jurisdiction of Liberia.

In view of an increased manifestation of a determination of some of the European powers to take advantage of the commercial possibilities of this Country: it is now a time of considerable anxiety with this government. The efforts of British traders in our immediate neighborhood in the North, French enterprises in the Northeast, the restless condition of the Aborigines in some sections of our Republic, all conspire to cause us no little alarm. Every thing is harassingly uncertain, and if we do not get an accession of intelligent and enterprising immigrants over   conditions may become very serious indeed.

It is in view of these facts that the President directs me to ask the assistance of your Government to send us a few of the thousands of civilized Christian negroes in your Country, who are anxious to cast in their lot with Liberia, but who lack the means of transportation and of a start on their arrival in the land of their fathers.

Large numbers of people in the Southern section of your Country of good industrious habits, who would be hardly missed from the Six millions of colored people, have been for years making application to the government of the United States for aid to emigrate to the land of their fathers.

These people who would not be missed among you would create a nation on this side of the water.

Liberia is the offspring of American benevolence and this little Republic in Western Africa has exerted an influence upon the adjacent tribes almost without a parallel in the history of Colonies. A few thousand immigrants from the United States have drawn under their influence from the stagnant and barren heathenism of ages over a million of people, and without force or money have assimilated them by industrial, social and religious influence and made them a part of a christian Republic based upon the traditions of the United States.

 

The Republic of Liberia in these features of freedom and equal rights of universal education, without distinction of classes, which so favorably impress the aboriginal mind, is an illustration of the kind and practice of American institutions. Half of the public domain of Liberia is held in trust for the settlement of the black people of the United States. Every black man in America has a claim to a homestead in Liberia and the Government is bound by law to grant ten acres of land to every adult, and twenty-five acres to every family who come to this country from the United States.

Our relations then to the American Government are peculiar, and our claim to sympathy and assistance from the United States is almost that of an American Colony.

In view of these facts therefore, and of the fact that the American Colonization Society is unable to cope with the present exigences of the work, and in view also of the part which the American Government from the beginning of the Colony has always taken in forwarding its interests, the President feels that he may venture to solicit aid from your Government to increase our civilized population, strengthen the foundations, and enlarge the boundaries of the State, and consequently spread peace and quietness throughout districts ever harried by restless marauders thus opening and keeping open a highway for the vast trade of Soudan which could be easily drawn in this   direction instead of giving in such large quantities across the desert to the Mediterranean; and the demand for American manufacture in the interior is practically unlimited.

The President also desires me to say that it would be of incalculable advantage if one of the United States could be detailed from the Mediterranean squadron once a year between the months of December and March to cruise on the Liberian Coast. This would greatly assist us in maintaining the prestige of the Republic among the Aborigines at distant points from the settlements along the Coast, and in infusing new energy into the movement of our revenue officers. For want of a Coast police the revenue from the Customs is very inadequately collected.

The President ventures to call attention to the fact that among the members of these who fostered Liberia in her early days, and who did not think it contrary to the laws of the United States that your Government should assist this infant nation were leading American statesmen such as President Madison and Monroe in honor of whom the Capital is named, Chief Justice Marshall, Bushrod Washington, General Harper, Henry Clay, Daniel Webster, Rufus King, Edward Everett and others. The President hope therefore that it will be considered within the competence and ability of the United States Government to come to the help of Liberia in the present emergency   emergency by small appropriation to aid deserving persons to come and occupy the localities threatened from abroad, and thus save us from the anxieties which perplex, and the dangers which menace us.

I am further interested to say that for these advantages which we ask of the United States, the Government of Liberia will be prepared under a special treaty to concede certain privileges to American citizens doing business in Liberia, not granted to other foreigners, privilege which if American capitalists would avail themselves of them, would give them the virtual control of u large portion of the trade of Coastal Africa, and American products and shipping would enjoy advantages which European traders have many years sought in vain at the hands of the Government of Liberia. The whole [?] country lies before the American traders and Agriculturists through Liberia the country to the penetration and commercial possession of which French inspirations and energies are now directed.

Should your Government take into favorable consideration the requests and propositions made in this communication and should desire it, the President will appoint one or two special Commissioners to visit Washington to treat with the Government of the United States in relation to these important matters.

Availing myself of this opportunity to assure you of   my very high consideration and esteem.

I have the honor to be Sir
Your Most Obedient Servant.
G. W. Gibson

Join Resolution of thanks to the government of the United States of America, and an expression of gratitude to Commodore. R. W. Shufeldt, U.S.N.

Whereas the government of the United States of America, at the request of the governments of Great Britain and Liberia readily consented to act as Umpire in the settlement of the Liberian North West Boundary Question and appointed Commodore R. W. Shufeldt, U.S.N. of the Flagship "Ticonderoga" to act on their part in the adjustment of the Liberian North West Boundary called in question by the English Government and in view of the remarkable patience with which the Commodore and his officers waited upon the movements of the Commissioners to their adjournment. Therefore Resolved by the Senate and House of Representatives of the Republic of Liberia in Legislature assembled:

1. That the Legislature of Liberia by this resolution tender the thanks of the People of Liberia to the Presidents of the United States and   and to the American Nation for the cheerful manner in which they accepted the joint solicitation of the governments of England and Liberia to use their good offices and Umpire, as well as for their choice of so able a naval officer as Commodore Shufeldt to perform this important Mission.

Resolved, That the government of Liberia express their entire satisfaction with the efforts of the Commodore to have the final and satisfactory adjustment of this vexed[?] question; and they would record their gratitude to Commodore R. W. Shufeldt, U.S.N., and his gallant officers.

Approved Jany. 1st: 1880.
