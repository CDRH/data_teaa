Subject.

Indigent American children in Liberia

Synopsis.

J. C. Todd and wife of Mineapolis, dies soon after reaching Liberia; leaving five helpless children, one of whom has since died, leaving four in very needy circumstances.

 

No. 4, Consular Series.
Legation of the United States,
Monrovia Liberia;
Sept. 19, 1898.

Hon. John B. Moore,
Assistant Secretary of State,
Washington.

Sir:

I have the honor to call your attention to the lamentable fact, that in the year 189- one J. C. Todd, wife and five children of Mineapolis, Minnesota, moved to this country. I am told, that in a very short time, possibly, not exceeding 30 days, after their arrival, both the father and the mother sickened and died, leaving five small children, in a strange country, unknown, friendless, penneyless and helpless. It was learned,   prior to the death of the father–J. C. Todd, that he was a Mason; whereupon, the fraternity, here, made some effort, to temporarily, provide for the relief of the suffering children. Having no Orphanage, alms house, nor charitable institution here, the children were brought before the Court, where a guardian was appointed to look after them. They were separated and placed in such families as would take them. One of them has since died; leaving four, the oldest of whom is a boy, I am told of 12 or 13 years. He, I am told, is with an old lady, in the Country, very badly and poorly provided for, being very scantily fed, and almost entirely   entirely without clothing; and for these and other cruelties, he has recently, twice run away from her, and has endeavored to make his way to a Colony of Americans, more recently in this Country; but in each effort to escape he has been overtaken and returned to this cruel and merciless house. We have a letter from William R. Morris Esq., Atty at Law, No. 927 Guaranty Loan Building Minneappolis, who says that if it was possible to get the children back to Minneappolis, that the friends of the deceased father and mother would take the children, rear and educate them.

While I know of no provission   provission of law, whereby I can render the children the necessary and proper relief, yet, for the sake of innocent, infant, suffering humanity, I appeal to the State Department, for permission or authority to send these little sufferers back to America.

By authority of the State Department, I can very easily arrange a safe transit for them, with Messrs. Elder, Dempster & Co., of Liverpool, whose ships touches here, every two weeks. They are also the Liverpool Agents of the "White Star Line," between Liverpool and New York.

Upon reaching New York, the children could be delivered to the Chief of Police, who doubtless, having   having been previously advised, by the Department, could and would take pleasure, in arranging their transportation and sending them off for Minneappolis.

Anxiously awaiting your pleasure and advice;

I have the honor to be your obedient servant;
Owen L. W. Smith
United States,
Consul General,
Monrovia Liberia.
